# AI adoption: what to do at each phase — on one page

This intervention card gives you the signs, the right action and the pitfalls for every phase. Print it out, keep it next to your laptop, or pull it up during a team meeting.

## Interventions per phase

| Phase | Recognition | Do this | Don't do this |
|-------|------------|---------|---------------|
| **Dismissal** | AI never comes up. Pilot invitations are ignored. "Not relevant for us." | Show, don't tell. Use AI visibly yourself. Invite low-stakes experimentation at information level. | Give presentations about the future of AI. Make usage mandatory. |
| **Resistance** | Actively hunts for errors. Only shares failed outputs. Sarcastic remarks about AI. | Acknowledge shortcomings openly. Reframe expectations: starting point, not finished product. Coach on prompt techniques. | Play it down ("you just need to learn how to use it"). Force your way through the resistance. |
| **The Hit (Ego Shock)** | Silence after a demo. Cynicism. Overworking to compensate. Avoids AI conversations. | Normalise the feeling. Redefine the role. Create space for one-to-one conversations. | Promise "your job is safe". Increase delegation level. Push for quick acceptance. |
| **The Deal (Bargaining)** | Sets explicit boundaries ("AI may do X but not Y"). Reacts emotionally when boundaries are crossed. | Respect the boundaries. Be transparent when a boundary is crossed. Celebrate the hybrid approach. | Push to move boundaries. Ridicule limitations. |
| **Building Trust** | Tests systematically, compares, takes notes. Rational, not emotional. | Facilitate the testing phase. Ask about findings. Share results across the team. | Skip this phase or pressure for speed. |
| **Understanding and Overcompensation** | Accepts output uncritically. Pressures others to adopt. Quality drops. | Set quality standards. Address fear-driven dependency individually. Protect team members in earlier phases. | Encourage uncontrolled use. Punish over-enthusiasm. |
| **Stabilisation** | AI is no longer a special topic. A workable balance has been reached. | Schedule periodic check-ins. Leave room for regression. Scale delegation level up gradually. | Declare adoption "complete". Ignore regression. |

## Delegation depth as a planning tool

Not everyone is ready for the same level. Use these four levels to determine what you ask of your team — and when.

| Level | Description | Timeline |
|-------|------------|----------|
| **Information** | Summarising, searching, comparing | Month 1–2 |
| **Task Execution** | Emails, reports, data cleaning | Month 2–4 |
| **Creative Delegation** | Recommendations, drafts, policy briefs | Month 4–8 |
| **Autonomous Partnership** | Analysis, decision-making, quality control | Month 8+ |

Always start at information level. Only scale up when your team is ready — not when the roadmap says so.

## Two types of dropout

- **Emotional dropout:** The cause is identity threat. Intervention: acknowledgement, role redefinition, low delegation levels and individual coaching.
- **Practical dropout:** The cause is poor output or workflow friction. Intervention: better tools, prompt templates and technical support.

Watch out: the wrong intervention for the wrong type makes things worse. You don't fix emotional dropout with a better tool — and you don't fix practical dropout with a good conversation.
