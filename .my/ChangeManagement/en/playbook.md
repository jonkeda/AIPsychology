# Why your AI rollout is stalling — and how to get it moving

The tools are in place. The licences are sorted. There was a kickoff. And yet three quarters of your team barely use AI — or don't use it at all. That's not a technology problem. It's about what AI does to people: their sense of craftsmanship, their professional identity, and their confidence in their own expertise.

This playbook gives you a concrete framework for guiding AI adoption as what it truly is: a psychological change process. Not an IT project with a go-live date, but a journey where people get stuck, disengage, or regress at different moments — and where you, as a leader, make the difference with the right intervention at the right time.

---

## Not all AI use feels the same — and that explains why teams get stuck

Before you understand where someone gets stuck, you need to understand *what* you're asking of them. Using AI to summarise an article feels fundamentally different from letting AI co-author a strategy. The deeper the delegation, the more someone's professional identity is at stake.

### Four levels of delegation

| Level | What you delegate | Example | Psychological impact |
|-------|------------------|---------|---------------------|
| **Information** | AI searches, filters, summarises | "Summarise this report in five bullet points" | Low — feels like a handy assistant |
| **Task execution** | AI performs a concrete task | "Draft this email for me" | Moderate — you're handing over a piece of work |
| **Creative delegation** | AI delivers creative or strategic work | "Create a first draft for this campaign" | High — touches craftsmanship and identity |
| **Autonomous partnership** | AI co-thinks and makes partial decisions | "Analyse this data and propose a recommendation" | Very high — touches on replaceability |

This distinction is crucial for your rollout. Always start at the information level and only move up when the team is ready. People who get stuck at creative delegation don't need more training — they need time and guidance to redefine their relationship with their craft.

### Seven psychological phases

People go through a recognisable pattern during AI adoption. Not linear, not neat — but predictable enough to steer on. The phases are:

- **Dismissal** — "AI isn't relevant to what we do."
- **Resistance** — "We've tried it. It's not good enough."
- **The Hit** — "If AI can do this… what are we even here for?"
- **The Deal** — "We'll use it, but only for X."
- **Building Trust** — "Let me test it properly first."
- **Understanding and Overcompensation** — "I get it — and I want to use it for everything."
- **Stabilisation** — "It's just part of how we work."

Not everyone goes through every phase. People with little emotional attachment to their tasks often skip Resistance and move straight to Building Trust. People with a strong professional identity — writers, designers, strategists — tend to go through the heavier phases.

Below, you'll find what to look for, what to watch out for, and what to do in each phase.

---

## Dismissal — "AI isn't relevant to what we do"

### Recognition

This is the starting point for most teams. AI is something that appears in the news, not something relevant to their own work. You hear statements like "that might work for customer service, but not for what we do" or "by the time I've explained it to the tool, I could have done it myself." There's no active resistance — there's simply no interest.

In meetings, AI doesn't come up. When you raise it, people nod politely and the conversation moves on to the agenda.

**Warning signs:**

- AI never comes up organically in any team meeting
- Colleagues refer to AI as something for "other departments" or "other industries"
- Pilot invitations are ignored or forwarded to someone else
- Reactions aren't negative, just indifferent: "interesting, but not for us"
- Nobody has tried an AI tool on their own

**Intervention:**

- **Do this:** Show, don't tell. Use AI visibly in your own work — share a summary you made with AI, show in a meeting how you accelerated an analysis. Make it concrete and relatable.
- **Do this:** Invite low-stakes experimentation at the information level. "Try once this week to have a long document summarised." No obligation, no reporting.
- **Do this:** Share success stories from comparable roles in comparable organisations. Recognition is more powerful than argumentation.
- **Don't do this:** Don't open with a presentation about the future of AI or the necessity of digital transformation. That confirms the feeling that it's abstract and far away.
- **Don't do this:** Don't mandate use at this stage. Compulsion turns dismissal into resistance — and resistance is much harder to guide.

## Resistance — "We've tried it, but it's not good enough"

### Recognition

The team has tried AI — and is actively looking for shortcomings. Every error gets magnified. Every clumsy output becomes proof that it doesn't work. "See? It doesn't understand the context." This isn't objective evaluation: it's ego protection. People want confirmation that their own expertise is irreplaceable.

You see colleagues deliberately giving AI difficult or unreasonable tasks to prove it fails. Or they compare AI output to their own best work instead of to an average first draft.

Note: not all resistance is emotional. Some people drop off here for purely *practical* reasons — the tool is slow, the output doesn't fit their workflow, the interface is clunky. That distinction is essential (see the chapter on two types of dropout below).

**Warning signs:**

- Colleagues share only failed AI outputs, never successful ones
- "I tried it and it doesn't work" without specific elaboration
- AI tools are tested with deliberately complex or vague prompts
- Sarcastic remarks about AI quality in group conversations
- Refusal to iterate on output — it has to be right first time or it "proves" that it doesn't work

**Intervention:**

- **Do this:** Acknowledge the shortcomings openly. AI makes mistakes. The output isn't perfect. By naming that, you take the wind out of the proof-seeking.
- **Do this:** Reframe the expectation. AI doesn't deliver a finished product — it delivers a starting point. Compare it to an intern delivering a first draft: you don't expect perfection, you expect acceleration.
- **Do this:** Provide guidance on prompt techniques. Much resistance stems from disappointing results that improve with better instructions. Organise an informal session where colleagues improve each other's prompts.
- **Don't do this:** Don't dismiss the criticism with "you need to learn to use it better." That feels like victim blaming and strengthens the resistance.
- **Don't do this:** Don't force usage through resistance. Give space to voice doubts without consequences.

## The Hit — "If AI can do this, what are we even here for?"

### Recognition

This is the emotional core of the entire adoption process. The moment someone realises that AI can do something they considered uniquely theirs. A copywriter who sees AI produce in thirty seconds a text that normally takes an hour. An analyst who notices AI finding patterns they missed. An advisor who realises AI can draft client recommendations that are "actually pretty good."

The intensity correlates directly with the delegation level. For information tasks (summarising, searching), the reaction is mild: surprise, perhaps slight unease. For creative delegation, it can feel like an existential threat. "If a tool can do this, why are they paying me?"

You recognise this by silence. People who are normally vocal become withdrawn. Or the opposite: excessively emphasising what AI *can't* do. Some colleagues start working harder to prove they're indispensable.

**Warning signs:**

- A colleague who goes noticeably quiet after an AI demonstration
- Statements like "they might as well replace us" — even as a joke
- Sudden emphasis on human skills: "AI can't truly be creative"
- Overworking or perfectionism as a compensation mechanism
- Avoiding AI-related conversations after earlier enthusiasm
- Cynicism about the direction of the company or the industry

**Intervention:**

- **Do this:** Normalise the feeling. Name it explicitly: "It makes sense that this affects how you see your work. A lot of people feel the same way." This isn't weakness — it's a sign that someone cares about the craft.
- **Do this:** Redefine the role. Shift the conversation from "AI replaces what I do" to "AI changes how I do it." The copywriter becomes an editor and creative director. The analyst becomes a strategist. The advisor becomes a quality gatekeeper.
- **Do this:** Give concrete examples of how the role becomes *more valuable*. Not abstract ("you'll become more strategic"), but specific: "You assess whether this recommendation is right for *this* client. AI can't do that."
- **Do this:** Create space for one-on-one conversations. This isn't a group discussion — The Hit is personal and vulnerable.
- **Don't do this:** Don't respond with "your job is safe." That's a promise you may not be able to keep, and it doesn't address the underlying feeling.
- **Don't do this:** Don't expect this to pass in a week. The Hit takes time — weeks, sometimes months. Don't push for quick acceptance.
- **Don't do this:** Don't increase the delegation level while someone is in this phase. Stay at the level where they feel comfortable.

## The Deal — "We'll use it, but only for X"

### Recognition

After The Hit, people look for a compromise. "I use AI for first drafts, but I always do the final edit myself." Or: "AI can look up data, but I write the conclusions." This is a healthy intermediate step — a way to maintain control while learning to trust.

The Deal is recognisable by explicit boundaries. Colleagues state precisely what they do and don't use AI for, and actively defend those boundaries. "No, I'm not leaving that to AI — that's real human work."

The risk is that The Deal is fragile. Every time AI does something that crosses the agreed boundary — an unexpectedly strong creative output, an analysis that's better than expected — The Deal gets renegotiated. That can be productive (the boundary shifts) or destabilising (regression to The Hit).

**Warning signs:**

- Rigid boundaries that are disproportionate to the actual quality of AI output
- Strong emotional reactions when someone else uses AI for something "outside The Deal"
- Repeatedly emphasising "but I do the final result"
- Resistance to trying AI on new tasks, even when they logically fit

**Intervention:**

- **Do this:** Respect the boundaries. The Deal is a coping mechanism, and that's fine. Let people set their own pace.
- **Do this:** Be transparent when AI crosses a boundary. "I understand you didn't want to use AI for client advice. Want to see what it produced for team X? No pressure, just for information."
- **Do this:** Celebrate the hybrid approach. State explicitly that human-plus-AI is better than either alone. That reinforces the sense of value.
- **Don't do this:** Don't push for expanding boundaries. The person will do that themselves as trust grows.
- **Don't do this:** Don't ridicule the limitations. "You could just let AI do everything" undermines the process.

## Building Trust — "Let me test it properly first"

### Recognition

This is the rational phase — and one of the most valuable. The emotional charge has subsided. The colleague now wants to systematically figure out what AI can and can't do. You see people deliberately comparing outputs, experimenting with different prompts, keeping notes on when AI performs well and when it doesn't.

This isn't resistance. This is due diligence. Treat it as such.

Some people arrive here directly after the Dismissal phase — particularly colleagues with little emotional attachment to the tasks they're delegating. For them, AI has been a practical question from the start, not an identity question.

**Warning signs:**

- Honestly, there are few *warning* signs in this phase — this is desired behaviour
- Watch for: if the testing phase drags on for months without anyone moving to actual use, there may be a hidden emotional block masquerading as rationality
- Excessive testing can also be avoidance: "I need to test more first" as an excuse not to commit

**Intervention:**

- **Do this:** Facilitate the testing phase. Give people time, access to tools, and space to experiment without performance pressure.
- **Do this:** Ask about findings. "What's working well? What isn't?" This gives you valuable information for the rest of the team and makes the tester feel heard.
- **Do this:** Share test results team-wide. When someone discovers AI works well for a particular task, share that — with attribution. It validates the tester and informs the team.
- **Don't do this:** Don't skip this phase. Mandating use before someone has completed their own trust-building process undermines adoption in the long run.
- **Don't do this:** Don't push for speed. "You've been testing for two weeks now, isn't it time to just start using it?" destroys the very trust you're trying to build.

## Understanding and Overcompensation — from "I get it" to "I want to use it for everything"

### Recognition

The emotional fog lifts. Your colleague understands what AI can and can't do, sees it as a tool, and has developed a workable relationship with the technology. This is a turning point — but it brings a new risk: overcompensation.

Overcompensation has four faces:

1. **Enthusiastic over-adoption** — "Let's use AI for *everything*!" Every task gets run through AI, even when that's slower or worse than doing it manually.
2. **Fear-driven dependency** — "I don't trust my own writing anymore without an AI check." Self-confidence is damaged; AI becomes a crutch rather than an accelerator.
3. **Efficiency obsession** — "Why would you spend time on that manually when AI can do it?" Every minute without AI feels like waste. Context and quality are sacrificed for speed.
4. **Social overcompensation** — "You're not using it yet?" Pressure on colleagues to adopt as well, sometimes from genuine enthusiasm, sometimes from the need to validate one's own choice.

**Warning signs:**

- AI output is accepted uncritically without human review
- Colleagues no longer trust their own work and have AI "check" everything
- Pressure on teammates to use AI, even when they're still in earlier phases
- Quality loss from over-reliance on AI output
- Time saved becomes the only criterion — even where quality or nuance matters more

**Intervention:**

- **Do this:** Set clear quality standards. AI output is a starting point, not a finished product. Define where human judgement remains mandatory — client communications, legal texts, strategic decisions.
- **Do this:** Address fear-driven dependency individually. "Your work was good before AI. AI makes it faster, not better than you." This is a confidence conversation, not a training issue.
- **Do this:** Protect team members still in earlier phases from social pressure by early adopters. "Everyone gets to set their own pace" — and enforce that actively.
- **Don't do this:** Don't encourage unchecked AI use, even when it looks productive. The risks of unreviewed output are real — factual errors, tone-of-voice drift, privacy breaches.
- **Don't do this:** Don't punish over-enthusiasm. Channel it. Turn your early adopters into ambassadors who help others — by invitation, not on their own initiative.

## Stabilisation — "It's just part of how we work"

### Recognition

AI is no longer a topic of discussion. It's a tool, like email or spreadsheets. Colleagues know when to use AI and when not to. There's a workable balance between human judgement and AI support. The work has changed, but so has the professional — and that change no longer feels threatening.

Stabilisation is not an endpoint. It's an equilibrium that can be disrupted by external triggers: a new AI model that delivers significantly better output, a reorganisation that changes roles, an AI error that damages trust. After such a disruption, people can regress to earlier phases — and that's normal.

**Warning signs:**

- Actually: watch for the *absence* of signals. When AI no longer comes up as a special topic, you're here.
- Risk: complacency. "We've got this sorted" while half the team barely uses AI but has stopped complaining about it
- Risk: stagnation. The team uses AI at one level and doesn't explore further

**Intervention:**

- **Do this:** Schedule periodic check-ins. Not as evaluations, but as learning moments. "What have we learned? Where can we go further?"
- **Do this:** Keep space for regression. When a new model comes out or AI makes a notable error, acknowledge that this can cause unease. "It makes sense that this raises questions."
- **Do this:** Continue to gradually increase the delegation level for those who are ready. Stabilisation at the information level is good, but there's more value in creative delegation and autonomous partnership.
- **Don't do this:** Don't declare adoption "complete." It's an ongoing process.
- **Don't do this:** Don't ignore regression. A colleague who falls back to distrust after an AI error doesn't need a repeat of training — they need acknowledgement and space.

## Two types of dropout — and why the difference matters

Not everyone who disengages does so for the same reason. There are two fundamentally different types of dropout, and the wrong intervention makes it worse.

| | **Emotional dropout** | **Practical dropout** |
|---|---|---|
| **Cause** | Identity threat, ego shock, fear of replacement | Poor output, slow tools, workflow friction |
| **Recognition** | Silence, avoidance, cynicism, overcompensating on own work | Concrete complaints: "it doesn't work", "it takes more time than it saves" |
| **Typical statement** | "If AI can do this, why am I even here?" | "I've tried it three times and it produces rubbish" |
| **Emotional charge** | High — touches self-image and professional worth | Low — pragmatic frustration |
| **Wrong intervention** | More training ("you need to learn to prompt better") | Emotional conversations ("how does this make you feel?") |
| **Right intervention** | Acknowledgement, role redefinition, low delegation levels, individual guidance | Better tools, better defaults, prompt templates, workflow integration, technical support |

### Why the distinction is crucial

A colleague who drops out emotionally and then gets sent to a training session doesn't feel heard. The problem isn't that they *can't* use AI — the problem is that AI touches who they are as a professional.

Conversely: a colleague who drops out for practical reasons and then gets a conversation about professional identity doesn't feel taken seriously. The problem is that the tool doesn't work, not that there's an emotional block.

**Rule of thumb:** Listen to the complaint. If it's about *what AI means*, it's emotional. If it's about *what AI does* (or doesn't do), it's practical.

## Start low, build up — a rollout sequence that works

Many organisations make the same mistake: they introduce AI at the creative delegation level ("let AI write your reports") without first building trust at lower levels. That's like putting someone who just learned to ride a bicycle on a racing bike.

### Phase 1: Information (month 1–2)

**What:** Use AI for summarising, searching, comparing, and structuring existing information.

**Examples:** Summarising meeting notes, searching long documents, clustering market intelligence.

**Expected resistance:** Minimal. Most people experience this as useful. Some will ignore it — that's acceptable at this level.

**Action:** Offer two to three concrete use cases that connect directly to daily work. Not a generic workshop, but specific instructions: "Use this tool to summarise your weekly report."

### Phase 2: Task execution (month 2–4)

**What:** Have AI perform concrete tasks that were previously manual.

**Examples:** Drafting emails, generating standard reports, cleaning data.

**Expected resistance:** Moderate. This is where the first resistance appears ("but the tone isn't right") and for some, the first glimpse of The Hit ("this used to take me an hour").

**Action:** Introduce prompt templates for common tasks. Organise informal exchange sessions: "Show how you use AI for your emails." Peer learning works better than formal training here.

### Phase 3: Creative delegation (month 4–8)

**What:** Use AI for work that requires creativity, analysis, or strategic thinking.

**Examples:** First drafts of recommendations, campaign concepts, policy briefs.

**Expected resistance:** High. This is where The Hit strikes for people with a strong professional identity. Expect regression, emotional reactions, and renegotiation of boundaries.

**Action:** Don't rush this. Offer this level as an option, not an expectation. Guide individually. Celebrate hybrid results: "This recommendation got better because you sharpened the AI version."

### Phase 4: Autonomous partnership (month 8+)

**What:** Structurally involve AI in analysis, decision-making, and quality control.

**Examples:** Running scenario analyses, having AI review client recommendations, automated quality checks.

**Expected resistance:** Very high for some, surprisingly low for others who have completed the earlier phases well. The difference lies in the trust built earlier.

**Action:** Define clearly where the human retains final judgement. Autonomous partnership doesn't mean AI decides — it means AI is a full part of the thinking process. Make that boundary explicit.

### Time expectation

Plan for months per phase, not weeks. A full adoption journey from information to autonomous partnership takes most teams eight to twelve months. Teams that try to force this into a single quarter end up with superficial adoption and hidden resistance.

## External triggers — plan for change from outside

AI adoption doesn't happen in a vacuum. External events can accelerate, delay, or reverse the entire process. The key triggers to anticipate:

### New model releases

Every significant leap in AI capability (a new model that performs noticeably better) is a change moment. People who have stabilised can regress to earlier phases when the new model can handle tasks that were previously impossible. This is normal and not a sign of failed adoption.

**What you can do:** Announce major updates before they become available. Frame them as "more possibilities", not "even more replacement." Give the team space to explore the new model at their own pace.

### Top-down mandates

A leadership team that decides everyone must use AI compresses the early phases. Dismissal and Resistance are skipped, not processed. The risk is that the trust-building process gets short-circuited — people use AI because they have to, not because they trust it.

**What you can do:** Translate the mandate into a realistic pace. "Leadership wants us to integrate AI. We will — step by step, in a way that works for our team." Protect the trust phase, even under pressure.

### Media and public opinion

Headlines about AI replacing jobs amplify The Hit. Stories about AI errors feed Resistance. Positive stories accelerate acceptance — but can also create unrealistic expectations.

**What you can do:** Discuss media coverage actively with your team. Not to debunk it, but to put it in perspective. "This article is about a specific situation. Let's look at what it means for us."

### Adoption by peers

Nothing accelerates adoption as strongly as seeing a respected colleague use AI successfully. And nothing slows it down as much as seeing a colleague who tried it walk away disappointed.

**What you can do:** Facilitate visible success. Ask early adopters to share their experiences — not as evangelists, but as colleagues who tell you what works and what doesn't.

### AI errors and incidents

A notable AI error — a factual inaccuracy in a client document, a privacy incident, an output that's painfully off — can destroy trust in one blow. Not just for the person who experienced it, but for the entire team.

**What you can do:** Be honest about the error. Analyse what went wrong and which controls were missing. Use it as a learning opportunity, not a reason to stop. "This is exactly why we build in human review. The system failed; the control worked."

## Where does your team stand right now? — a quick diagnosis

Answer the following questions about your team. Be honest — the answers are only for you.

**1. How does your team react when AI comes up?**
- Indifferent or absent → probably **Dismissal**
- Critical, looking for flaws → probably **Resistance**
- Quiet, withdrawn, or defensive → probably **The Hit**
- "We only use that for X" → probably **The Deal**

**2. Has anyone on your team systematically tested and compared AI?**
- No, nobody → your team is pre-**Building Trust**
- Yes, and they now use it selectively → **Understanding** reached
- Yes, and they want to use it for everything → check for **Overcompensation**

**3. Are there colleagues who actively avoid AI?**
- Yes, with concrete complaints about the tool → **practical dropout**
- Yes, with vague unease or cynicism → **emotional dropout**
- No → good sign, but check it isn't invisible avoidance

**4. At what delegation level does your team use AI?**
- Not at all or barely → start at **information level**
- Summarising and searching → ready for **task execution**
- Tasks and first drafts → evaluate readiness for **creative delegation**
- Co-thinking and co-deciding → **autonomous partnership** — monitor for overcompensation

**5. How does your team respond to AI errors?**
- "See, it doesn't work" → **Resistance**, possibly reinforced by the error
- "Unfortunate, but we learn from it" → healthy **Stabilisation**
- Panic or loss of trust → **regression** — identify which phase

**6. Is there social pressure around AI use in your team?**
- Yes, pressure to use it → check for **social overcompensation**
- Yes, pressure not to use it → group norm reinforcing **Dismissal** or **Resistance**
- No → neutral playing field, good starting point

**7. How long has your team been working with AI?**
- Not started yet → start at Dismissal, plan for months
- A few weeks → expect Resistance and first signs of The Hit
- A few months → evaluate where individuals are — the team is probably spread across multiple phases
- More than six months → look at Stabilisation, but check for hidden Resistance or practical dropout

### What do you do with the results?

Find the phase in this playbook that best matches the majority of your team. Read the corresponding interventions. And remember: your team probably isn't in a single phase — individuals diverge. The art is guiding everyone at their own level without slowing down the frontrunners or forcing the stragglers.

## Three things you can do tomorrow

Guiding AI adoption doesn't have to start with a programme, a budget, or a strategy. It starts with paying attention to what's happening in your team.

**1. Use AI yourself — visibly.** Open your next meeting with: "I used AI to prepare the agenda — here's what it came up with." Show that it's normal, that it's not perfect, and that you're learning too. Leading by example is the most powerful intervention you have.

**2. Have one conversation.** Pick the colleague who's most reluctant. Not to convince them, but to listen. "How do you see AI fitting into our work?" The answer tells you whether the issue is emotional or practical — and with that, which support will work.

**3. Start at the information level.** Choose one concrete task your team does daily that AI can accelerate without it feeling like replacement. A summary, a search query, a comparison. Start small, make it visible, give space.

The core of this entire playbook in one sentence: AI adoption isn't an IT project with a go-live date — it's a human process that requires guidance, patience, and psychological insight. The technology is ready. The question is whether you're ready to lead your team through it.
