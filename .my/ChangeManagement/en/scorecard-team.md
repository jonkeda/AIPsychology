# Where is your team? — A scorecard for leaders

This card helps you map where your team stands in the AI adoption process. Not by asking them to fill in a form, but by scoring what you observe. Be honest about what you see, not what you hope.

---

## How to use this scorecard

Read each statement and score how strongly you recognise this behaviour **in your team right now**:

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **0** | No, I don't see this |
| **1** | In one or two people / occasionally |
| **2** | In several people / regularly |

Add up per block. After scoring each block, note how widespread the behaviour is — that spread matters as much as the score itself. The phase mapping is revealed in the results section.

## Bonus — Emotional or practical?

Two extra statements. Score each 0 / 1 / 2:

| | Statement | Score |
|---|-----------|-------|
| a | "The resistance I see is mainly emotional — about roles, identity, and fear." | _ |
| b | "The resistance I see is mainly practical — about tool quality, workflow fit, and output reliability." | _ |

**Reading your bonus score:**

- **a > b** → The resistance is mainly emotional. Pushing harder on tools and training won't help — you need to address the underlying concerns about identity and value.
- **b > a** → The resistance is mainly practical. Better tools, clearer use cases, and workflow integration will move things forward.
- **a = b** → Both factors are at play. You'll need to work on both fronts.

## Results — What does your score tell you?

Your highest-scoring block maps to one of these phases:

### Block A → Dismissal — "AI isn't relevant to us"

Your team hasn't engaged with AI in any meaningful way. Conversations about it fizzle out. You'll notice a lack of curiosity rather than active opposition. **Right intervention:** don't mandate — create low-pressure opportunities to experiment. Show, don't tell. See the [intervention card](intervention-card.md) for specific approaches.

### Block B → Resistance — "It's not good enough"

Your team is actively pushing back. You'll hear cherry-picked failures, impossibly high benchmarks, and dismissive humour. This often masks deeper concerns. **Right intervention:** acknowledge the valid criticisms, then redirect to realistic use cases. Don't argue about AI's potential — show what it does today. The [intervention card](intervention-card.md) has detailed tactics.

### Block C → The Hit / Ego Shock — "What's my role now?"

Some team members are quietly struggling with what AI means for their professional identity. You'll see withdrawal, overperformance, or jokes that land a bit too hard. This is the most sensitive phase. **Right intervention:** create space for honest conversation. Name the elephant in the room yourself first. Never dismiss these feelings as irrational. See the [playbook](playbook.md) for how to facilitate this.

### Block D → The Deal / Bargaining — "Only for X"

Your team has drawn lines around AI use — often rigid ones. You'll see strong reactions when boundaries are crossed and an emphasis on human-final-check workflows. **Right intervention:** respect the boundaries but keep them visible and revisable. Schedule regular "boundary reviews" where the team decides together what to adjust. The [intervention card](intervention-card.md) covers this in detail.

### Block E → Building Trust — "Let us test first"

Part of your team is in productive exploration mode. You'll see methodical testing, comparisons, and practical discussions replacing emotional ones. **Right intervention:** support this with time, tools, and a way to share findings. This is where internal champions emerge — give them a platform. The [intervention card](intervention-card.md) shows you how.

### Block F → Understanding and Overcompensation — "Use it for everything!"

Some team members have swung to uncritical AI adoption. You'll see quality drops, reduced checking, and social pressure on others to follow suit. **Right intervention:** reintroduce quality standards without killing enthusiasm. Make AI-output review a team norm, not an individual burden. The [intervention card](intervention-card.md) has practical frameworks.

### Block G → Stabilisation — "Just a tool"

AI has found its place in the team's workflow. It's no longer a special topic — it's just how things are done, where it makes sense. **Right intervention:** maintain this by staying open to new capabilities and keeping the team's critical eye sharp. Don't let stabilisation become stagnation. The [playbook](playbook.md) covers long-term maintenance.

## Key insight for leaders

Your team is probably not in one single phase. Individuals are spread across multiple phases — and that's the reality you're managing. The score tells you where the **centre of gravity** is. The "Spread" column tells you how evenly distributed the behaviours are.

A team with a high Block C score concentrated in 1–2 people needs a different approach than one where the majority is in Block C. The first needs a private conversation; the second needs a team-level intervention.

**Watch for the gap between your highest and second-highest blocks.** A large gap means a clear dominant phase. A small gap means your team is transitioning — handle with care.
