# The identity crisis that nobody budgeted for

*AI's biggest disruption may be psychological, not economic*

---

WHEN a management consultant in Amsterdam watched a colleague produce a corporate strategy in three minutes using AI, she did not feel excited. She felt empty. She had spent 15 years honing precisely that skill. That evening she searched for examples of AI getting things spectacularly wrong.

Her reaction follows a pattern now visible across industries. It is not the rational adoption curve that economists expected. It is something messier, more human, and far less discussed.

It begins with dismissal. *Overhyped. Makes errors. Cannot truly think.* This reads as scepticism but functions as self-defence. Genuine evaluation costs effort; dismissal is free. Then comes active resistance: searching for flaws not to assess the technology fairly, but to find reasons to discount it. Every hallucination becomes a shield.

Then comes the reckoning. The consultant tries AI herself, half-hoping it will fail. The output is mediocre—but when she edits it, the work takes 15 minutes instead of half a day. *If this thing can build the framework, what is the part that only I can do?*

The intensity depends on what is delegated. Summarising an article provokes no crisis. Drafting a creative strategy—the work that defines a professional self-image—can provoke something closer to grief. Writers, designers, programmers, strategists: anyone whose identity is woven into a skill AI can replicate hits this point.

Most people do not resolve it cleanly. They bargain. *First drafts, yes. Final version, mine.* This works—sometimes for years. But each new model forces renegotiation. The boundary shifts inward, closer to the core.

Some quit entirely, and here a crucial distinction is overlooked. Some quit because AI threatens their identity—an emotional response. Others quit because the tool simply does not work well enough—a usability judgment. The interventions are opposite. Better documentation will not cure an identity crisis. A supportive conversation will not fix bad software.

Those who push through often overcorrect: uncritical enthusiasm, anxious dependence, or adoption driven purely by social pressure. All unstable. The pendulum seeks rest.

Eventually, most find equilibrium. Some develop mature, selective use. Some expand their identity—the designer who now makes films. Some burn out and step back. Some become guides, helping others through the cycle they have traversed.

But the cycle is not linear. A new model, a junior colleague outperforming with AI, a high-profile failure—any of these can restart it. People do not pass through once. They spiral: each revolution shorter and less intense, but a revolution nonetheless.

The discussion about AI has focused on capabilities, economics, and regulation. It has largely ignored the psychological disruption now unfolding among millions of knowledge workers forced to answer a question they had comfortably avoided: *if a machine can do the thing I spent years learning, who am I?*

The consultant recognises the spiral now. That, perhaps, is the real adaptation.
