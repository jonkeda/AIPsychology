# The identity crisis that nobody budgeted for

*As artificial intelligence reshapes the workplace, the biggest disruption may not be economic but psychological*

---

WHEN a management consultant in Amsterdam recently watched a colleague produce a passable corporate strategy in three minutes using an AI model, she did not feel excited. She felt, as she later described it, "a sort of emptiness". She had spent 15 years honing precisely the skill the machine had just approximated over a cup of coffee. That evening she spent 45 minutes searching for examples of AI getting things catastrophically wrong.

Her reaction was not unusual. Across industries and continents, a remarkably consistent psychological pattern is emerging among knowledge workers confronted with capable AI. It is not the pattern that economists predicted—a rational cost-benefit analysis followed by adoption or rejection. It is something messier, more human, and considerably less well understood.

**A predictable spiral**

The sequence runs roughly as follows. First comes dismissal: the tool is overhyped, it makes errors, it cannot truly think. This is presented as level-headed scepticism but functions, psychologically, as a defence mechanism. Genuine evaluation requires cognitive effort; dismissal is cheaper.

Next comes active resistance. People who feel their professional identity is threatened begin searching for flaws—not to assess the technology fairly, but to find reasons to discount it. Every hallucination becomes evidence. Every limitation becomes a shield. The search for deficiencies masquerades as objectivity but is, at root, fear.

Then comes what might be called the reckoning. The consultant tries the AI herself, half-hoping it will fail. The output is mediocre. But when she edits it, the work takes 15 minutes rather than half a day. The structure is sound, the logic solid. She adds her own experience and context, and the result is better than her usual output. The relief she felt at the AI's initial mediocrity evaporates. *If this thing can build the framework,* she thinks, *what is the part that only I can do?*

The intensity of this moment depends on what is being delegated. Asking AI to summarise an article provokes no crisis. Asking it to draft a creative strategy—the sort of work that defines a person's professional self-image—can provoke something closer to grief.

**The bargain and its fragility**

Most people do not resolve this crisis in one go. Instead, they negotiate. *I will use it for first drafts, but the final version is mine. It can write the code; I do the thinking.* This bargaining is not a failure of adoption. It is a stabilisation strategy, and many people operate productively within it for months or years.

But it is inherently fragile. Each new model release, each capability jump, forces renegotiation. The self-imposed boundaries shift inward. What was "mine" last quarter is now shared territory. The deal must be struck again, and again, and again.

**Two kinds of quitting**

Some people abandon AI entirely during this process. Policymakers and managers tend to treat all such departures alike, but they should not. Some people quit because the technology threatens their identity—an emotional response that might be eased by gradual exposure, social support, and low-stakes initial tasks. Others quit because the tool simply does not work well enough: the outputs are poor, the prompting is tedious, the workflow integration is clumsy. This is a usability judgment, not an existential one. Confusing the two leads to precisely the wrong interventions. Better documentation will not cure an identity crisis. And a supportive conversation will not fix bad software.

**The overshoot**

Those who do push through tend to overcorrect. Some become uncritical enthusiasts, intoxicated by the dopamine of a new creative superpower. Others, whose confidence was eroded by the reckoning, become dependent—unable to trust their own output without AI validation. A growing number adopt AI not out of genuine enthusiasm but because everyone around them has, and the social cost of abstention feels too high.

None of these responses is inherently destructive. But all are unstable. The pendulum seeks a resting point.

**Where it settles**

Most people eventually find equilibrium—though not all the same one. Some develop a mature working relationship with AI, deploying it selectively and with clear-eyed understanding of its limits. Some expand their identity: the designer who now makes films, the writer who now builds software. Some turn philosophical, grappling with questions about creativity, authorship, and what human cognition is actually for.

Some burn out. The relentless pace of adaptation, the constant renegotiation of professional identity, the feeling that the ground will not stop shifting—it exhausts them. They step back, temporarily or permanently. This is not a failure. It is a legitimate outcome.

And some become evangelists, guiding others through the cycle they have already traversed. Their experience of the full journey—from dismissal through crisis to understanding—makes them unusually effective at it.

**The spiral, not the line**

The tempting narrative is linear: from ignorance to mastery, resistance to acceptance. Reality is recursive. A new model release can send someone who has been in equilibrium for months tumbling back into the reckoning. A junior colleague outperforming with AI triggers fresh identity questions. A high-profile AI failure rekindles doubt. People do not pass through this cycle once. They pass through it repeatedly, each time AI takes a step that disrupts their mental model.

This is not pathological. It is how adaptation works—not as a straight line, but as a spiral. Each revolution is shorter and less intense than the last. But it is a revolution nonetheless.

The cultural context matters, too. In societies with strong craft traditions—academia, literary culture, artisanal trades—the resistance and identity phases are more intense and more prolonged. In techno-optimistic environments, the early stages compress but the overshoot hits harder. In hierarchical organisations, mandates from above can force compliance without emotional processing, which tends to surface later as a delayed crisis.

**The question beneath the question**

The discussion about AI adoption has focused overwhelmingly on capabilities, economics, and regulation. These matter enormously. But they obscure a more fundamental disruption: the psychological one. Millions of people are being forced to answer a question that most had comfortably avoided for their entire careers. *If a machine can do the thing I have spent years learning to do, who am I?*

It is not a new question. Every wave of technological change has posed some version of it. But AI poses it with unusual breadth—reaching not just factory workers and clerks, but writers, strategists, designers, programmers, lawyers, and academics. The class of people who thought themselves immune to automation is discovering that they are not.

The Amsterdam consultant uses AI daily now. She is good at it—better than most of her colleagues. She has found a way of working that augments her expertise rather than replacing it. But she knows the next model is coming, and the next boundary will shift, and the next turn of the spiral awaits.

The difference is that she recognises the spiral now. And that, perhaps, is the real adaptation: not learning to use the tool, but learning to live with the question it keeps asking.
