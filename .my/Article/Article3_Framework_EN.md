# Where are you on the AI spiral?

You've probably tried AI by now. Maybe you use it daily. Maybe you tried it once and quietly stopped. Maybe you're somewhere in between, using it for some things while drawing a firm line around others.

Wherever you are — that's fine. But it helps to know where you are. Because the way people adapt to AI follows a surprisingly recognisable pattern, and knowing the pattern makes the ride a lot less confusing.

## "It's just hype"

The most common first reaction. Not because you're uninformed, but because dismissal is the easiest response to something that might change how you work. Evaluating a tool that could reshape your role takes real energy. Waving it off costs nothing.

Some people genuinely feel no threat — AI touches tasks, not talents they identify with. They move on to curiosity quickly. But if your professional identity is closely tied to skills AI can now replicate — writing, analysis, strategy, design, coding — dismissal often shifts into something sharper.

## "I'll find the flaws"

You try it, but with your guard up. Every mistake confirms your suspicion. Every hallucination is proof. This feels like objective evaluation, but if you're honest with yourself, you might notice that you're not testing — you're defending.

That's not irrational. It's human. Something feels at stake.

## "Wait — it can do *that*?"

This is the moment the defences don't hold. AI produces something genuinely good in your domain. Not perfect, but good enough that you finish the work in a fraction of the time. The relief you expected doesn't come. Instead: *if this thing can do what I spent years learning, what's the part that's uniquely mine?*

The intensity depends on what you're handing over. Summarising an article? No big deal. Drafting a strategy you'd normally spend half a day on? That hits differently.

This is the most uncomfortable stage. And it can come back — every time a new model leaps past what you thought was the boundary.

## "I'll use it, but only for X"

Most people don't resolve the discomfort in one go. Instead, you draw a line. *First drafts, yes. Final version, mine. It writes the boilerplate; I do the thinking.* This works. You're productive, you use AI, and you protect the part of your work that feels most like you.

But each model update nudges that line. What was safely on your side last month is now shared territory. The deal holds — until it doesn't.

## "Actually, I get it now"

Gradually, the fog lifts. You understand what AI does well and where it falls short. You know where your judgment matters. You stop feeling like you're cheating and start feeling like you've got a sharper set of tools.

This is real stability — but it needs two things: your own understanding *and* a sense that the people around you see it the same way. If you're the only one on your team who's comfortable with AI, the equilibrium feels fragile. If everyone around you uses it but you haven't fully processed it yourself, you might be complying rather than understanding.

## "I want to use it for everything"

Some people overshoot. Pure enthusiasm — the thrill of a creative superpower. Or the opposite: the earlier discomfort eroded your confidence, and now you don't trust your own output without AI. Or simply: everyone else is doing it, and you don't want to fall behind.

None of these are problems in themselves. But they're not stable either. The pendulum swings back.

## Where it settles

Most people find a balance eventually. Clear-eyed use, based on understanding rather than fear. Some expand what they can do — taking on work that wasn't possible before. Some step back for a while because the pace of change is exhausting. That's legitimate, not failure.

And the whole thing can restart. A new model. A colleague who suddenly outperforms you with AI. A spectacular AI failure that reignites doubt. You don't go through this once — you spiral through it, each loop shorter and less intense than the last.

## Why this matters

Recognising where you are isn't about self-diagnosis. It's about cutting yourself some slack. Resistance isn't ignorance. Bargaining isn't weakness. Overenthusiasm isn't naivety. They're all stages in a process that everyone goes through — at their own pace, in their own way.

So: where are you right now?
