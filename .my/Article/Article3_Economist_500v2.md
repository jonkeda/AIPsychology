# The identity crisis that nobody budgeted for

*AI's biggest disruption may be psychological, not economic*

---

WHEN a management consultant in Amsterdam recently watched a colleague produce a passable corporate strategy in three minutes using an AI model, she did not feel the thrill of discovery; she felt, as she later described it, a kind of emptiness, the particular hollow sensation of watching a machine approximate the skill she had spent 15 years refining. That evening she devoted 45 minutes to searching for examples of AI getting things spectacularly wrong, which she found oddly comforting.

Her reaction, it turns out, is not unusual. Across industries, a remarkably consistent psychological pattern has emerged among knowledge workers confronted with capable AI—and it bears little resemblance to the rational adoption curve that economists predicted. It begins with dismissal, which presents itself as sober scepticism but functions as a defence mechanism; genuinely evaluating a technology that might threaten one's professional identity requires considerable cognitive effort, whereas writing it off as hype costs nothing at all. When dismissal becomes untenable—because the results are too good, or the boss has mandated adoption—it gives way not to acceptance but to active resistance: a determined search for flaws whose purpose is not fair assessment but psychological protection.

Then comes what might be called the reckoning. The consultant, half out of curiosity and half hoping to confirm her doubts, uses the AI to draft a strategy herself. The raw output is mediocre, which brings relief; but when she edits it, the work takes a quarter of an hour rather than half a day, and the result is at least as good as her usual product. The relief evaporates. The question that replaces it—*if this thing can build the framework, what exactly is the part that only I can do?*—is not a technical one but an existential one, and its intensity depends almost entirely on how close the delegated task sits to the core of a person's professional identity.

Most people resolve this crisis not through a clean epiphany but through bargaining: *first drafts, yes; final version, mine.* The arrangement works, sometimes for years, but it is inherently fragile, because each new model release forces renegotiation and the boundary between human and machine contribution shifts steadily inward. Some people abandon AI altogether during this process, and here a distinction that managers consistently overlook becomes important: some leave because the technology threatens their sense of self, an emotional response requiring gradual exposure and social support; others leave because the tool simply produces poor results, a usability judgment requiring better software. The interventions are not merely different but opposite.

Those who persist tend to overcorrect before they stabilise—through uncritical enthusiasm, anxious dependence, or adoption driven less by conviction than by the social cost of abstention. Eventually, most find equilibrium, though not all the same kind: some develop selective, clear-eyed use; some expand their identity into domains AI has opened to them; some burn out and step back, which is a legitimate outcome rather than a failure.

But the process is recursive, not linear. A new model, a junior colleague outperforming with AI assistance, a high-profile hallucination that rekindles doubt—any of these can restart the cycle. The real adaptation, the consultant has come to think, is not learning to use the tool but learning to recognise the spiral; to know that the ground will shift again, and that this is not a deficiency in one's character but simply how human beings absorb change when the change keeps coming.
