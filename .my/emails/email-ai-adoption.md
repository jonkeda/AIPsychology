---

## The pattern

When people start using AI for meaningful work, they tend to move through a sequence of psychological stages. Not everyone hits every stage, and the order isn't always rigid, but the pattern is consistent enough to plan around.

**Stage 0 — Pre-Exposure.** "AI is irrelevant to me." No opinion, no engagement. Many of our people are still here.

**Stage 1 — Dismissal.** "It won't work. It's overhyped." A protective stance. People avoid the cognitive and emotional effort of engaging. This splits depending on how strongly someone's identity is tied to the skills AI can replicate — people with low attachment often skip straight to curiosity, while those with high attachment move into resistance.

**Stage 2 — Defensive Resistance.** "I'll try it, but I'm going to find flaws." People nitpick, criticise, and highlight weaknesses — not because the tool is bad, but because it threatens their sense of competence. This is ego defence, not objective evaluation.

**Stage 3 — Ego Shock.** "If AI can do this… what am I worth?" This is the stage nobody talks about openly. When the defences collapse and someone realises AI can do work they trained years for, it hits hard. Anxiety, grief, loss of identity, fear of obsolescence. The intensity scales with what's being delegated — using AI to summarise an article feels different from watching it produce creative work at your level.

**Stage 3B — Bargaining.** "I'll use it, but only for X." People set artificial boundaries: "It can draft, but I do the final version." "It handles code, not writing — writing is *me*." This is a coping mechanism that preserves identity. Many people stay here for months. It works, until a capability jump breaks the bargain.

**Stage 4 — Trust Evaluation.** "Let me test this carefully." A rational assessment phase — distinct from emotional resistance. People systematically test AI on known problems, compare outputs, and evaluate reliability. Not all caution is fear. Some of it is due diligence.

**Stage 5 — Understanding.** "It's powerful, but it's just a tool." The emotional fog clears. People learn what AI is good at, where human judgement matters, and how to use it effectively. This is the first stable phase — but it requires both personal reframing *and* social normalisation. If someone reaches understanding but nobody around them uses AI, they feel like an outlier.

**Stage 6 — Overcompensation.** "I want to use it for everything." The pendulum swings too far. This can be enthusiasm-driven, anxiety-driven ("I don't trust my own abilities anymore"), efficiency-driven, or socially driven ("Everyone else is using it, I need to keep up"). Not destructive, but unstable.

**Stage 7 — Stabilisation.** People settle into one of several long-term patterns: healthy integration, identity expansion (AI becomes part of who they are), ethical reflection, burnout/withdrawal, or evangelism (they start helping others adopt). The cycle can also loop — a major model release or capability jump can send someone back to earlier stages.

## Why this matters for change management

Most AI rollout plans treat adoption as a training problem: give people access, show them how to prompt, measure usage. That works for the rational stages (Trust Evaluation, Understanding) but completely misses the emotional ones (Resistance, Ego Shock, Bargaining). And it's the emotional stages where adoption stalls, resentment builds, and people quietly disengage.

Here's what I think this means for us specifically:

### 1. Our rollout speed needs to match emotional readiness, not just technical readiness

If we mandate AI adoption before people have processed Stages 2–3, we don't skip those stages — we compress them. People comply on the surface but resist underneath. Delayed ego shock is harder to manage than the upfront version because it looks like disengagement or quality decline, not resistance.

**Implication for the CHRO and Director:** Adoption timelines should account for emotional processing, not just training completion. Forcing the pace creates compliance without buy-in.

### 2. Managers need to recognise the stages — especially ego shock

Most managers will interpret Stage 2 (Defensive Resistance) as stubbornness and Stage 3 (Ego Shock) as a performance problem. Neither is correct. Both are predictable psychological responses to identity threat. If managers respond with pressure or performance management, they will make it worse.

**Implication for all:** Managers across the organisation need a basic understanding of these stages so they can support people through them rather than punishing them for having a normal human reaction.

### 3. The people who resist loudest may be your most valuable adopters

High-identity users — the ones with the most expertise, the strongest craft identity, the most to lose — are the ones who hit Stages 2 and 3 hardest. They're also the ones who, once through it, become the most effective AI users and the most credible evangelists (Stage 7F). If we alienate them during resistance, we lose our best potential advocates.

**Implication for the CTO and Director:** Don't mistake resistance for incompetence. Your strongest resisters may be your strongest eventual champions.

### 4. Not all resistance is emotional — some of it is the tool's fault

The model distinguishes emotional dropout (identity threat, fear, burnout) from practical dropout (bad outputs, workflow friction, prompt engineering fatigue). These require completely different interventions. You can't fix practical dropout with psychological support, and you can't fix emotional dropout with better documentation.

**Implication for the CTO:** If people are dropping off, diagnose *why* before choosing the intervention. Survey for both emotional and practical barriers separately.

### 5. Social normalisation is as important as individual training

A person can understand AI perfectly (Stage 5) and still feel like an outlier if their team doesn't use it. Adoption is social, not just individual. Peer adoption accelerates phase transitions; peer scepticism reinforces early stages.

**Implication for the CHRO and Director:** Create visible communities of practice. Let early adopters be seen. Normalise AI use through team culture, not just policy.

### 6. Every major model release resets the cycle

This isn't a one-time change management exercise. Each significant AI capability jump — and they're coming regularly — can push people who've reached Understanding back to Defensive Resistance or Ego Shock. The cycle loops. Our change management approach needs to be ongoing, not a one-off programme.

**Implication for the CEO and CTO:** Budget and plan for continuous adaptation support, not a single rollout.

### 7. Bargaining is where most of the organisation will sit

Stage 3B — "I'll use it for drafts but not final work" — is where the majority of knowledge workers will land for months, possibly years. This is productive and stable, and it shouldn't be rushed. Forcing people past bargaining before they're ready triggers ego shock.

**Implication for all:** Partial adoption is not failed adoption. It's a healthy coping strategy. Meet people where they are.
