## The cycle

People don't adopt AI rationally. They go through an emotional sequence:

**0. Pre-Exposure**
Most people start here. AI simply isn't on their radar. No opinion, no reaction, no relevance.

**1. Dismissal**
"It's overhyped." A self-protective response. People underestimate AI to avoid confronting what it might mean for their worldview and identity.

**2. Defensive Resistance**
"See? It got that wrong." People find flaws to feel safe. This is ego defence — not objective evaluation. The criticism protects their sense of competence.

**3. Ego Shock**
"What am I worth?" The phase nobody talks about openly. When defences collapse, people face identity crisis — anxiety, grief, fear of obsolescence, existential discomfort.

**3B. Bargaining**
"I'll use it for drafts, not final work." People negotiate partial adoption to preserve identity. This is a coping mechanism, not a failure. Many stay here for months or years.

**4. Trust Evaluation**
"Let me test this properly." A rational assessment phase — distinct from emotional resistance. People systematically test AI on known problems and evaluate reliability. Not all caution is fear.

**5. Understanding**
"It's a tool. I can work with this." The first stable phase. People learn what AI is good at and where human judgement matters. But stability requires social normalisation too — not just personal reframing.

**6. Overcompensation**
"I want to use it for everything." The pendulum swings too far. Can be enthusiasm-driven, anxiety-driven ("I don't trust my own abilities anymore"), efficiency-driven, or socially driven ("everyone else is using it").

**7. Stabilisation**
People settle into one of several long-term patterns: healthy integration, identity expansion, ethical reflection, evangelism — or burnout and withdrawal. The cycle also loops. Every major model release can push someone back to earlier stages.

The intensity of each stage depends on two things: how strongly someone's identity is tied to skills AI can replicate, and how much cognitive authority they're handing over. Summarising articles is psychologically trivial. Watching AI produce creative work at your level is an identity event.

The cycle also **loops**. Every major model release can push someone who's reached Understanding back to Resistance or Ego Shock.

---

## What this means for us

**1. Mandating AI without emotional readiness creates hidden resistance.** People comply but disengage. If we force the pace, we get surface adoption with underground resentment. The CHRO and Director should factor emotional readiness into adoption timelines, not just training completion.

**2. Your loudest resisters may be your best future champions.** High-skill people resist hardest because they have the most to lose — and the most to gain. If we alienate them during Stage 2, we lose the people who would eventually become our most credible advocates.

**3. Bargaining is where most of the organisation will sit — and that's fine.** "I'll use it for X but not Y" is a healthy coping strategy, not failed adoption. Pushing people past it before they're ready triggers ego shock. Meet people where they are.

**4. Not all resistance is emotional.** Sometimes the tool gives bad outputs, breaks the workflow, or takes too long to learn. Practical dropout and emotional dropout need different interventions — the CTO should diagnose which one is happening before choosing a response.

**5. Adoption is social, not just individual.** Someone can understand AI perfectly and still feel like an outlier if their team doesn't use it. Visible communities of practice and peer normalisation matter as much as training.

**6. This isn't a one-off rollout.** Every capability jump resets the cycle. We need ongoing adaptation support, not a single change programme.
