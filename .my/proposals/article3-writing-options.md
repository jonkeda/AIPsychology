# Article 3 — Writing Options by Audience

Options for turning the AI Psychological Adaptation Cycle V3 into targeted articles for different readers.

---

## Option A: LinkedIn / Professional Audience

**Title idea:** "The 5 Stages of Coming to Terms with AI at Work"

**Target:** Managers, team leads, HR professionals, L&D professionals

**Format:** 1,200–1,800 words. Conversational, benefit-first.

**What to keep:**
- Delegation-depth axis (reframed as "not all AI use feels the same")
- Bargaining (Stage 3B) — this is the most recognizable pattern for managers watching teams adopt
- Emotional vs. practical dropout — actionable distinction for leaders
- Regression loop — "your team's comfort with AI isn't permanent"

**What to cut:**
- Version numbering and changelog
- Cultural variability section (too academic)
- Stage subtypes (6A–6D, 7A–7F) — collapse into themes
- The ⚡ notation

**Angle:** "Here's why your team reacts differently to AI — and what to do about it." Practical, prescriptive. End with 3–5 things a manager can actually do at each stage.

**Tone:** Driven + Personal. Direct, no jargon. Address the reader as "you."

---

## Option B: Psychology / Academic Audience

**Title idea:** "A Two-Axis Model of Psychological Adaptation to Generative AI"

**Target:** Researchers, organizational psychologists, academics in technology adoption

**Format:** 4,000–6,000 words. Structured as a conceptual paper or working paper.

**What to keep:**
- Full stage model with all subtypes
- Both axes developed thoroughly (build out the stage × delegation-depth matrix the review calls for)
- Cultural variability — expand with citations
- External trigger and social context layers — formalize with mechanisms
- Dropout typology

**What to add:**
- Literature grounding: link stages to Kübler-Ross, Rogers' diffusion of innovation, identity threat theory, self-determination theory
- A full stage × delegation-depth interaction matrix
- Testable propositions (e.g., "Individuals with high craft identity will spend longer in Stage 3B than those with low craft identity")
- Limitations section acknowledging speculative elements

**What to fix:**
- Drop "cycle" — call it a model or framework
- Remove version numbering — present as standalone
- Replace ⚡ with formal notation or inline references
- Qualify Stage 3 intensity claim by delegation depth

**Tone:** Reliable. Precise, measured, evidence-aware. Third person.

---

## Option C: General / Blog Audience

**Title idea:** "Why AI Makes You Feel Weird (And Why That's Normal)"

**Target:** Individual knowledge workers, creatives, developers, writers — anyone who's felt uncomfortable using AI

**Format:** 1,500–2,500 words. First/second person. Informal, relatable.

**What to keep:**
- The emotional arc: dismissal → resistance → ego shock → bargaining → understanding → stabilization
- Delegation depth reframed as relatable scenarios ("asking AI to summarize vs. asking it to write your strategy")
- Bargaining — quote the exact lines ("I'll use it for first drafts, but I always do the final version")
- Regression as normal ("a new model drops and you're right back to feeling threatened")
- Overcompensation subtypes — people will recognize themselves

**What to cut:**
- Stage numbering (use narrative flow instead)
- Tables and matrices
- Cultural variability
- External trigger layer (weave in as anecdotes instead of a formal layer)

**Angle:** "Here's the emotional journey most people go through with AI — and where you probably are right now." Validation-first. Make the reader feel seen, not analyzed.

**Tone:** Personal + Driven. Warm, direct, no academic distance. "You" throughout.

---

## Option D: Organizational / Change Management Audience

**Title idea:** "An AI Adoption Playbook: Understanding the Psychology Behind Resistance"

**Target:** Change managers, CTOs, digital transformation leads, consultants

**Format:** 2,500–4,000 words. Framework-oriented with actionable guidance per stage.

**What to keep:**
- Full stage model (simplified labels)
- Delegation-depth axis as a planning tool ("start people at D1, not D3")
- Trust Evaluation (Stage 4) — this is the stage change managers most need to understand and protect
- Emotional vs. practical dropout — different interventions for each
- Social context layer — peer adoption, normalization, mandates
- External triggers — model releases as change events to plan for

**What to add:**
- Intervention recommendations at each stage
- "Warning signs" that someone is regressing
- A rollout sequence based on delegation depth (start low, escalate gradually)
- A simple diagnostic ("where is your team right now?")

**What to cut:**
- Stage 7 subtypes (simplify to "they settle into a pattern")
- Cultural variability (unless writing for a multinational audience)
- Version numbering

**Tone:** Reliable + Driven. Authoritative, structured, prescriptive. The reader should walk away with a plan.

## Option E: Newsletter / Short-Form Series

**Title idea:** "The AI Feelings Series" (5–7 installments)

**Target:** Broad professional audience via Substack, internal newsletter, or email series

**Format:** 500–800 words per installment. One stage per piece.

**Suggested installments:**
1. "You Dismissed It" — Stages 0–1
2. "You Fought It" — Stage 2
3. "It Got Personal" — Stage 3 (Ego Shock)
4. "The Deal You Made" — Stage 3B (Bargaining)
5. "You Tested It Carefully" — Stage 4 (Trust Evaluation)
6. "You Got It — Then Overdid It" — Stages 5–6
7. "Where You Ended Up" — Stage 7

**What to keep per installment:**
- One stage, one core emotion, one recognizable scenario
- Delegation depth as flavor (a quick example at D1 vs. D3)
- A single "you might be here if…" checklist

**What to cut:**
- All academic framing
- Cross-references between stages (each installment stands alone)
- Cultural variability, external triggers (save for a wrap-up piece if needed)

**Tone:** Personal. Short, punchy, one idea per piece. Each installment should feel like a conversation.

## Option F: Visual / Infographic Format

**Title idea:** "The AI Adaptation Map"

**Target:** Conference talks, social media, internal training decks

**Format:** Single-page visual + 300–500 word companion text

**Core visual:**
- A directed graph (not a linear sequence) showing stages as nodes with transition arrows
- Delegation depth as a color gradient or size modifier on each node
- ⚡ triggers as incoming arrows from outside the graph
- Dropout exits marked clearly
- Regression loops visible

**What to keep:**
- Stage names (short labels)
- Key transitions and regression paths
- Delegation depth as a visual dimension
- Dropout types as distinct exit paths

**What to add:**
- A "you are here" self-assessment prompt
- Color coding by emotional intensity

**Companion text:** Brief description of each node. No subtypes, no cultural variability. Pure navigation aid.

## Option G: Therapist / Coach Perspective

**Title idea:** "What Your Client's Resistance to AI Is Really About"

**Target:** Executive coaches, therapists, career counselors, EAP professionals

**Format:** 2,000–3,000 words. Clinical-adjacent but accessible.

**What to keep:**
- Ego Shock (Stage 3) as the centerpiece — this is what walks into their office
- Delegation depth reframed as "what's being threatened" (skill vs. identity vs. agency)
- Bargaining (Stage 3B) — coaches will recognize this as a familiar coping pattern
- Emotional dropout — their clients are the ones who leave for emotional reasons
- Regression loop — "they seemed fine with AI last month, now they're spiraling again"

**What to add:**
- Parallels to grief models, identity transition literature, career disruption psychology
- Conversational prompts a coach can use at each stage ("What specifically feels threatened?")
- Red flags for when AI anxiety masks deeper career dissatisfaction
- A note on when Stage 3 intensity warrants clinical referral vs. coaching

**What to cut:**
- Practical dropout (not their domain)
- External trigger mechanics
- Cultural variability
- Stage subtypes beyond the emotional core

**Tone:** Reliable + Personal. Warm but precise. Clinically informed without being clinical.

## Option H: Developer / Technical Audience

**Title idea:** "You Mass-Deleted Your Side Projects and Other Signs AI Broke Your Brain"

**Target:** Software engineers, data scientists, technical creators

**Format:** 1,500–2,500 words. Conversational, self-aware, slightly irreverent.

**What to keep:**
- Ego Shock at D3/D4 — "it wrote in 30 seconds what took me a week" is a universal dev experience
- Bargaining — "I use Copilot for boilerplate but I write the architecture myself"
- Overcompensation (6A and 6C) — vibe coding, automating everything, prompt-engineering rabbit holes
- Anxiety-Driven Dependence (6B) — "I can't code without autocomplete anymore"
- Regression triggered by model releases

**What to add:**
- Dev-specific examples at each stage (pair programming with AI, code review dynamics, "is my PR actually mine?")
- The imposter syndrome amplification angle — AI doesn't create it, it accelerates existing doubt
- The skill atrophy question — when does tool dependence become a real problem vs. an irrational fear?

**What to cut:**
- Academic framing entirely
- Cultural variability
- Change management angle
- Most of Stage 7 subtypes (devs want to know "am I normal?" not "here are five end states")

**Tone:** Personal + Driven. Write like a senior dev talking to peers. Self-deprecating humor is fine. No corporate voice.

---

## Option I: Education / Faculty Audience

**Title idea:** "Your Students Use AI. You Hate It. Here's What's Actually Happening."

**Target:** University professors, K-12 teachers, academic administrators, instructional designers

**Format:** 2,000–3,000 words. Direct, empathetic, structured.

**What to keep:**
- Dismissal (Stage 1) — "it's just plagiarism machines" is the dominant faculty take
- Defensive Resistance (Stage 2) — AI detection tools, honor code rewrites, banning AI
- Ego Shock (Stage 3) at D3 — "if a student can generate a passable essay in 10 seconds, what am I teaching?"
- Bargaining — "they can use AI for research but not for writing"
- Trust Evaluation — faculty who test AI on their own assignments and discover it passes
- Social context — the department where one professor uses AI openly changes the dynamic for everyone

**What to add:**
- The dual identity threat: AI threatens both "teacher as knowledge authority" and "assessment as proof of learning"
- The student-faculty gap: students are often at Stage 5+ while faculty are at Stage 2
- Practical guidance: assignment redesign, AI-inclusive pedagogy, what "academic integrity" means now
- The accreditation and institutional pressure dimension

**What to cut:**
- Delegation depth axis (reframe as "what are students delegating?")
- Stage 6/7 subtypes
- Organizational change management framing

**Tone:** Reliable + Personal. Speak to faculty as fellow educators, not as people who need to be dragged forward. Acknowledge that their concerns are legitimate before reframing.

## Option J: Creative Professional Audience

**Title idea:** "AI Can Make Art Now. So What Are You?"

**Target:** Designers, illustrators, writers, musicians, photographers, filmmakers

**Format:** 2,000–3,000 words. Lyrical, honest, emotionally direct.

**What to keep:**
- Ego Shock (Stage 3) at D3 — this is ground zero for creatives. The identity threat is existential, not just professional.
- Bargaining — "I use AI for mood boards but never for final work"
- The delegation-depth axis reframed as creative territory: reference gathering (D1) → drafting (D2) → ideation and execution (D3) → co-creation (D4)
- Influence/Evangelism (7F) — the creatives who survived ego shock and now teach others
- Emotional dropout — creatives who quit not because the tool was bad but because using it felt like betrayal

**What to add:**
- The authenticity question front and center: "Is it still mine if AI helped?"
- The economic dimension: clients demanding AI-speed turnarounds at lower rates
- The portfolio crisis: "Do I show AI-assisted work? Do I disclose?"
- The craft-vs-output tension: the process is the identity, not just the result
- Examples from specific creative disciplines (a designer's Stage 3 is different from a novelist's)

**What to cut:**
- Trust Evaluation (Stage 4) — creatives rarely take the rational-testing path; the journey is emotional
- Efficiency Obsession (6C) — not the primary driver here
- Organizational/management framing entirely

**Tone:** Personal. First-person-compatible. Write like someone who's been through it, not someone observing from outside. Vulnerability is the access point.

---

## Option K: Executive / C-Suite Briefing

**Title idea:** "Why Your AI Rollout Stalled — A Psychological Explanation"

**Target:** CEOs, CIOs, VPs, board members

**Format:** 800–1,200 words. Executive summary style. Dense, no filler.

**What to keep:**
- The core insight in one paragraph: people resist AI for identity reasons, not just skill gaps
- Delegation depth as a risk axis: D1 adoption is easy; D3/D4 adoption triggers resistance
- Emotional vs. practical dropout — two problems, two budgets, two solutions
- Bargaining as the most common stall point in enterprise rollouts
- Regression triggered by model releases — "your adoption metrics will dip every time OpenAI ships"

**What to add:**
- A one-page diagnostic framework: "where is your organization on this map?"
- ROI framing: the cost of ignoring psychological resistance (shadow AI, attrition, compliance theater)
- Three concrete executive actions (not ten — three)
- A timeline expectation: this takes months per stage, not weeks

**What to cut:**
- All stage subtypes
- Cultural variability
- Overcompensation details
- Academic framing, citations, theory names
- Everything that takes more than 5 minutes to read

**Tone:** Driven + Reliable. No warmth needed — respect their time. Lead with the business problem, explain the psychology in service of the solution, close with actions.

---

## Recommendation

The options cluster into three effort tiers:

**Fast to produce (1–2 days):** Option A (LinkedIn), Option C (blog), Option K (exec briefing). These force sharp editorial cuts and produce testable content quickly.

**Medium effort (3–5 days):** Option D (change management), Option G (coach/therapist), Option H (developer), Option I (education), Option J (creative). Each requires audience-specific examples and reframing but can reuse the article's structure.

**Slow / high effort:** Option B (academic — needs literature, matrix, propositions), Option E (newsletter series — needs 5–7 standalone pieces), Option F (visual — needs design work).

Start with the audience you can reach and get feedback from. Use that feedback to decide what to expand next.
