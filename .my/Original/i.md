# Multi-Directional Comparison: What Each Model Captures That Yours Doesn't

The original comparison in d.md asked: *where do other models fall short?* This document asks the harder question: **where does your model fall short — and what can it learn from theirs?**

Each section goes both ways.

---

## 1. Concentrix (Exposure → Trust → Experimentation → Integration)

### What your model captures that theirs doesn't:
- Emotional turbulence (fear, anger, depression)
- Identity threat and ego collapse
- Addiction / overuse as a distinct phase
- The possibility of negative outcomes (burnout, withdrawal)

### What their model captures that yours doesn't:
- **Trust as a discrete phase.** Your model jumps from Ignorance to Fear/Anger without acknowledging that many people go through a genuine trust-evaluation step — not out of ego defense, but out of reasonable caution. Not all hesitation is emotional resistance; some of it is rational risk assessment.
- **Exposure as a precondition.** Your Phase 1 (Ignorance) assumes people are aware of AI and actively dismissing it. Concentrix recognizes that many people haven't even been meaningfully exposed yet — they're not dismissing AI, they simply haven't encountered it in a way that matters.
- **Organizational context.** Your model treats adoption as a solo psychological journey. Concentrix acknowledges that leadership, team culture, and institutional support shape how people move through phases. Depression in your model might be mitigated — or amplified — by workplace dynamics your model doesn't address.

### What your model could absorb:
Add a "Trust Evaluation" substage between Ignorance and Fear — a rational assessment window before ego defenses activate. Acknowledge that not everyone who hesitates is afraid.

## 2. BCG (Information Assistance → Task Assistance → Delegation → Semiautonomous Collaboration)

### What your model captures that theirs doesn't:
- Any emotional dimension at all
- Identity crisis and self-worth questioning
- The reality that people don't adopt AI in neat capability tiers
- Regression, overuse, and burnout

### What their model captures that yours doesn't:
- **Granularity of use patterns.** BCG distinguishes between using AI to *find information*, *complete tasks*, *delegate work*, and *collaborate autonomously*. Your model treats all AI use as one undifferentiated thing. But a person using AI to summarize emails is psychologically different from someone delegating creative decisions to it. The identity threat scales with the depth of delegation.
- **Capability progression as a driver.** In your model, the shift from Fear to Understanding is emotional. In BCG's model, it's functional — people gain confidence by gradually increasing what they hand to AI. Your model doesn't account for the fact that *incremental capability exposure* may prevent ego shock entirely.
- **The organizational layer.** BCG frames adoption as happening within teams, workflows, and business processes. Your model is purely individual. But most people encounter AI at work, mediated by managers, policies, and peer behavior. Those factors shape which of your phases people hit and how hard.

### What your model could absorb:
Layer a "depth of delegation" axis underneath your emotional phases. Someone in Phase 4 (Understanding) using AI for information lookup is in a very different psychological position than someone in Phase 4 who's delegating core creative work.

## 3. Kübler-Ross / Cyferd (Shock → Anger → Bargaining → Depression → Acceptance → Experimentation → Decision → Integration)

### What your model captures that theirs doesn't:
- Addiction / overuse (they stop at integration and assume stability)
- Identity-level depression (they treat it as transient adjustment grief)
- The possibility that integration fails (burnout, withdrawal)
- Emotional specificity — your phases are observed from real AI use, not borrowed from a generic grief template

### What their model captures that yours doesn't:
- **Bargaining.** This is a real and common phase you skip entirely. People bargain with AI constantly: "I'll use it for drafts but not final work." "I'll use it for code but not for writing." "I'll let it help but I'll always review everything." This negotiation phase — where people try to use AI *without* fully confronting what it means — sits between your Fear/Anger and Depression phases. It's a defense mechanism that delays ego shock.
- **Decision as a conscious step.** Cyferd includes an explicit "Decision" phase — the moment someone *chooses* to adopt. Your model implies this happens passively during Understanding, but for many people it's a deliberate, conscious commitment. Naming it acknowledges the agency involved.
- **Shock as distinct from Ignorance.** Your Phase 1 is active dismissal ("It won't work"). Cyferd's Phase 1 is passive shock ("Wait, this exists?"). These are different psychological states. Some people don't dismiss AI — they're genuinely blindsided by it.

### What your model could absorb:
Insert a **Bargaining** phase between Fear/Anger and Depression. This is where people try to maintain their identity by setting artificial boundaries on AI use — and it's a phase many people get stuck in for months or years. Also consider splitting Ignorance into passive (unawareness) and active (dismissal).

## 4. FourWeekMBA (Market Evolution / Social Impact / Psychology — Macro Level)

### What your model captures that theirs doesn't:
- Individual emotional experience
- Personal identity threat
- Phase-by-phase psychological progression
- Anything at the human level

### What their model captures that yours doesn't:
- **Societal-level feedback loops.** Your model treats each person as an isolated psychological unit. But individual AI adoption is shaped by societal narratives: media coverage, public debates, viral AI failures, job displacement stories, regulatory actions. A person's Phase 2 (Fear/Anger) may be triggered not by their own experience but by a news cycle. Your model doesn't account for external psychological triggers.
- **Market dynamics as psychological drivers.** When a new model launches (GPT-5, Claude 4), it doesn't just add features — it resets people's psychological state. Someone in Phase 4 (Understanding) can be thrown back to Phase 2 (Fear) by a capability jump. FourWeekMBA's macro lens captures these reset events; your model's linearity doesn't.
- **Network effects on adoption psychology.** How your peers use AI shapes how you feel about it. If your entire team adopts AI, your Phase 1 (Ignorance) collapses under social pressure. If no one around you uses it, your Phase 5 (Addiction) feels isolating. Your model is individual; real adoption is social.

### What your model could absorb:
Add **external trigger events** as a mechanism that can push people forward or backward through phases. Model releases, workplace mandates, peer adoption, and media narratives all act as phase accelerators or phase regressors.

## 5. Plum (Try → Persist → Normalize → Influence)

### What your model captures that theirs doesn't:
- Emotional reality
- The possibility of failure, regression, or burnout
- Identity crisis
- Addiction / overcompensation
- Any acknowledgment that adoption is psychologically difficult

### What their model captures that yours doesn't:
- **Influence as an end state.** Plum's final phase is where adopters become evangelists — they don't just use AI, they shape how others use it. Your model's end states (Maturity, Identity Expansion, Burnout) are all self-focused. But many power users move into a phase where they teach, advocate, mentor, and influence organizational adoption. This is a meaningful end state your model misses.
- **Persistence as a distinct skill.** Plum names "Persist" as a phase — the period where initial enthusiasm fades and people have to push through friction. Your model captures the *emotional* friction (Fear, Depression) but not the *practical* friction: bad outputs, workflow integration problems, tool limitations, prompt engineering frustration. Some people leave not because of ego threat but because the tool is genuinely annoying to use.
- **Normalization as a social process.** Plum treats "Normalize" as something that happens between people, not just within one person. Your Phase 4 (Understanding) is internal cognitive reframing. But normalization also requires social validation — seeing peers use AI, hearing others talk about it casually, watching it become unremarkable. Your model doesn't capture this social dimension.

### What your model could absorb:
Add **Influence/Evangelism** as a possible Phase 6 end state. Also consider that practical friction (not just emotional resistance) drives dropout — some people leave at Phase 2 not because of fear but because the tool failed them three times in a row.

## Synthesis: What Your Model Is Missing

Drawing from all five comparisons and the seven critiques in g.md, here are the structural gaps:

| Gap | Source | Impact |
|-----|--------|--------|
| **No trust-evaluation phase** | Concentrix | Conflates rational caution with emotional resistance |
| **No bargaining phase** | Cyferd/Kübler-Ross | Misses the "I'll use it but only for X" negotiation stage |
| **No depth-of-delegation axis** | BCG | Treats all AI use as psychologically equivalent |
| **No external triggers** | FourWeekMBA | Model releases, media, peer pressure can reset phases |
| **No social dimension** | Plum, FourWeekMBA | Adoption is treated as purely individual |
| **No practical friction** | Plum | Not all resistance is emotional — sometimes the tool just fails |
| **No influence/evangelism end state** | Plum | Power users who teach others have a distinct trajectory |
| **Linear, not cyclical** | g.md critique #4 | People regress, loop, and spiral — the model should show this |
| **No population scoping** | g.md critique #1, #2 | Model fits identity-threatened knowledge workers, not everyone |
| **Narrow addiction definition** | g.md critique #3 | Overreliance has multiple psychological drivers |
| **No cultural variability** | g.md critique #6 | Western individualistic framing assumed |
| **Underdeveloped Phase 6** | g.md critique #7 | Multiple stable end states needed |

## What Your Model Has That None of Them Do

Despite these gaps, your model remains unique in ways that matter:

- **Ego shock / identity collapse** — no other model names this
- **Addiction as a phase** — every other model assumes stable integration
- **Negative end states** — burnout and withdrawal are real but unacknowledged elsewhere
- **Emotional honesty** — your model describes what people *actually feel*, not what organizations wish they'd feel
- **Observed from frontier AI use** — not borrowed from generic change management theory

The path forward isn't to abandon what makes your model distinctive. It's to absorb the structural insights from competing frameworks — trust evaluation, bargaining, social dynamics, external triggers, practical friction — without losing the emotional core that makes it real.
