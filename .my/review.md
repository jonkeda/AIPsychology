# Review of Original Documents

## Overview

The seven documents in `Original/` trace the iterative development of a **psychological model of AI adoption** through a conversational dialogue. The sequence moves from initial exploration → model definition → research validation → competitive analysis → blog post → TLDR → self-critique.

---

## Document-by-Document Review

### [a.md](Original/a.md) — Initial Exploration (GPT-4 Response)

**Content:** A generic 6-phase model of AI interaction (Curiosity → Overestimation → Disillusionment → Adjustment → Integration → Reflection), plus references to established concepts (ELIZA effect, automation bias, algorithm aversion).

**Assessment:** This is the "wrong answer" that catalyzes the real model. It reads like a textbook summary — rational, clean, and emotionally flat. It serves as a contrast point: it describes how researchers *think* people adopt AI, not how they actually *feel* doing it. The document is useful as a foil but has no original contribution on its own.

---

### [b.md](Original/b.md) — The Core Model Emerges

**Content:** The author rejects the GPT-4 model and proposes their own 5+1 phase sequence based on direct observation:

1. Ignorance / Dismissal
2. Fear / Anger / Defensive Rejection
3. Depression / Ego Shock
4. Understanding / Integration of Self
5. Addiction / Over-reliance
6. Unknown (speculated as Maturity, Identity Expansion, Philosophical Integration, or Burnout)

**Assessment:** This is the most important document in the set — the original insight. The model's strength is its emotional honesty: it captures ego threat, identity crisis, and addictive overuse, none of which appear in standard adoption frameworks. The reframing by the AI assistant adds psychological depth (pre-contemplation, cognitive reframing, overcompensation) without overwriting the author's voice. The four candidate Phase 6 options are well-differentiated.

**Weakness:** The model is presented as a universal sequence but is likely specific to knowledge workers and creative professionals with strong skill-identity attachment.

---

### [c.md](Original/c.md) — Research Validation

**Content:** Maps each phase to published research (2025 studies from Frontiers, MDPI, Nature, Springer, PMC). Provides a summary table showing all five phases are independently supported by evidence.

**Assessment:** Strong supporting document. The citations are specific and the mapping is clear. The key finding — that no existing paper unifies these phases but each is independently validated — is compelling for establishing originality. The table at the end is effective.

**Weakness:** The citations are presented as links without full bibliographic details (authors, titles, DOIs). For any formal publication, these would need to be properly referenced and verified. Some links may be to opinion pieces rather than peer-reviewed research.

---

### [d.md](Original/d.md) — Competitive Analysis

**Content:** Compares the author's model against five existing frameworks:

1. Concentrix (Exposure → Trust → Experimentation → Integration)
2. BCG (Information → Task → Delegation → Semiautonomous)
3. Kübler-Ross applied to AI / Cyferd (8-stage grief mapping)
4. FourWeekMBA (macro-level societal model)
5. Plum (Try → Persist → Normalize → Influence)

**Assessment:** This is the document that establishes the model's differentiation. The key insight is well-articulated: existing models are rational/functional/organizational, while this model is emotional/identity-focused/personal. The Cyferd grief-mapping model is identified as the closest competitor, but it notably lacks the addiction phase and treats depression as transient rather than existential.

**Weakness:** The comparison is one-directional — it only highlights where others fall short. A stronger analysis would also acknowledge what those models capture that this one doesn't (e.g., organizational dynamics, trust calibration, team-level adoption).

---

### [e.md](Original/e.md) — Blog Post Draft

**Content:** A polished, narrative-style blog post presenting the model for a general audience.

**Assessment:** Well-written and engaging. The short-sentence, punchy style works for the medium. The framing of AI as "a mirror" is effective. Each phase gets a clear emotional hook. The tone balances insight with accessibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The blog post doesn't cite research (c.md's evidence is dropped entirely). Adding even a few references would strengthen credibility.
- The "Unknown Next Phase" ending feels unfinished rather than provocative. Either commit to a position or frame the ambiguity more deliberately.
- No mention of who this model applies to (and who it doesn't). The universal framing invites the exact critique raised in g.md.

---

### [f.md](Original/f.md) — TLDR

**Content:** A condensed summary of the model in bullet-point format.

**Assessment:** Clean and effective. The one-liner — "It's not a tech adoption curve — it's an identity transformation curve" — is the single best summary of the entire project. This should be the tagline.

**Weakness:** Minimal — it does what a TLDR should do.

---

### [g.md](Original/g.md) — Self-Critique

**Content:** Seven critiques of the model:
1. Overgeneralizes emotional intensity
2. Centered on identity-threatened knowledge workers
3. "Addiction" phase is too narrow
4. Linear model vs. cyclical reality
5. Doesn't account for emotionally detached users
6. No cultural variability
7. Underdeveloped final phase

**Assessment:** This is the second most important document after b.md. The critiques are fair, specific, and actionable. Points #2 (target population) and #4 (cyclical vs. linear) are the most significant — addressing them would materially improve the model. The suggestion to reframe as a "cycle with spirals" rather than a linear sequence is particularly strong.

**Weakness:** The critique doesn't prioritize. All seven points are presented equally, but some (target population, linearity) are structural issues while others (cultural differences, narrow addiction definition) are refinements. A tiered assessment would help guide revision.

## Cross-Cutting Observations

### Strengths of the Collection
- **Clear narrative arc**: The documents tell a coherent story from discovery through validation to self-critique.
- **Original insight**: The core model (b.md) identifies emotional phases that mainstream frameworks ignore, particularly ego shock and addiction.
- **Research grounding**: c.md provides evidence that each phase is independently supported.
- **Honest self-assessment**: g.md doesn't pull punches.

### Gaps and Recommendations

1. **Target audience is undefined.** The model clearly applies best to knowledge workers whose identity is tied to skills AI can replicate. This should be stated explicitly rather than implied.

2. **Phase 6 needs resolution.** It appears in b.md as speculation, in e.md as an open question, and in g.md as a noted weakness. Four documents later, it's still undefined. Either commit to a position (e.g., "Phase 6 is Maturity, with Burnout as a failure mode") or formally designate it as an open research question.

3. **The model needs a name.** "The AI Identity Cycle" is suggested in d.md but never adopted. A named model is easier to reference, cite, and remember.

4. **Linear vs. cyclical structure is unresolved.** g.md correctly identifies that real behavior loops back. The model should be revised to show regression paths (e.g., a new model release can send someone from Understanding back to Fear).

5. **No empirical methodology.** The model is based on observation ("watching hundreds of people"), but there's no description of how those observations were collected, categorized, or validated. For publication, even an informal methodology section would help.

6. **Citations need verification.** The research links in c.md should be checked for accessibility, relevance, and peer-review status.

7. **Missing: practical applications.** The blog post asks "why this matters" but doesn't offer concrete guidance (e.g., how a manager should support someone in Phase 3, or how an AI product should onboard users in Phase 1).
