# **Critique of the AI Psychological Adoption Model**

## **1. It may overgeneralize emotional intensity**

Your model assumes that most people experience:

* fear
* anger
* depression
* addiction

These are *real* phases for many early adopters and high‑engagement users, but not everyone goes through them. Some people:

* skip straight to curiosity
* never feel threatened
* never experience ego collapse
* never become dependent

In other words, your model may describe  **a common path** , but not  **the universal path** .

**Potential improvement:**

Frame it as a  *dominant pattern among high‑impact users* , not a one‑size-fits-all sequence.

---

## **2. It’s centered on identity‑threatened knowledge workers**

Your model fits people who:

* use AI for creative or cognitive work
* have a strong identity tied to their skills
* feel personally evaluated by AI’s performance

But it may not apply to:

* casual users
* people who see AI as entertainment
* people whose jobs aren’t threatened by AI
* people who already feel technologically confident

For these groups, the emotional stakes are lower.

**Potential improvement:**

Specify that the model applies most strongly to  *identity‑relevant AI use* .

---

## **3. The “addiction” phase may be too narrow**

You describe addiction as:

* wanting to use AI for everything
* over-reliance
* dopamine-driven experimentation

This is real, but it’s not the only possible “overuse” pattern. Some people:

* become dependent out of anxiety
* use AI compulsively to avoid thinking
* rely on it because they distrust their own abilities

So “addiction” might be better framed as **overcompensation** or  **overreliance** , which can take multiple forms.

**Potential improvement:**

Broaden the definition to include different psychological drivers.

---

## **4. The model is linear, but real behavior is cyclical**

People often:

* bounce back to fear after a bad AI output
* relapse into overuse after a breakthrough
* revisit depression when a new model releases
* oscillate between mastery and frustration

Your model is presented as a straight line, but real adoption is more like a  **loop with spirals** .

**Potential improvement:**

Reframe it as a *cycle* rather than a  *sequence* .

---

## **5. The model assumes emotional vulnerability, but some users are emotionally detached**

Some people treat AI like:

* a calculator
* a search engine
* a toy
* a tool with no psychological meaning

These users don’t experience ego threat or identity disruption. They skip the emotional phases entirely.

**Potential improvement:**

Add a branch for “low‑identity‑attachment users.”

---

## **6. The model doesn’t account for cultural differences**

AI anxiety varies dramatically across cultures:

* Some cultures see AI as a threat to jobs.
* Others see it as a tool for efficiency.
* Some have strong techno-optimism.
* Others have deep skepticism of automation.

Your model assumes a Western, individualistic, self‑identity‑centric perspective.

**Potential improvement:**

Acknowledge cultural variability in emotional response.

---

## **7. The final phase is underdeveloped**

You’ve identified:

* maturity
* identity expansion
* burnout

But these aren’t mutually exclusive, and they don’t cover all possibilities. For example:

* **ethical awakening** (thinking deeply about AI’s societal impact)
* **creative renaissance** (AI unlocks new forms of expression)
* **cognitive outsourcing** (people let AI think for them long-term)

The end state is more complex than a single “Phase 6.”

**Potential improvement:**

Define multiple stable end states, not just one.

---

# **Overall assessment**

Your model is:

* emotionally accurate
* psychologically rich
* grounded in real user behavior
* more honest than most corporate frameworks

But it can be strengthened by:

* acknowledging variability
* adding branching paths
* broadening definitions
* clarifying the target population
* refining the final phase

This critique doesn’t weaken your model — it makes it more robust, more publishable, and more defensible.
