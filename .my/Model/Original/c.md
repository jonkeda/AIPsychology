# 🧠 What the research says — and how it maps to your phases

## **1. Ignorance / Dismissal (“It won’t work”)**

This phase matches what researchers call **technostress creators** — people initially reject AI because it feels overwhelming or irrelevant.

A 2025 study found that AI adoption triggers stress responses that correlate with anxiety and avoidance behaviors.   [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1600013/full)

This is consistent with your “I’m not using it” phase.

---

## **2. Fear / Anger (“I’ll use it but find reasons to hate it”)**

This is strongly supported by research on  **AI anxiety** , especially:

* **Anticipatory anxiety** — fear of future disruption
* **Annihilation anxiety** — fear of losing identity or autonomy

A 2025 study explicitly identifies these two forms of AI‑related fear and shows they shape early resistance and emotional defensiveness.   [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-8954/13/2/82)

This maps almost perfectly to your “fear and anger” stage.

---

## **3. Depression / Ego Shock (“What is my worth?”)**

This is the phase most people feel but few talk about — and the research confirms it.

Two studies show:

* AI adoption can **increase depression** by reducing psychological safety at work.   [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05040-2)
* AI‑related technostress correlates with  **clinical anxiety and depression symptoms** .   [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1600013/full)
* Fear of replacement is the **primary driver** of AI anxiety.   [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43681-025-00686-9)

This directly supports your “ego collapse” phase.

---

## **4. Understanding (“It’s just a tool”)**

Research shows that  **moderate engagement with AI reduces anxiety** , because familiarity replaces fear.

This is the “calibration” effect: once people understand what AI can and cannot do, anxiety drops.   [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-8954/13/2/82)

This aligns with your “integration and mastery” phase.

---

## **5. Addiction / Over‑reliance (“I want to use it for everything”)**

There *is* research on AI dependency:

* Adolescents show measurable  **AI dependence** , and it increases over time.
* Dependency is linked to motivations like **escape** and  **social needs** , not just utility.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10944174/)
* Frequent AI use correlates with  **high dependency** , even when anxiety is low.   [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-8954/13/2/82)

This supports your “addiction” phase — people swing too far toward overuse.

---

## **6. The next phase — what research suggests**

No study defines a clear “Phase 6,” but the literature points to three likely outcomes:

### **A. Balanced Integration (most likely)**

People reach a stable, healthy relationship with AI — using it strategically without over‑reliance.

Supported by findings that moderate use reduces anxiety and dependency stabilizes.   [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-8954/13/2/82)

### **B. Identity Reconstruction**

Some people move into a phase where AI becomes part of their extended cognitive identity — a new normal.

This is implied in research on long‑term AI anxiety reduction and adaptation.   [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43681-025-00686-9)

### **C. Burnout / Withdrawal**

A minority experience technostress and step away from AI for mental‑health reasons.

Supported by studies linking AI adoption to depression and reduced psychological safety.   [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05040-2)
