# Multi-Directional Comparison: Version 2 vs. Competing Models

Version 2 (h.md) already absorbed several critiques from g.md — branching paths, cyclical structure, user-type differentiation, overcompensation subtypes, and multiple end states. This comparison asks: **after those improvements, what do the competing models still capture that Version 2 doesn't — and vice versa?**

---

## 1. Concentrix (Exposure → Trust → Experimentation → Integration)

### What V2 captures that theirs doesn't:
- Emotional turbulence across multiple phases (Fear, Ego Shock, Overcompensation)
- Identity threat as a central mechanism
- Negative end states (Burnout, Regression Loop)
- User-type branching (low-identity vs. high-identity paths)

### What theirs captures that V2 still doesn't:
- **Trust as a standalone phase.** V2 added Stage 0 (Pre-Exposure) and split Dismissal into low/high-identity paths — but neither path includes a trust-evaluation step. Concentrix puts Trust between Exposure and Experimentation: "Is this safe? Is this reliable? Can I depend on it?" This isn't ego defense or fear — it's rational due diligence. V2's high-identity path goes Dismissal → Defensive Resistance, skipping the possibility that someone cautiously evaluates AI *without* emotional resistance.
- **Experimentation as a named phase.** V2 moves from Cognitive Reframing (Stage 4) directly into Overcompensation (Stage 5). But there's a gap: the period where someone deliberately tests AI on low-stakes tasks before committing. Concentrix names this; V2 buries it inside Understanding.

### Remaining gap:
V2 still lacks a **rational assessment** phase. Not all hesitation is identity-driven. Some people evaluate carefully, find AI useful, and adopt without ever hitting Stages 2 or 3.

---

## 2. BCG (Information Assistance → Task Assistance → Delegation → Semiautonomous Collaboration)

### What V2 captures that theirs doesn't:
- Any psychological dimension
- Identity disruption and ego shock
- Overreliance and its subtypes
- Regression and non-linear progression
- The reality that emotional state shapes capability adoption

### What theirs captures that V2 still doesn't:
- **Depth of delegation as a psychological variable.** V2 improved the addiction phase by splitting it into Enthusiastic Overuse (5A), Anxiety-Driven Dependence (5B), and Efficiency Obsession (5C). But these are emotional subtypes — they don't distinguish *what* is being delegated. Using AI to proofread an email is psychologically trivial. Delegating creative direction is an identity event. BCG's four-tier capability model (information → task → delegation → semiautonomous) maps this gradient. V2 treats all AI use as having equal psychological weight.
- **Functional progression within each emotional phase.** Someone in V2's Stage 4 (Understanding) who uses AI only for information lookup is in a fundamentally different position than someone in Stage 4 who's delegating strategy. BCG captures this; V2 doesn't.

### Remaining gap:
V2 needs a **delegation depth axis** that runs alongside the emotional phases. The psychological intensity of each stage depends on how much cognitive authority is being handed to AI.

---

## 3. Kübler-Ross / Cyferd (Shock → Anger → Bargaining → Depression → Acceptance → Experimentation → Decision → Integration)

### What V2 captures that theirs doesn't:
- Overcompensation / addiction (Cyferd stops at Integration)
- Multiple end states including burnout and regression
- Identity-level ego shock (not just transient grief)
- User-type branching (low-identity users skip emotional phases)
- Cyclical structure with regression loops

### What theirs captures that V2 still doesn't:
- **Bargaining.** This is the single biggest phase V2 still lacks. Cyferd includes it; V2 doesn't. Bargaining is everywhere in real AI adoption: "I'll use it for first drafts but never for final copy." "I'll let it write code but I'll review every line." "It can do research but I make the decisions." This negotiation — where people try to adopt AI while preserving their identity — sits between V2's Defensive Resistance (Stage 2) and Ego Shock (Stage 3). It's a coping mechanism that *delays* identity disruption. Many people stay in bargaining for months or years without ever hitting ego shock.
- **Decision as a conscious moment.** Cyferd names the point where someone *chooses* to adopt. V2's transition from Understanding to Overcompensation is presented as a drift, not a choice. But for many people, there's a specific moment — "I'm going to use this seriously now" — that deserves recognition. It's an act of agency, not a passive slide.

### Remaining gap:
**Bargaining** is a critical missing phase. It explains why many people get stuck in partial adoption — they've moved past fear but haven't confronted the identity implications. V2's branching improved the model, but adding a Bargaining stage between Resistance and Ego Shock would capture a large population of users who are neither resistant nor fully engaged.

## 4. FourWeekMBA (Market Evolution / Social Impact / Psychology — Macro Level)

### What V2 captures that theirs doesn't:
- Individual-level emotional progression
- Personal identity threat and ego shock
- Specific phases with behavioral markers
- The overcompensation/addiction dynamic
- Anything actionable at the person level

### What theirs captures that V2 still doesn't:
- **External triggers that reset phases.** V2 added the Regression Loop (6E) — people can cycle back to fear or overuse. But it frames regression as internally triggered ("a bad output"). FourWeekMBA's macro view reveals that *external* events are the primary regression triggers: a new model release makes yesterday's Understanding feel obsolete; a viral AI failure reignites Fear; a workplace mandate forces people out of Dismissal. V2's regression loop needs to account for these external shocks.
- **Social contagion effects.** V2 improved by distinguishing low-identity and high-identity users, but it still treats each person as psychologically independent. In reality, peer adoption accelerates phase transitions: watching a colleague use AI confidently can pull someone from Stage 2 to Stage 4 in days. Conversely, hearing a respected peer dismiss AI can reinforce Stage 1. V2 has no mechanism for social influence.
- **Narrative environments.** Media coverage, public discourse, and cultural mood shape which phase people enter first and how intensely they experience it. Someone encountering AI during a wave of "AI will replace you" headlines starts at a different emotional baseline than someone encountering it during "AI is a creative partner" framing.

### Remaining gap:
V2 is still a **closed system** — it models the individual in isolation. Adding **external trigger events** (model releases, workplace mandates, cultural narratives, peer behavior) as phase accelerators and regressors would make the cycle more realistic.

---

## 5. Plum (Try → Persist → Normalize → Influence)

### What V2 captures that theirs doesn't:
- Emotional depth and psychological realism
- Identity threat, ego shock, and overcompensation
- Negative outcomes (burnout, regression)
- The cyclical nature of adoption
- Multiple overreliance subtypes

### What theirs captures that V2 still doesn't:
- **Influence / Evangelism as an end state.** V2 defined five end states: Maturity (6A), Identity Expansion (6B), Ethical Integration (6C), Burnout (6D), and Regression (6E). None of them capture the person who becomes an AI advocate — someone who teaches others, shapes organizational adoption, writes about it, mentors beginners. This is a common and distinct trajectory, especially among people who've passed through ego shock and come out the other side. It's missing from V2.
- **Practical friction as a dropout driver.** V2 models dropout as emotionally driven (burnout from overuse, regression from ego shock). But Plum's "Persist" phase acknowledges that many people leave for *practical* reasons: bad outputs, workflow friction, tool instability, prompt engineering fatigue. These aren't identity crises — they're usability failures. V2 doesn't distinguish between emotional dropout and practical dropout.
- **Normalization as a social process.** V2's Stage 4 (Cognitive Reframing) is internal: "I now understand what AI is." Plum's Normalize is external: "Everyone around me uses this, and it's unremarkable." These are different. Someone can cognitively understand AI but still feel like an outlier for using it. Normalization requires social validation that V2 doesn't model.

### Remaining gap:
V2 should add **Influence/Evangelism** as a sixth end state (6F). It should also distinguish between emotional and practical friction as separate dropout mechanisms, and acknowledge that normalization has a social component beyond individual reframing.

## Synthesis: What V2 Already Resolved vs. What Remains

### Gaps from g.md that V2 resolved:
| Critique | Status in V2 |
|----------|-------------|
| Overgeneralizes emotional intensity | ✅ Resolved — low/high-identity branching |
| Centered on identity-threatened workers | ✅ Resolved — low-identity path skips emotional phases |
| Addiction too narrow | ✅ Resolved — three overcompensation subtypes |
| Linear, not cyclical | ✅ Resolved — regression loop added |
| No emotionally detached user path | ✅ Resolved — Stage 1A branches to curiosity |
| Underdeveloped Phase 6 | ✅ Resolved — five end states defined |
| No cultural variability | ⚠️ Partially — branching helps, but cultural framing not explicit |

### Gaps that remain after V2:
| Gap | Source | Why it matters |
|-----|--------|---------------|
| **No trust-evaluation phase** | Concentrix | Rational caution is conflated with emotional resistance |
| **No bargaining phase** | Cyferd | "I'll use it only for X" is a major real-world pattern V2 misses |
| **No delegation-depth axis** | BCG | Proofreading vs. creative delegation have vastly different psychological weight |
| **No external triggers** | FourWeekMBA | Model releases, media, mandates reset phases — V2's regression loop is internally triggered only |
| **No social dimension** | Plum, FourWeekMBA | Peer adoption, cultural narratives, and normalization are absent |
| **No practical friction** | Plum | Not all dropout is emotional — sometimes the tool just doesn't work |
| **No influence/evangelism end state** | Plum | Teaching and advocating is a distinct trajectory V2 doesn't name |

---

## What V2 Still Has That No Model Matches

Even with these remaining gaps, V2 is the only model that:

- Names **ego shock / identity collapse** as a discrete, intense phase
- Includes **overcompensation subtypes** with distinct psychological drivers
- Models **negative end states** (burnout, regression) as real outcomes, not failures
- Provides **branching paths** based on identity attachment
- Treats AI adoption as a **cyclical identity transformation**, not a linear capability ramp

The remaining gaps are structural additions (trust, bargaining, social dynamics, external triggers) that would make V2 more complete without diluting what makes it unique. The emotional core — the thing no other model captures — stays intact.
