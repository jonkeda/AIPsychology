# **The AI Psychological Adaptation Cycle — Version 2**

A refined, flexible model of how people psychologically adapt to AI, based on identity, emotional response, and usage patterns.

This version includes:

* branching paths
* multiple end states
* clearer definitions
* a distinction between high‑identity and low‑identity users
* a cyclical (not linear) structure

---

# **🧠 Stage 0: Pre‑Exposure (Optional)**

**“AI is irrelevant to me.”**

Some people simply haven’t encountered AI in a meaningful way yet.

No emotional reaction. No threat. No curiosity.

This stage is skipped by many, but it matters for understanding adoption.

---

# **1. Dismissal / Minimization**

**“AI won’t work. It’s overhyped.”**

A protective stance that avoids cognitive effort and emotional risk.

People underestimate AI to protect their worldview or identity.

**Two paths forward:**

* **1A. Low‑identity users** → jump straight to curiosity.
* **1B. High‑identity users** → move into emotional resistance.

---

# **2. Defensive Resistance (Fear / Anger / Rationalization)**

**“Fine, I’ll try it — but I’m going to find flaws.”**

This is an ego-defense phase.

People feel threatened, so they:

* nitpick
* criticize
* downplay strengths
* highlight weaknesses

This is not about the tool — it’s about identity protection.

**Two possible transitions:**

* **Toward collapse** (if AI performs surprisingly well)
* **Back to dismissal** (if AI performs poorly or is misused)

---

# **3. Ego Shock / Identity Disruption**

**“If AI can do this… what does that make me?”**

This is the emotional core of the model.

People may experience:

* anxiety
* grief
* loss of identity
* fear of obsolescence
* existential discomfort

This phase is common among:

* creatives
* knowledge workers
* experts
* people with strong skill‑based identities

**This is the most psychologically intense stage.**

---

# **4. Cognitive Reframing / Understanding**

**“It’s powerful, but it’s still just a tool.”**

The emotional fog clears.

People begin to:

* understand limitations
* see where human judgment matters
* learn prompting
* integrate AI into their mental model of work

This is the first *stable* phase.

---

# **5. Overcompensation / Overreliance (Addiction Variant)**

**“This is incredible — I want to use it for everything.”**

This phase has multiple subtypes:

### **5A. Enthusiastic Overuse**

Dopamine-driven experimentation.

Feels like a creative superpower.

### **5B. Anxiety‑Driven Dependence**

“I don’t trust my own abilities anymore.”

### **5C. Efficiency Obsession**

“I must automate everything.”

This phase is not inherently bad — but it is unstable.

---

# **6. Stabilization — Multiple End States**

People eventually settle into one of several long-term patterns.

## **6A. Maturity (Healthy Integration)**

**“I know when to use AI and when not to.”**

Balanced, thoughtful, sustainable.

## **6B. Identity Expansion**

**“AI extends what I can be.”**

People develop new creative or cognitive identities with AI as a partner.

## **6C. Ethical or Philosophical Integration**

**“What does AI mean for society, creativity, or humanity?”**

A reflective, meaning-making stage.

## **6D. Burnout / Withdrawal**

**“I need a break from this.”**

Overuse leads to exhaustion or disillusionment.

## **6E. Regression Loop**

A bad output or new model release can send people back to:

* fear
* overuse
* ego shock

The cycle is **not linear** — it loops.
