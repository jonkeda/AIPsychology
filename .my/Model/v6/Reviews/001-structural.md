# Structural Critique of Model V6

## TL;DR

- The S2T → S2D edge collapses what should be a clean Identity Stakes fork into a fuzzy continuum; testing overrides the initial prediction.
- S6 states (Enthusiastic, Dependent, Driven, Social) have mutual-exclusion rules that look imposed rather than emergent from their definitions.
- S7 states allow complete overlap (S7M + S7I + S7E + S7V all at once), raising whether these are separate states or dimensions of a single integrated end-state.
- S7I is defined by self-report and cannot be distinguished from S7M or S7E on observable behavior alone.
- S3X has no within-model transitions; all three "resolutions" lead outside the model, making it a labeled exit, not a state.
- PEN vs S2D distinction rests on three unobservable signals the model admits are "none reliable on a single observation"; most real cases are mixed.
- S0 conflates pre-encounter users (transitional) with permanent non-users (terminal, outside scope); distinguishing them requires predicting future exposure.
- The S3E → S5 direct edge ("rare") gives difficult cases an escape hatch and undermines S3B Bargaining as a necessary stage.

## Verdict

V6 is more honestly framed than V5 — descriptive-first stance, explicit per-domain scoping, inline epistemic markers are real gains. But the structural additions (S3X, PEN) and refinements (the S2T → S2D edge, four S7 states, S6 occupancy rules) expose underlying taxonomy problems that honesty about framing doesn't solve. The model has shifted from "overstated mechanisms" to "underspecified states," which is progress on candor but not on diagnostic clarity. Three states (PEN, S3X, S7I) rest on unobservable inputs — ethical conviction, economic non-viability, identity self-concept — that require self-report or longitudinal observation to confirm. In a descriptive model that claims to be a shared vocabulary, that's a significant gap.

## 1. The S2T → S2D edge collapses the S1 fork from clean dichotomy to fuzzy continuum — testable

At S1 Initial Encounter, Identity Stakes drives a fork: low stakes → S2T Trust Evaluation (non-defensive testing); high stakes → S2D Defensive Resistance (identity protection). That's clean and architectural.

V6 adds the S2T → S2D edge: testing can uncover identity threat that wasn't anticipated at S1. The person enters S2T thinking low or moderate stakes, then discovers during evaluation that AI touches something identity-central. They convert to S2D.

Here's the structural damage: once this conversion exists, the fork is no longer a dichotomy. It becomes a probability space. Identity Stakes becomes a *prior*, not a determinant. The actual path depends on what testing reveals, not what the person predicted. Two people with identical self-assessed Identity Stakes can diverge if their expectations differ.

This is not wrong — discovery-driven path changes are real. But it means the foundational S1 fork loses its clarity: "identity stakes determine branch" becomes "identity stakes is one weak signal; testing is the real sort."

**Fix:** Either (1) remove the S2T → S2D edge and treat identity-threat discovery as a S2T selection failure or dropout, not a conversion path. Or (2) reframe the S1 fork as probabilistic and make clear that observed S2T vs S2D split is much messier than Identity Stakes alone predicts. The observation guide should include a procedure for checking whether someone's Identity Stakes self-assessment is stable or subject to discovery-driven revision.

## 2. S6 states have mutually-exclusive rules that look imposed rather than emergent from their definitions — not yet tested

S6E (Enthusiastic Overuse), S6D (Dependent Overuse), S6R (Driven Overuse), and S6S (Social Overuse) are defined by the *why*: dopamine reinforcement, eroded self-efficacy, productivity metrics, peer conformity.

The occupancy rules:

- S6E and S6D are mutually exclusive (opposite affect: energised vs anxious).
- S6E and S6R can coexist (startup cultures).
- S6R and S6S can coexist (compliance-heavy cultures).
- S6E + S6D and S6R + S6S are unstable.

The rules are careful and observed. But someone in a high-pressure, high-identity-stakes knowledge-work context could be experiencing all four simultaneously: dopamine loop from a fresh model (S6E), eroded confidence from repeated self-efficacy threat (S6D), metrics tracking AI usage (S6R), team norm that not using AI is falling behind (S6S). All four mechanisms firing at once.

The model forbids this by fiat. "Opposite affect" keeps S6E and S6D apart, but that's an observation summary, not a mechanistic barrier. A person can be energised about their *output volume* while anxious about their *autonomy*. The affective profiles don't need to be clean.

What's really happening: the four may not be separate *states* but separate *mechanisms of overuse* that can stack. The model has classified them as discrete occupancy zones when they might be better understood as independent drivers.

**Fix:** Either (1) redesignate S6E, S6D, S6R, S6S as "overuse drivers" or "mechanisms" rather than mutually-exclusive states, and model them as independent factors. Or (2) keep them as states but run a study checking whether the mutual-exclusion rules hold in high-pressure cultures or whether they are violated. The current rules read as post-hoc cardinality constraints, not emergent facts.

## 3. The five S7 states allow complete overlap but it's unclear whether they are separate states or dimensions of a single integrated end-state — not yet tested

S7M (Maturity), S7I (Identity Expansion), S7E (Ethical Integration), S7V (Evangelism), and S7B (Burnout). The model says: "Any S7 state can coexist with any other S7 state [except S7B as a phase]."

Common combinations listed:

- S7M + S7E (reflective practitioner)
- S7I + S7V (creative leader)  
- S7M + S7V (steady mentor)
- S7I + S7E (creator wrestling with what they're creating)

Someone in all four at once: uses AI with calibrated maturity, has built new identity around expanded capability, reflects on ethics, and guides others. From outside, that looks like: integrated use. The fact that you can decompose it into four labels doesn't make it four separate states. It makes it four *aspects* of integration.

The model's own observation-guide approach for S7M vs S7I signals they are distinct "internally" but identical behaviorally: "both look like 'uses AI competently' from outside. Confirming the distinction requires self-report."

If they coexist completely and require self-report to distinguish, the question is whether they should be labeled as separate states at all. They read more like dimensions: *calibration* (S7M), *identity work* (S7I), *values work* (S7E), *generativity* (S7V).

**Fix:** Clarify whether S7M, S7I, S7E, S7V are meant to be alternate paths to integration (separate trajectories) or overlapping dimensions of integrated use. If dimensions, restructure around them. If separate trajectories, explain why the endpoints coexist so completely — either the trajectories reconverge (inevitable coexistence) or they are not truly separate.

## 4. S7I (Identity Expansion) is defined by self-report and cannot be distinguished from neighboring states on observable behavior alone — not yet tested

S7M and S7I both look identical from outside: high-delegation use, competent work, deliberate routing choices. The model: "both look like 'uses AI competently' from outside. Confirming the distinction requires self-report."

S7I's observation marker: "The person identifies themselves through capabilities that did not exist for them pre-AI." Pure identity claim. Not observable; requires asking.

A translator using AI to edit AI output, now calling themselves "AI-assisted translator" — S7M (matured into a new boundary) or S7I (expanded identity to include AI as constitutive)? Both descriptions fit. Only difference is internal: does the person think "I'm a translator who uses tools" (S7M) or "I'm a translator-with-AI, where the AI is constitutive" (S7I)?

This matters for transitions. The model claims S6E Enthusiastic Overuse often moves to S7I, and S7I leads to S7V Evangelism. But if S7I is unfalsifiable on external evidence, practitioners cannot diagnose it in real time or predict its transitions reliably.

**Fix:** Either (1) identify observable markers that distinguish S7I from S7M — for example, S7I people pursue capability expansion aggressively, seek feedback on the expanded capability specifically, describe their work differently post-expansion. Or (2) accept that S7I requires self-report and acknowledge the state is useful for *conversation* but not for *prediction*. Restructure claims around S7I accordingly.

## 5. S3X (Structural Displacement) has no within-model transitions; all three "resolutions" lead outside the model — not yet tested

The model describes three paths from S3X:

1. Occupational transition (translator → AI output editor) — enters a new S0 → S1 arc in a *different domain*.
2. Principled exit — leaves the field entirely. Outside the model.
3. Adversarial advocacy — activist for regulation, labour protection. Explicitly noted as "structurally outside the integration cycle" and not modeled.

All three lead outside. No S3X → S5 transitions, no S3X → S7 paths. S3X: one incoming edge (from S3E), three outgoing edges that all exit.

Functionally identical to dropout, except labeled by economic cause instead of psychological cost. Both are one-way terminal exits. The model gains clarity by making the structural reason explicit — that is valuable. But calling it a "state" misclassifies it. States have transitions; exits are terminal.

**Fix:** Reclassify S3X as a labeled form of dropout (economic dropout), or reframe the model to include occupational transition as a real return path that re-enters the model in a new domain. If S3X is a state, it needs at least one within-model transition that doesn't require domain change, economic rescue, or field exit.

## 6. PEN (Pre-emptive Non-Adoption) vs S2D (Defensive Resistance) rests on three unobservable signals, all admittedly unreliable in practice — not yet tested

The model lists three distinguishing signals:

1. **Cross-domain consistency** — ethical objection applies uniformly across domains.
2. **Evidence response** — does position hold if the stated concern is addressed?
3. **Emotional register** — S2D has anxiety (threat appraisal); PEN has conviction (values protection).

Then: "none reliable on a single observation" and "most real cases are mixed."

This is honest and structural problem both. If practitioners cannot reliably distinguish PEN from S2D in real time, the states may not be separately operational. Both people decline to engage, both cite principled reasons, both resist evidence. The observer has no way to know the underlying mechanism without multiple observations across time and domains.

A person can genuinely hold both: protecting identity *and* believing the ethical objections. A freelance journalist won't use AI for fact-checking. Is that PEN (genuine concern about algorithmic bias) or S2D (threatened by the implication AI fact-checking is better)? Likely both. Labeling such a case as one or the other is not just hard; it may be meaningless.

**Fix:** Run a diagnostic study on people who decline AI use citing values-based reasons. Check whether cross-domain consistency and emotional register are actually distinguishable by trained observers. If most real cases are mixed, consider merging PEN and S2D into a single "Defended Non-Adoption" state with two named mechanisms (identity-protective and values-grounded) that often co-occur.

## 7. S0 (Baseline) conflates two distinct populations into one box, creating categorical confusion — not yet tested

S0 has "two readings":

1. **Pre-encounter users** — haven't used AI yet but likely will. S0 → S1 expected.
2. **Permanent non-users** — won't use AI. S0 is terminal.

These are not two readings of the same thing; they are two *different categories*. Pre-encounter users are in the system; permanent non-users are outside it.

The model tries to distinguish them by checking "whether the person has formed a stance." S2D requires active defended stance; permanent non-users require no stance. But this requires the practitioner to *predict future AI exposure*, which is not observable from current data.

Practically: a 58-year-old accountant in a domain where AI is not yet embedded may appear to be a permanent non-user today. In two years, their firm adopts AI tools and they become pre-encounter user entering S1. Current observation cannot tell the difference.

Putting both in S0 obscures the distinction. Permanent non-users should not be in the model (they're outside scope). Pre-encounter users should be in S0, with explicit acknowledgment that distinguishing pre-encounter from permanently-outside-scope requires knowledge of future context.

**Fix:** Either split S0 into **S0P (Pre-Encounter)** and **S0N (Permanent Non-User, Out of Scope)**, or remove permanent non-users from the model entirely. Make S0 applicability conditional on "expected future exposure" and acknowledge this condition cannot always be assessed from current data. If permanent non-users remain, provide an explicit decision tree for practitioners that doesn't require predicting future exposure.

## 8. The S3E → S5 direct edge (marked "rare") gives difficult cases an escape hatch and undercuts the necessity of S3B Bargaining — testable

The model includes a direct S3E → S5 edge: people confronting identity disruption fully, without negotiating scope, and integrating directly. Marked "rare."

The note: "Most people who appear to take this path are actually doing accelerated S3B Bargaining and revisiting it later." But the edge exists, and "distinguishing the direct path from a fast S3E → S3B → S5 path requires longitudinal observation."

Structural problem: if the direct path exists, S3B is not *necessary*. It becomes optional, available for people who need a coping layer but not required for everyone. What determines who needs S3B and who can skip it? The model doesn't say.

The edge suggests that psychological resilience, identity flexibility, or strong external support can bypass bargaining. If true, S3B is not a structural element; it is a *coping mechanism* some people use. That's a different architectural claim.

This weakens the graph's necessity story. Every state can be explained as either necessary or optional, and the model does not distinguish which.

**Fix:** Either (1) remove the S3E → S5 direct edge and make S3B the only path from S3E to S5. Or (2) add an upstream factor that predicts the direct path — identity resilience, coping history, support structure — and conditionally show when S3B can be skipped. The current state of "rare and indistinguishable in real time" provides no predictive power.

## 9. Related-literature anchors are conceptually elegant but unevenly grounded across the model — not yet tested

S3E's anchor (self-efficacy collapse + terror management) is compound and plausible. S7M (metacognitive calibration) is specific and grounded. But others are looser.

S6E borrows from variable-reward reinforcement (slot machines, social media). The claim: dopamine-driven engagement with unpredictably-reinforcing tools predicts AI overuse. Conceptually coherent but requires the assumption that AI output variability triggers the same reward loop as social-media notifications. That hypothesis is not tested; it is borrowed vocabulary.

S6D inverts Bandura — instead of mastery building self-efficacy, outsourcing erodes it. Described as "reverse application" and "offered as vocabulary." That is honest. But it is describing a *feedback loop*, not a *tested mechanism*. The loop plausibly runs, but whether it is the *primary driver* of S6D occupancy is unclaimed.

Not a flaw in descriptive modeling, but it means "related literature" is doing more work than the model acknowledges. Some anchors are grounded (S3E, S7M). Others are borrowed from adjacent domains (S6E, S6D, S6S) where mechanism transfer is assumed but untested.

**Fix:** Tier the related-literature anchors by strength of grounding. Mark which are directly borrowed from tested constructs (Bandura, Festinger, Kahan) vs which are borrowed from research domains where the transfer to AI use is assumed (variable-reward from slots, normative social influence from Asch). Readers will understand the vocabulary better knowing which anchors carry empirical weight.

## 10. S3B vs S7M distinction requires longitudinal data across multiple model releases and is contaminated by self-report — testable

The model claims S3B (Bargaining) and S7M (Maturity) differ on two axes:

1. **Where the boundary sits** — S3B sits on the *identity line*; S7M sits on the *competence line*.
2. **How boundaries move** — S3B moves under *capability pressure*; S7M moves on *measured evidence*.

Also: "The proposed distinguishing signals…require longitudinal observation across multiple model releases to confirm." And: "Contaminated by self-report once a subject has read this model."

Real diagnostic problem: if the distinction requires multi-release observation and is contaminated by self-knowledge, practitioners cannot reliably diagnose it in real time. Someone saying "I won't use AI for the core of my craft" could be either S3B protecting identity or S7M protecting against unreliability. You need to wait for the next model release and see whether the boundary moves under capability pressure (S3B) or stays until tested (S7M).

If the diagnostic signal only becomes clear in transition, the states are less useful for understanding where someone currently is.

**Fix:** Identify single-observation behavioral markers that distinguish S3B from S7M without requiring multi-release data. Candidates: S3B person shows more anxiety about crossing the boundary; S7M person can explain specific failure modes; S3B descriptions are emotional ("I won't let AI touch that") while S7M descriptions are evaluative ("AI isn't good enough yet"). Test these markers in a cross-sectional study before recommending the distinction for real-time diagnosis.

## Recommendations (prioritized by impact on structural clarity)

### 1. Remove or reframe the S2T → S2D edge (highest priority)

The edge softens the foundational fork. Either remove it (treat S2T failure as dropout, not conversion) or reframe S1 fork as probabilistic. This is the most fundamental architectural claim in the early model; clarity here cascades through everything downstream.

### 2. Clarify whether S7M, S7I, S7E, S7V are states or dimensions (high priority)

The complete mutual permittal of overlap suggests these are dimensions of integration, not separate states. Restructure around dimensions or explain why separate states reconverge completely. This affects how practitioners understand the endpoint of the model.

### 3. Develop single-observation diagnostic markers for PEN vs S2D, or merge them (medium-high priority)

Current three signals don't work in real time. Either find observable markers that distinguish them acutely, or merge both into "Defended Non-Adoption" with mechanisms listed. This affects real-time diagnosis in practice.

### 4. Reclassify S3X as a labeled exit or add within-model recovery (medium priority)

S3X currently has no returning transitions. Either remove state status or add a real within-model path. The current structure is terminal with three labels.

### 5. Split S0 into pre-encounter and permanent-non-user categories, or remove non-users from scope (medium priority)

The conflation obscures whether someone is in the system. Make this distinction explicit or acknowledge it cannot be diagnosed from current observation.
