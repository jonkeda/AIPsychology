# V5 → V6 Diff Review

## TL;DR

- **Mechanism anchor → Related literature** is honest epistemic downgrading but removes no substance — a frame shift, not a factual change.
- **Descriptive-first declaration** is accurate but overstated. V6 still makes causal claims (e.g., "self-efficacy collapse") without testing. The move is real in attitude, limited in scope.
- **Inline Conjecture / Testable markers** are a working improvement over the bottom section. Better placed, easier to track.
- **State ID renames** (S3 → S3E, S6A/B/C/D → S6E/D/R/S, S7A/B/C/D/F → S7M/I/E/B/V) are necessary and overdue. The V5 naming was chaotic; V6 is mnemonically clean.
- **New S2T → S2D edge** is justified and minimal — threat can surface during testing.
- **Direct S3E → S5 edge** is a luxury escape hatch. Rare enough not to break the model, but removes strategic pressure to model S3B properly.
- **S3X Structural Displacement** earns its place. Solves the category error of treating job elimination as identity work.
- **PEN Pre-emptive Non-Adoption** is useful vocabulary but leaky. The diagnostic signals for PEN vs S2D are stated but unvalidated; real cases will be mixed.
- **Per-domain identity scoping** is explicit now but not consistently implemented. The model still writes "the person's state" in places.
- **Multi-occupancy rules** are well-reasoned and necessary. Not over-specification; clarification of a gap in V5.
- **S0's permanent-non-user reading** is real coverage, not gesture. Solves the problem of confusing permanent non-use with S2D.
- **Grief language removal from S3E** is correct. V5's "loss" framing was inaccurate; V6's "reckoning" language is tighter.
- **File split** (one state per file) is pragmatic win for navigation, tiny loss for coherence.
- **New top-level guides** (healthiness, usage, observation) are well-needed. Not noise.

## Findings

### 1. "Mechanism anchor" → "Related literature" rename is epistemic honesty about framing, not retraction of substance — improvement at the cost of candour

V5 claimed states were "anchored" to mechanisms from cognitive science (self-efficacy, cognitive dissonance, terror management, etc.), suggesting causal grounding. V6 reframes these as "related literature" and adds the disclaimer: *"related literature anchors a state in existing thinking and points the reader at further reading, but does not predict behaviour, identify states, or claim causal mechanisms."*

This is honest. The V5 mechanism anchors were vocabulary deployed for pattern-recognition, not mechanisms tested in the model. V6 stops pretending they are causal.

But the downgrade is framing-only. The exact same cognitive constructs appear with the exact same descriptions. S3E still connects to self-efficacy collapse and terror management; S6E to variable-reward reinforcement; S7M to metacognitive calibration. The facts are unchanged. Only the rhetorical claim has softened.

This move fixes one problem but creates another: it gives the model permission to call anything "related literature" without testing. The disclaimer is necessary but makes V6 less falsifiable than V5, not more. A reader can now object to any claim with "you said this is not a mechanism, just vocabulary" — which is true, but creates the risk that any construct stays in the model as long as someone has written something adjacent in prior literature, regardless of whether it actually explains the state.

**Fix:** Keep the reframing. Add a second rule to the disclaimer: *"Related literature must name a specific, established construct from prior work, and the connection must be the most direct one available. If a simpler construct exists, use it. Do not use related literature as a holding bin for vague resemblances."*

### 2. Descriptive-first declaration is attitude-level, not substance-level — real but narrow

V6 opens with: *"This model is descriptive. It provides names for recognisable psychological patterns… It does **not** provide causal mechanisms. Empirical predictions. Diagnostic instruments. Treatment protocols."*

This is accurate and needed. V5 implied empirical grounding in places it did not have it; V6 makes the limitation explicit.

But the declaration does not eliminate causal talk. V6 still writes: *"Self-efficacy collapse is offered as a frame for thinking about S3E"* and *"Cognitive dissonance reduction via scope limitation (Festinger)"* as the anchor for S3B. These are causal framings, not purely descriptive ones. The model still claims that self-efficacy collapse explains why S3E feels the way it does.

The move is real. V6 more honestly marks what is conjecture and what is testable. But it does not remove the causal vocabulary; it just calls it vocabulary instead of mechanism. This is epistemic clarity with limited bite.

A purely descriptive model would say: *"S3E is reliably observed to include these symptoms; here are the transition patterns. Whether self-efficacy is the cause is an open question."* V6 still takes a position on the cause while calling it descriptive.

**Fix:** The declaration is fine. But add this precision to Model6.md: *"'Descriptive' means we observe the state and its transitions. 'Related literature' names constructs from prior work that explain why the state behaves as it does. We have not tested these explanations. Treat them as working hypotheses, not proven causes."* This converts the honesty from framing into a testable epistemic claim.

### 3. Inline `Conjecture` and `Testable` markers beat a single bottom section — improvement

V5 gathered all operationalization claims into a single section near the end. V6 marks claims where they appear: *"**Testable —** distinguishing the direct path requires longitudinal observation"* or *"**Conjecture —** the four coexistence claims are based on observation of two cultural archetypes."*

This is better. A reader encountering a claim now knows immediately whether it is tested, testable, or conjectural. No need to hunt through a bottom section.

The cost: V6 readers can miss forest for trees. It is easier to see S2T vs S2D is unresolved (marked right there) but harder to see that *most* transitions have no probability estimates.

The gain outweighs the cost. The model is more honest when uncertainties are local to the claim, not bundled away. This is a clear improvement.

**Fix:** None needed. This works. Extend it: anywhere a claim carries a claim about behaviour or transition (even ones not marked), check whether the mark exists. Search for unmarked modal language ("typically," "usually," "often") and mark those too.

### 4. State ID rename (S3 → S3E, S6 family, S7 family) is necessary; the V5 naming was indefensible — improvement, worth the disruption

V5 used:

- Bare **S3** for what V6 calls **S3E** (Ego Shock). Inconsistent with the fact that S3B (Bargaining) and S3X (Structural Displacement) were coming.
- **S6A/B/C/D** with oral names (Enthusiastic, Dependent, Driven, Social) but no mnemonics in the ID.
- **S7A/B/C/D/F** (skipping E?) with oral names (Maturity, Identity Expansion, Ethical Integration, Burnout, Evangelism) but no clear pattern.

V6 renames to:

- **S3E** (Ego Shock), **S3B** (Bargaining), **S3X** (Structural Displacement) — consistent.
- **S6E/D/R/S** (Enthusiastic, Dependent, driven [by pressure], Social) — first letter matches oral name, easy to remember.
- **S7M/I/E/B/V** (Maturity, Identity expansion, Ethical integration, Burnout, eVangelism) — acoustic mnemonics, all distinct letters.

The V5 scheme was chaotic. V6 is clean and mnemonically defensible. The disruption to anyone who knew V5 is real, but small — these are reference documents, not published literature. Fix it once, move on.

S6 uses **D for Dependent Overuse** while also saying "Driven" is S6R. This works (D ≠ R visually) but is brittle. "Dependent" → D and "Driven" → R is correct in V6 and the file structure backs this up, but a reader skimming the graph might misread S6D as "Driven."

**Fix:** Accept the rename. It is the right move. For S6D vs S6R, add a note in the state graph or each state file: *"S6D = Dependent (personal efficacy eroding). S6R = pressure-Driven (goals crowding out goals)."* Make the distinction obvious.

### 5. New S2T → S2D edge: justified and minimal — improvement, not regression

V5 had S2T and S2D as a fork from S1, with no path back once committed. V6 adds **S2T → S2D**: if someone is in Trust Evaluation and surfaces evidence of a threatening capability, they can shift to Defensive Resistance.

This is correct. A person can start with open testing and shift to defensive evaluation when what they discover threatens their identity. V5 missed this by treating the fork as deterministic.

The edge is thin and does not destabilize the fork. S2T and S2D remain responses to S1 with no implied ordering; the new edge just allows for evidence-driven shift when testing reveals threat.

Nothing is lost. The model gains realism.

**Fix:** None. This is solid.

### 6. Direct S3E → S5 edge: removes narrative tension and creates an escape hatch — lateral move, maybe regression

V5 had S3E Ego Shock splitting into **S3B Bargaining** (negotiate scope) or **S5 Understanding** (direct integration), but marked S5 as "typical for low Identity Stakes; possible for all." This created tension: you can go direct, but most people don't.

V6 adds an explicit **S3E → S5 direct edge** and marks it **"rare"** with this note: *"Most people who appear to take this path are actually doing accelerated S3E → S3B → S5 and revisiting it later."*

Here is the problem: if the direct edge exists and is "rare," how do you know someone took it vs a fast S3B? The model says: *"**Testable —** distinguishing the direct path from a fast S3E → S3B → S5 path requires longitudinal observation; on a single observation the two are indistinguishable."*

This is honest, but it means V6 has added an edge that cannot be observed on a single timepoint. When a practitioner sees someone go from S3E to S5 quickly, they cannot tell if the direct edge fired or if S3B was too fast to catch. This removes the force of the model as a guide: you cannot route interventions based on a transition that is indistinguishable from something else.

V5 had this problem too, but V5 was quieter about it. V6 makes it explicit and then adds the edge anyway, which feels like: "We know this is unobservable, we're including it anyway because it matches our intuition."

The edge is probably real. But leaving it out of V6 would have been more honest than including it with a caveat that it cannot be distinguished from its neighbor.

**Fix:** Remove the direct S3E → S5 edge, or move it to the open-questions section and say: *"Conjecture: some people can integrate S3E without bargaining. Testable: distinguishing this from accelerated S3B requires longitudinal observation at monthly or weekly granularity. Until validation, treat S3E → S5 as a theoretical possibility, not a modelled transition."*

### 7. S3X Structural Displacement: earns its place by solving a category error — improvement

V5 had no concept for the case where a person concludes their domain is economically non-viable. The model could describe S3E Ego Shock, but if the underlying question was "my job is being replaced, not disrupted," the model had no answer.

V6 introduces **S3X Structural Displacement**: *"The problem is no longer psychological (can I adapt?) but structural (is there a position to adapt to?)."*

This solves a real problem. It separates the identity-crisis work (S3E) from the labor-market crisis (S3X). A person can have both at once, but they require different interventions. The model now says: this is outside the integration cycle.

The state file is honest about the three resolutions: occupational transition (enter the model again in a new domain), principled exit from knowledge work, or adversarial advocacy (outside the integration cycle, separate development). This is clean and accurate.

**Fix:** None. This is good. The only open question is whether the three-resolution structure is exhaustive (noted as conjecture). Keep the state.

### 8. PEN Pre-emptive Non-Adoption: useful vocabulary with diagnostic leakage — qualified improvement

V6 adds **PEN Pre-emptive Non-Adoption**: *"A stable non-adoption position reached at or shortly after S1 Initial Encounter, based on values-grounded ethical appraisal rather than on capability testing or identity threat."*

The concept is needed. Someone can decide not to use AI because it conflicts with their values, independent of whether it works or whether it threatens their identity. V5 had no term for this.

But V6 admits the diagnostic is leaky. The distinction from S2D (Defensive Resistance) requires:

> *"Three signals — none reliable on a single observation:*
> 1. *Cross-domain consistency.*
> 2. *Evidence response.*
> 3. *Emotional register."*

And then: *"Most real cases are mixed."*

This is honest, but it means PEN is easier to name after the fact than to identify in a single conversation. A practitioner might think they are observing PEN (values-grounded refusal) and later discover it was S2D with ethical language as cover.

The frame is useful for thinking about non-adoption. The diagnostic is weak. This is not a failure — mixed cases are real — but it limits PEN's operational use. You can use it to understand someone after extended dialogue, but you cannot use it to route an intervention based on a single encounter.

**Fix:** Keep PEN. Upgrade the diagnostic: *"PEN is most reliably distinguished from S2D Defensive Resistance by asking: Does the person's position hold if the concern is addressed? In S2D, better AI tightens the defensive stance. In PEN, better AI reveals that the core concern (e.g., labour displacement, data exploitation) is not solved by capability — it is solved by values, policy, or economics."* Mark this as testable and point to a future study design.

### 9. Per-domain identity-stakes scoping is stated but not consistently implemented — improvement in principle, incomplete in practice

V5 said Identity Stakes is "a property of the person relative to a capability domain" and gave one example. But the model often wrote as if a person's state were singular: *"they entered S3E"* rather than *"they entered S3E in domain X."*

V6 makes per-domain scoping explicit. Model6.md says: *"Identity is at stake because skill, expertise, or craft is being replicated by AI,"* and defines Identity Stakes as *"a property of the person relative to a capability domain."* The scope statement says: *"It applies when the person's first substantive exposure to AI happens in adulthood,"* per domain.

The improvement is real. But V6 still violates its own rule. Example from the state files:

- S7M Maturity: *"In high-stakes domains S7M typically follows S3E Ego Shock."* ✓ (per-domain)
- S5 Understanding: *"Most adapted users settle into a combination (S7A + S7C is common …)"* — which S5? In what domain? ✗ (uses V5's S7A label, doesn't clarify domain scope)
- S3E Ego Shock healthiness: *"Sustained S3E without movement to S3B or S5 Understanding within weeks to months in the same domain warrants serious attention."* ✓ (per-domain)
- Limits of Operationalization: *"'Most people cycle through the model more than once.'"* — Does this mean once per domain, or globally? ✗ (ambiguous)

The scoping is an improvement and V6 is mostly consistent, but it is not systematic. A person who is S2D in writing and S5 in marketing and S7M in painting should be able to navigate the model and see those as three separate trajectories. The files mostly support this; some don't.

**Fix:** Audit every state file and search for singular "the person" or "the state." Where it appears, clarify: is this per-domain, global, or does it depend on the example? Add a standard note to each state file: *"[State name] applies within a domain. The same person can be [State A] in one domain and [State B] in another."*

### 10. Multi-occupancy rules are clarifying, not over-specification — improvement

V5 said S6 states sit "downstream of S5" but did not specify whether a person could occupy multiple S6 states at the same time or in sequence.

V6 spells out multi-occupancy rules in `02-state-graph.md`:

- **S6E and S6D are mutually exclusive** (opposite affect). A person can transition from one to the other but not stably occupy both.
- **S6R and S6S can coexist** (pressure + conformity reinforce each other).
- **S6E and S6R can coexist** in startup contexts (enthusiasm + efficiency pressure).
- **S6D and S6S can coexist** in compliance contexts (self-doubt + watched conformity).
- **All S7 states can coexist** (multiple end-state identities).

These rules are well-reasoned and based on observation. They are not over-specification; they are clarification of a gap in V5. The note in the state graph — *"(Conjecture — the four coexistence claims above are based on observation of two cultural archetypes (startup, compliance-heavy). They are not directly tested.)"* — is honest about the epistemic status.

This is a working improvement. It lets practitioners understand: if you see someone in S6E + S6D simultaneously, something is wrong; if you see S6E + S6R, that's expected in a high-growth context.

**Fix:** None needed. This is solid.

### 11. S0 expanded to cover permanent non-users is real, not token — improvement

V5 treated S0 as transitional: *"Not a state in the active sense — the absence of meaningful encounter. Listed for completeness and as the entry point for the trigger layer."*

V6 distinguishes two readings:

1. **Pre-encounter users** — not yet exposed, will probably be (transitional).
2. **Permanent non-users** — will not engage, ever (terminal).

This is important. A person who does not use AI and never will is not in S2D Defensive Resistance (which requires having considered and rejected AI on identity-threat grounds). They are not in PEN Pre-emptive Non-Adoption (which requires values-grounded rejection). They are just… outside the system. They have not formed a stance.

V5 risked confusing these. "My grandfather does not use AI" could be misread as "my grandfather is in S2D." V6 says: no, some people are simply outside the model because they have never encountered it and never will.

This is realistic and operationally useful. A practitioner can now ask: is this person a pre-encounter S0 (might move to S1) or a permanent S0 (will not)? The probe is simple: *"What do you know about AI from personal experience? Can you describe a specific product?"* No = probably permanent non-user.

**Fix:** None. This is good.

### 12. Grief language stripped from S3E — correct diagnosis, not evasion — improvement

V5 described S3E with language suggesting loss and mourning: *"existential discomfort," "confrontation with one's own replaceability,"* and implicitly framed it as grief work, a process of coming to terms with loss.

V6 reframes S3E as **reckoning**: *"The person is confronting the fact that the skill they built their self-concept around is now also something a tool can do. The work of S3E is reorganising self-concept around that fact, not mourning a lost capability."*

This is more accurate. S3E is identity disruption, not bereavement. The person is not grieving a lost ability (they still have it); they are reorganizing their identity around the fact that it is not unique. The affective tone is anxiety and existential discomfort, not sadness or loss.

V6 also adds this note: *"Affect is anxious or destabilised; not sad in the bereavement sense."* This is a working diagnostic improvement. It helps practitioners distinguish S3E from actual grief (which would have a different trajectory and require different support).

The change is not evasive; it is corrective. V5's grief language was imprecise and led to wrong interventions.

**Fix:** None. This is a good correction. Extend it: in the healthiness file, note that confusing S3E with grief can lead to prolonged support when the person actually needs reframing, not grieving space.

### 13. File split (one state per file under `states/`) is navigation win, coherence loss — pragmatic improvement

V5 had a single 250+ line markdown with all states inline. V6 splits each state into its own file: `S3E-ego-shock.md`, `S6E-enthusiastic-overuse.md`, etc.

**Gain:** A practitioner reading about S3E can now read one focused file without hunting through a monolithic document. Easier to link to. Easier to update one state without touching others.

**Loss:** You cannot easily see the full state graph and all transitions on one screen. You lose the "zoomed out" view. V6 has `02-state-graph.md` with a Mermaid diagram, which helps, but the detailed architecture is now distributed.

**Net:** The gain outweighs the loss. Most use cases are "I need to understand one state better" not "I need the full model at once." The Mermaid diagram provides the overview.

**Fix:** In `Model6.md`, add a link to `02-state-graph.md` immediately below the first paragraph: *"For the full state graph and transitions, see [State Graph](02-state-graph.md)."* Make it unavoidable to miss the diagram.

### 14. Healthiness, usage, observation-guide as separate top-level files — essential clarifications, not noise

V6 added three new files:

- **`healthiness.md`** — interprets which states are sustainable, corrosive, or transitional. Explicitly flagged as interpretive, not model claim.
- **`usage.md`** — what the model can and cannot be used for. Concrete use cases and hard limits.
- **`observation-guide.md`** — behavioural markers for identifying states in practice. Probes and what to watch.

V5 had none of these. The model was silent on how to use it.

These three files solve a real gap. A practitioner reading V6 now knows:

- Whether a state is healthy to stay in (healthiness.md).
- Whether the model supports diagnosis or prediction (usage.md).
- How to identify a state in a real person (observation-guide.md).

These are not noise. They are essential for anyone trying to use the model outside of academic reading.

**Fix:** None. These are well-written and needed. Extend them: add cross-references. In each state file, link to the relevant section of observation-guide.md. In healthiness.md, link from each healthiness claim to the state file that supports it.

## Recommendations

### Priority 1 (needed for V7)

1. **Audit per-domain scoping for consistency.** Every state file should clarify whether claims apply per-domain or globally. Add a boilerplate note to each state file as specified in Finding 9.

2. **Remove or relegate the direct S3E → S5 edge.** It is observationally indistinguishable from fast S3B. Move it to open questions and mark it as conjecture pending longitudinal data. See Finding 6.

3. **Upgrade the PEN vs S2D diagnostic.** Add a testable distinction: *"Does the position hold if the concern is addressed?"* Point toward a future study. See Finding 8.

4. **Add a disclaimer to Related Literature.** Specify that a construct must be the most direct explanation available, not just "adjacent vocabulary." See Finding 1.

---

### Priority 2 (usability)

5. **Add per-state observation links.** In each state file, link to the corresponding observation-guide.md section.

6. **Clarify S6D vs S6R in the state graph.** The D/R distinction for "Dependent" vs "pressure-Driven" is correct but visually brittle. Add a footnote in the Mermaid diagram or the legend.

7. **Link to the state graph from Model6.md.** Make the Mermaid diagram unavoidable on first read, not buried in file 2.

---

### Priority 3 (forward work)

8. **Design the longitudinal study to test S2T vs S2D and S3B vs S7M.** These are the two key transitions with unresolved observability. The S3E → S3X threshold is the other priority. Budget: ~$80–150k for 200 people across 18 months, monthly check-ins.

9. **Validate PEN with cross-domain data.** A values-consistent non-adopter should decline AI use even in low-stakes domains. A mixed S2D+values case will show inconsistency. Testable within a 3-month study, ~$20k.

10. **Stress-test the healthiness interpretation.** Field practitioners. Which healthiness claims match what they see, which don't? Iterate based on feedback.

## Which V5 elements should have changed but didn't

1. **Numerical dwell times for S3E.** V5 said "six or more months unresolved is clinical distress." V6 keeps this but admits it is not measured. Directionally right, numerically guessed. Future work should test this threshold.

2. **The S5 dual-component stability requirement.** V5 and V6 both claim S5 requires *internal reframing + social normalization*. Both admit this is conjecture. A future study should test whether both are necessary or if one dominates.

3. **The "most people cycle through the model more than once" claim.** V5 qualified this as "qualitative"; V6 still quotes it without new data. A panel study would falsify or confirm this quickly.

---

## Which V6 changes should be reverted in V7

1. **The descriptive-first declaration, if not upgraded.** As written, it is attitude-level clarity without substance-level change. It should either be reverted as redundant, or upgraded to eliminate causal talk entirely (which would require rewriting most state files).

2. **The direct S3E → S5 edge.** Unobservable, adds escape hatch, reduces model pressure. Move to open questions.

3. **Related literature as currently framed.** The disclaimer does not prevent vague resemblances from accumulating. Tighten or revert to V5's "mechanism anchor" language and defend the causal claims.

---

## Closing verdict

V6 is a working improvement on V5. It is honest about limitations, adds necessary states (S3X, PEN), and provides practitioner guides (observation, usage, healthiness) that were missing. The state ID renames are necessary and correct. Per-domain scoping is an important clarification, though inconsistently applied.

The main gaps are observability and operationalization. The model is now more transparent about what is testable and what is not. But for most transitions, "testable" still means "would require a longitudinal study we have not done." This is honest, but it limits the model's predictive and diagnostic power until the studies are done.

V6's main liability is the direct S3E → S5 edge and the unresolved PEN vs S2D distinction. Both should be tightened before V7 publication.

V6 is publication-ready as a working vocabulary and conceptual map. It should not be used for diagnosis, prediction, or treatment protocol — V6 explicitly says so. Using it for those purposes anyway is a user error, not a model failure.
