# Structural Critique of Model V6 — Plain Language

## TL;DR

- V6 adds an edge so someone can switch mid-path from fair-minded testing (S2T) to defensive resistance (S2D). That's realistic, but it breaks the clean fork at S1 that the model is built on. Identity stakes (how much AI threatens your sense of self) is now just one weak signal, not the actual decider.
- The four S6 overuse states have rules about which can coexist, but those rules look made-up rather than falling out naturally from the definitions. In practice, all four mechanisms could fire at once.
- The four S7 end-states can all overlap completely. If they can all coexist, it's not clear they're separate states — they might just be four angles on the same thing.
- S7I (Identity Expansion) looks identical to neighboring states from the outside. You can't tell someone is in S7I without asking them.
- S3X (Structural Displacement — when someone loses their job to AI) has no paths back into the model. All three "resolutions" lead out. It's a labeled exit, not a state.
- The model admits that the three signals distinguishing PEN (Pre-emptive Non-Adoption — a principled refusal to use AI) from S2D (defensive resistance) are unreliable in practice. If practitioners can't tell them apart, the states may not be worth keeping separate.
- S0 (Baseline — haven't used AI yet) lumps together two different groups: people who haven't encountered AI yet, and people who never will. Those require completely different treatment.
- The model marks a direct path from S3E (Ego Shock) → S5 (Understanding) as "rare but real." That makes S3B (Bargaining) optional, not required. The model doesn't say what determines who can skip it.
- V6 is more honest than V5. It says "this is descriptive" and "we don't know this yet" in the right places. But honesty about framing doesn't fix the underlying classification problems. Three states (PEN, S3X, S7I) can only be confirmed by asking the person or watching them over time. For a model that claims to be a shared vocabulary, that's a real gap.

## 1. The S2T → S2D edge turns the foundational S1 fork from a clean binary into a messy probability spread — testable

At S1 (Initial Encounter), the model splits based on **Identity Stakes** (how much AI threatens something central to who you are). Low stakes → S2T Trust Evaluation (you evaluate AI fairly). High stakes → S2D Defensive Resistance (you evaluate AI defensively). Clean, architectural.

V6 adds a new edge: someone can enter S2T believing they have low or moderate stakes, discover during evaluation that AI actually does threaten something identity-central, and convert to S2D. That's plausible — people do underestimate how much something matters to them until they encounter it.

Here's the structural problem. Once that conversion path exists, the S1 fork is no longer a clean binary. Identity stakes becomes an initial guess, not a determinant. Two people with identical self-assessed stakes can end up on different paths depending on what testing reveals. The S1 fork's explanatory power drops from "this determines your path" to "this is one weak signal."

This isn't wrong as a description of reality. But it means the foundational claim — "identity stakes drives the branch" — is now much weaker than the diagram implies.

**Fix:** Either (1) remove the S2T → S2D edge and treat identity-threat discovery as an S2T failure or dropout rather than a conversion; or (2) reframe the S1 fork as probabilistic and be explicit that the observed S2T vs S2D split is much messier than identity stakes alone predicts. The observation guide should include a check for whether someone's identity stakes self-assessment is stable or likely to shift during testing.

## 2. S6 states have mutual-exclusion rules that look imposed rather than falling out naturally from the definitions — not yet tested

The four S6 overuse states are defined by *why* someone overuses AI:

- **S6E Enthusiastic Overuse** — dopamine-driven reward loops from unpredictably-good outputs (same mechanism as social media notifications).
- **S6D Dependent Overuse** — self-efficacy (belief in your own ability to do the work) has eroded from repeatedly outsourcing tasks.
- **S6R Driven Overuse** — external metrics or mandates push usage up (productivity tracking, manager pressure).
- **S6S Social Overuse** — team norms make not using AI look like falling behind.

The model's rules about which states can coexist:

- S6E and S6D cannot coexist (opposite emotional profiles: energized vs anxious).
- S6E and S6R can coexist (common in startup cultures).
- S6R and S6S can coexist (common in compliance-heavy cultures).
- S6E + S6D and S6R + S6S are unstable combinations.

Those rules are careful. But consider someone in a high-pressure knowledge-work job: they get dopamine hits from a fresh model (S6E), their confidence has eroded from repeated outsourcing (S6D), their company tracks AI usage (S6R), and their team treats non-use as a red flag (S6S). All four mechanisms firing at once.

The model rules this out because S6E and S6D have "opposite affect" (opposite emotional profiles). But that's a summary observation, not a structural reason they can't overlap. A person can be energized about their output volume while anxious about their autonomy. The emotional profiles don't have to be clean.

What's really happening: the four may not be separate *states* but separate *causes of overuse* that can stack. The model has drawn them as exclusive occupancy zones when they might be better understood as independent drivers.

**Fix:** Either (1) relabel S6E, S6D, S6R, S6S as "overuse drivers" or "mechanisms" rather than mutually-exclusive states, and treat them as independent factors that can combine; or (2) keep them as states but run a study checking whether the mutual-exclusion rules actually hold in high-pressure work cultures. The current rules read as after-the-fact constraints, not established facts.

## 3. The four S7 end-states can fully overlap, making it unclear whether they are separate states or just dimensions of integrated use — not yet tested

S7M (Maturity), S7I (Identity Expansion), S7E (Ethical Integration), S7V (Evangelism), and S7B (Burnout as a phase). The model says any S7 state can coexist with any other except S7B.

Common pairings the model lists:
- S7M + S7E (the reflective practitioner)
- S7I + S7V (the creative leader)
- S7M + S7V (the steady mentor)
- S7I + S7E (the creator wrestling with what they're building)

But here's the natural endpoint: someone uses AI with calibrated judgment, has built new identity around expanded capabilities, reflects on the ethical implications, and helps others navigate the same path. That person is simultaneously S7M + S7I + S7E + S7V. From the outside, that just looks like integrated use.

The fact that you can decompose it into four labels doesn't mean they're four separate states. It might mean they're four aspects of the same thing.

The model's own observation guide for S7M vs S7I signals the problem: "both look like 'uses AI competently' from outside. Confirming the distinction requires self-report." If they coexist completely and require self-report to distinguish, the question is whether they should be labeled separate states at all. They read more like dimensions: *calibration* (S7M), *identity work* (S7I), *values work* (S7E), *helping others* (S7V).

**Fix:** Clarify whether S7M, S7I, S7E, S7V are alternate paths to integration (separate trajectories that happen to reconverge) or overlapping dimensions of integrated use. If dimensions, restructure around them. If separate trajectories, explain why the endpoints coexist so completely.

## 4. S7I (Identity Expansion) looks identical to S7M from the outside — you can't distinguish them without asking — not yet tested

S7M (Maturity) and S7I both present as: high-delegation use, competent work, deliberate choices about when to use AI. The model says so directly: "both look like 'uses AI competently' from outside. Confirming the distinction requires self-report."

S7I's defining marker: "The person identifies themselves through capabilities that did not exist for them pre-AI." That's a pure identity claim. Not observable. Requires asking.

A translator using AI to edit AI output, now calling themselves "AI-assisted translator" — is that S7M (matured into a new working boundary) or S7I (AI is now **constitutive**, meaning it's part of how the person defines what they do)? Both descriptions fit. The only difference is internal: does the person think "I'm a translator who uses tools" or "I'm a translator-with-AI, where the AI is part of the job description"?

This matters for predictions. The model claims S6E Enthusiastic Overuse often leads to S7I, and S7I often leads to S7V Evangelism. But if S7I can't be confirmed from external evidence, practitioners can't diagnose it in real time or predict what comes next.

**Fix:** Either (1) find observable markers that distinguish S7I from S7M — for example, S7I people actively seek new capabilities, describe their work differently than they did pre-AI, pursue feedback on the expanded scope specifically; or (2) accept that S7I requires self-report and be explicit that the state is useful for conversations but not for predictions. Restructure any claims about S7I transitions accordingly.

## 5. S3X (Structural Displacement) has no paths back into the model — it's a labeled exit, not a state — not yet tested

S3X covers people whose job is eliminated by AI — not just disrupted, but economically non-viable in its current form. The model describes three paths out of S3X:

1. Occupational transition (for example, translator → AI output editor) — enters a new S0 → S1 arc in a different domain.
2. Principled exit — leaves the field entirely.
3. Adversarial advocacy — becomes an activist for regulation or labor protection. The model explicitly notes this is "outside the integration cycle" and not modeled.

All three lead outside the model. No S3X → S5, no S3X → S7. One way in, three ways out, all terminal.

Functionally, S3X is identical to dropout, except labeled by economic cause rather than psychological cost. The label is valuable — knowing someone left because their job was eliminated is different from knowing they left because AI made them anxious. But calling it a "state" overstates it. States have transitions. Exits don't.

**Fix:** Reclassify S3X as a labeled form of dropout (economic dropout), or add a real return path that doesn't require domain change, economic rescue, or field exit. If S3X is a state, it needs at least one within-model transition.

## 6. PEN vs S2D rests on three signals the model admits are unreliable in practice — not yet tested

PEN (Pre-emptive Non-Adoption) is when someone refuses to use AI based on genuine values or ethical conviction — not because they feel threatened. S2D (Defensive Resistance) is when someone refuses because AI threatens their sense of who they are.

The model lists three ways to tell them apart:

1. **Cross-domain consistency** — does the ethical objection apply uniformly, or only where the person has skin in the game?
2. **Evidence response** — does the stated concern hold up even if you fix it, or does the person just find another objection?
3. **Emotional register** — S2D has anxiety (threat to identity); PEN has conviction (values protection).

Then the model says: "none reliable on a single observation" and "most real cases are mixed."

That's honest. It's also a structural problem. If practitioners cannot reliably distinguish PEN from S2D in real time, the states may not be operationally useful. Both people decline to engage. Both cite principled reasons. Both resist evidence at some level. The observer has no reliable way to know the underlying mechanism.

A person can genuinely hold both at once: protecting identity *and* holding genuine ethical objections. A freelance journalist who won't use AI for fact-checking — is that PEN (real concern about algorithmic bias) or S2D (threatened by the implication that AI fact-checking is better)? Likely both. Labeling such a case as one or the other may be meaningless.

**Fix:** Run a diagnostic study on people who cite values-based reasons for declining AI. Check whether cross-domain consistency and emotional register are actually distinguishable by trained observers. If most real cases are mixed, consider merging PEN and S2D into a single "Defended Non-Adoption" state with two named mechanisms (identity-protective and values-grounded) that often co-occur.

## 7. S0 (Baseline) lumps two different populations into one box — not yet tested

S0 is described as having "two readings":

1. **Pre-encounter users** — haven't used AI yet but likely will. S0 → S1 expected.
2. **Permanent non-users** — won't use AI. S0 is terminal.

Those aren't two readings of the same thing. They're two different categories. Pre-encounter users are in the system; permanent non-users are outside it.

The model tries to distinguish them by checking whether the person has formed an active stance. But this requires the practitioner to predict future AI exposure, which isn't observable from current data.

In practice: a 58-year-old accountant in a domain where AI isn't yet standard might look like a permanent non-user today. In two years, their firm adopts AI tools and they become a pre-encounter user entering S1. Nothing about the current observation tells you which one they are.

Putting both in S0 obscures the distinction. Permanent non-users should not be in the model at all (they're outside scope). Pre-encounter users should be in S0, with explicit acknowledgment that telling the two groups apart requires knowing something about their future context.

**Fix:** Either split S0 into **S0P (Pre-Encounter)** and **S0N (Permanent Non-User, Out of Scope)**, or remove permanent non-users from the model entirely. Make S0 applicability conditional on "expected future exposure" and acknowledge this condition can't always be assessed from current data.

## 8. The rare direct S3E → S5 edge makes S3B Bargaining optional rather than required — testable

The model includes a direct path from S3E (Ego Shock — the panic state, when AI feels like a direct threat to your professional identity) straight to S5 (Understanding — stable, integrated use), bypassing S3B (Bargaining — where you negotiate a partial deal with yourself about where AI is and isn't allowed).

The path is marked "rare." The model also notes: "Most people who appear to take this path are actually doing accelerated S3B Bargaining and revisiting it later." But the edge exists, and "distinguishing the direct path from a fast S3E → S3B → S5 path requires longitudinal observation."

Structural problem: if the direct path exists, S3B is not required. It becomes optional — a coping layer some people need and others can skip. The model doesn't say what determines who can skip it.

The implication is that psychological resilience, identity flexibility, or strong external support can bypass bargaining. If that's true, S3B isn't structural — it's a coping mechanism for people who need extra time. That's a different architectural claim.

**Fix:** Either (1) remove the S3E → S5 direct edge and make S3B the only path from S3E; or (2) add an upstream factor that predicts the direct path — identity resilience, coping history, support structure — and conditionally show when S3B can be skipped. The current "rare and indistinguishable" framing provides no predictive power.

## 9. The model's research anchors are unevenly grounded — some are solid, others are borrowed assumptions — not yet tested

V6 ties each state to relevant research. Some of these anchors are well-grounded; others borrow from adjacent fields where the transfer to AI use hasn't been tested.

Solid anchors:
- S3E (Ego Shock) → self-efficacy collapse (the feeling that you're no longer capable) and terror management (the psychological response to reminders of irrelevance). Specific and plausible.
- S7M (Maturity) → metacognitive calibration (knowing how well you know things). Specific and grounded.

Looser anchors:
- S6E borrows from variable-reward reinforcement — the same mechanism that makes slot machines and social media addictive. Plausible that AI output variability triggers the same reward loop. Not tested in this context.
- S6D inverts Bandura's self-efficacy research: instead of mastery building confidence, outsourcing erodes it. The model calls this a "reverse application" and "offered as vocabulary." Honest. But describing a feedback loop isn't the same as confirming it's the primary driver of S6D.
- S6S borrows from normative social influence (social pressure to conform, originally studied in group settings such as the Asch conformity experiments). Whether workplace AI adoption works the same way is assumed, not tested.

None of this is a flaw in descriptive modeling. But "related literature" is doing more work than the model acknowledges.

**Fix:** Tier the anchors by strength. Mark which are directly borrowed from tested constructs (Bandura, Festinger, Kahan) and which are borrowed from research domains where the transfer to AI use is assumed. Readers will calibrate their trust better knowing which anchors carry real empirical weight.

## 10. Telling S3B Bargaining apart from S7M Maturity requires tracking the same person across multiple model releases, and self-knowledge contaminates the test — testable

S3B (Bargaining) and S7M (Maturity) both look like "I use AI for some things but not others." The model's two distinguishing signals:

1. **Where the boundary sits** — S3B's boundary protects identity; S7M's boundary tracks competence.
2. **How the boundary moves** — S3B's boundary shifts under capability pressure (new model comes out, the line moves regardless of whether it was tested); S7M's boundary shifts on measured evidence (the person tested it and updated deliberately).

The model also says: "The proposed distinguishing signals require longitudinal observation across multiple model releases to confirm." And: "Contaminated by self-report once a subject has read this model."

So you can't tell them apart in a single conversation. You need to watch the same person across at least two model releases and see whether their boundary moves under pressure (S3B) or only after testing (S7M). Months of observation minimum.

And if the person has read this model, they know the distinction — and might describe their behavior using S7M language even if they're really in S3B.

If the diagnostic signal only becomes clear in transition, the states are harder to use for understanding where someone currently is.

**Fix:** Identify single-observation behavioral markers that distinguish S3B from S7M without waiting for a new model release. Candidates: S3B people show more anxiety when the boundary is questioned; S7M people can name specific failure modes; S3B descriptions are emotional ("I won't let AI touch that") while S7M descriptions are evaluative ("AI isn't reliable enough for this task yet"). Test these markers in a point-in-time study before recommending the distinction for real-time diagnosis.

## Recommendations (in order of impact on structural clarity)

### 1. Remove or reframe the S2T → S2D edge (highest priority)

This edge softens the foundational fork. Either remove it (treat discovery of identity threat during testing as dropout, not conversion), or reframe the S1 fork as probabilistic. The S1 fork is the first architectural decision in the early model — clarity here flows into everything downstream.

### 2. Clarify whether S7M, S7I, S7E, S7V are separate states or dimensions (high priority)

Their complete mutual overlap suggests dimensions of integration, not separate states. Restructure around dimensions, or explain why separate trajectories reconverge so completely. This affects how practitioners understand the model's endpoint.

### 3. Develop single-observation diagnostic markers for PEN vs S2D, or merge them (medium-high priority)

The three current signals don't work in real time. Either find observable markers that distinguish them acutely, or merge both into "Defended Non-Adoption" with mechanisms listed. This affects real-time diagnosis in practice.

### 4. Reclassify S3X as a labeled exit or add within-model recovery (medium priority)

S3X has no returning transitions. Either remove state status or add a real within-model path. The current structure is terminal with three labels.

### 5. Split S0 into pre-encounter and permanent-non-user categories, or remove non-users from scope (medium priority)

The conflation obscures whether someone is in the system. Make this distinction explicit, or acknowledge it cannot be diagnosed from current observation.
