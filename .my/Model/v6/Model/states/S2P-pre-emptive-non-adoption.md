# S2P — Pre-emptive Non-Adoption

> *"I have thought about this. I don't need to try it to know it's not for me."*

A stable non-adoption position reached at or shortly after [S1 Initial Encounter](S1-initial-encounter.md), based on values-grounded ethical appraisal rather than on capability testing or identity threat.

The person has performed a rapid ethical appraisal at first encounter and reached a stable conclusion to decline engagement. The conclusion is **prior to capability assessment** — the person is not declining because AI fails at the task, but because they have concluded that using it conflicts with their values.

## Distinction from neighbouring states

S2P is distinct from:

- **[S7E Ethical Integration](S7E-ethical-integration.md)** — which requires extended use followed by reflective stance. S7E users are AI users who think carefully about their use. S2P users never substantively use AI in the affected domain.
- **[S2D Defensive Resistance](S2D-defensive-resistance.md)** — which is identity-protection cognition. S2P is moral identity prioritisation. The person is protecting their values, not their competence.
- **Emotional dropout** — which is a response to a negative experience. S2P happens before any significant engagement.
- **Practical dropout** — which is triggered by tool failure. S2P happens regardless of capability.

## Mechanism anchors

Conceptually related to **moral identity prioritisation** — the tendency to act in line with one's core values when those values conflict with a course of action, independent of whether that course of action would produce good outcomes by other measures. Moral identity (the degree to which being a moral person is central to one's self-concept) is an established construct in social psychology. Borrowed as vocabulary.

## Identity Stakes / Delegation modulation

S2P is largely independent of Identity Stakes — the position is values-driven rather than identity-driven. Delegation Level is effectively zero in the affected domain.

## Entry condition from S1

Pre-emptive non-adoption is most likely when:

- The person has prior ethical frameworks applied to technology (privacy advocacy, open-source ethics, labour rights).
- The ethical concern is specific and addressable in principle. The key diagnostic test: if resolving the concern would not change the position, the stated reason is a proxy for a deeper value.
- The encounter happens in a low-personal-stakes context — the person is not defending their own livelihood, they are acting on values.

## What makes S2P stable

The position is stable when the ethical objection is grounded in a value that is unlikely to shift under capability pressure. *"I believe in the economic value of human creative work"* or *"I believe AI training data practices are exploitative"* are positions that hold regardless of whether AI improves. They are not positions about AI's current capabilities.

## Transitions

- → **S5 Understanding** or **S7E Ethical Integration** — possible if the stated ethical concern is substantively addressed and the person genuinely re-evaluates. Rare, but S2P is not treated as permanent.
- → **Stable non-adoption** — the most common outcome. The person does not progress further in the model for this domain.
- → **S2D Defensive Resistance** — possible if closer engagement reveals identity stakes the person had not initially recognised. The ethical frame was covering a competence-threat dynamic.

## ⚡ External triggers

No strong ⚡ marker. The position is internally grounded. Regulatory changes (AI banned in a domain, copyright law outcomes) can validate the position externally but typically do not create it.

## Healthiness

**Sustainable.** S2P is a legitimate stable position, not a pathology and not a failure to adapt. It is values-rooted non-use.

## Diagnostic difficulty: S2P versus S2D Defensive Resistance with ethical language

From outside, pre-emptive non-adoption and S2D dressed in ethical language look almost identical. Both decline to engage, both cite principled reasons, both discount capability evidence. The distinction only becomes visible over time.

Three signals — none reliable on a single observation:

1. **Cross-domain consistency.** Does the ethical objection apply consistently across domains, including those where the person's identity is not at stake? An S2D person typically has no objection in a low-stakes domain; an S2P person maintains the same objection regardless of the domain's proximity to their own identity.
2. **Evidence response.** Does the position hold if the stated concern is demonstrably addressed? In both S2P and S2D, capability evidence does not break the position, but the *reason* differs. In S2P, better AI strengthens the ethical concern (more displacement, more bias at scale). In S2D, better AI tightens the defensive position. An S2P person is also unlikely to have generated a concern that capability improvement could dissolve.
3. **Emotional register.** S2D has anxiety underneath — fear of obsolescence. S2P has moral conviction, sometimes contempt. The threat appraisal is "this threatens something I care about" not "this threatens me."
4. **Identity-threat removal.** If the person's professional identity in the domain is taken off the table — for example, by asking about AI in an adjacent field they have no career stake in — an S2D position typically relaxes; an S2P position holds or intensifies. This is the most direct probe available in a structured interview.

**Scope note:** The diagnostic signals above are drawn from Western secular ethics traditions — privacy advocacy, labour rights, open-source ethics. They are not validated for religious rejection of AI (where the commitment is to a doctrinal position rather than an individual values framework) or for community-embedded rejection (where the stance is inherited from collective memory of technological disruption rather than individual reasoning). The modeller does not have sufficient knowledge of other cultural frameworks to extend the diagnostic frame to those contexts.

Most real cases are mixed. A person can be simultaneously in S2D (protecting competence) and making genuine ethical arguments that are also true and genuinely held. Labeling a mixed case as "pure S2P" would be wrong; so would "pure S2D." Use the frame to understand the mixture, not to classify it.

## Observation markers

- The position was formed at or shortly after first encounter, not after extended use.
- The person can articulate specific values being protected, not specific capability concerns.
- Stated values predate AI as an issue (privacy advocacy, labour ethics, environmental concern).

## Open questions

- **Testable —** cross-domain consistency over multiple months distinguishes values-driven S2P from identity-driven S2D dressed in ethical language.
- Whether S2P is best modelled as a single state or as a family of related stances (anti-displacement, anti-data-practices, anti-environmental-cost, etc.) is undefined.
