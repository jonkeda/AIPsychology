# Observation Guide

Practitioner-facing. What to look for when trying to identify which state someone is in. Not a diagnostic instrument — a set of heuristics for orienting a conversation or an observation.

## S6 states — distinguishing them in practice

All four S6 states share high AI usage volume. The driver differs.

### S6E Enthusiastic Overuse

- Usage spikes align with reward moments: a fresh model release, a team demo, a positive reaction to an AI-produced output.
- The person talks about AI with energy and little critical distance.
- They try AI on problems where it is the wrong tool and are surprised when it fails.
- Output volume is up; output quality is variable and the person doesn't track it.

**Probe:** *"When was the last time AI surprised you in a bad way? What did you do with that?"* An S6E person will either not have an answer or will quickly pivot back to a positive memory.

**What to watch:** usage patterns against reward timing. Remove the leaderboard, remove the demo — see if usage drops or shifts to more deliberate patterns.

### S6D Dependent Overuse

- High AI use but anxious affect, not enthusiastic.
- The person checks AI output against other AI output rather than against their own judgement.
- Self-reported confidence in their own work has declined since adoption.
- They struggle to make decisions without AI involvement, even for tasks they handled comfortably before.

**Probe:** *"If AI were unavailable for a week, what would you do differently?"* An S6D person will express more anxiety about this than the work difficulty actually warrants.

**What to watch:** changes in self-confidence over time relative to AI adoption. Does the person seek AI for low-stakes tasks they previously handled autonomously without noticing?

### S6R Driven Overuse

- The person uses AI for everything, but with an efficiency framing, not an excitement framing.
- They describe AI as something they "have to" use to stay competitive or meet targets.
- Their AI use tracks productivity targets — more use when targets are high or tracked, less when not.
- Goals shift: the AI efficiency goal is crowding out the original work goal.

**Probe:** *"If performance metrics stopped tracking AI use tomorrow, how would your usage change?"* An S6R person will often admit it would drop significantly, or reveal that they find the current pace unsustainable.

**What to watch:** goal-switching frequency, and usage against productivity reporting cycles.

### S6S Social Overuse

- Adoption closely tracks team or peer adoption.
- The person cannot articulate a personal case for their level of AI use — they describe what their team is doing.
- They express discomfort or anxiety about being seen as "behind" rather than about the work itself.

**Probe:** *"What would you do with AI if you were working on this entirely alone, with no one watching?"* An S6S person's hypothetical answer will often differ significantly from their observed behavior.

**What to watch:** whether individual adoption tracks team adoption or individual usage patterns.

### General S6 observation principles

1. **Volume alone does not distinguish.** All four S6 states can produce similar usage volume. The driver is what differs.
2. **Ask about affect, not behavior.** *"How do you feel about your AI use right now?"* surfaces the emotional register (energised, anxious, driven, compliant) faster than observing behavior.
3. **Look for what would change the behavior.** Remove the variable reward (S6E), remove the productivity tracking (S6R), change the social context (S6S), rebuild independent judgement (S6D). If removing that thing would change the behavior, the driver is identified.
4. **S6D is the hardest to observe because the person is least likely to report it.** They will not say "I no longer trust my own judgement." They will say "AI is just faster." Watch for the anxiety underneath.

## S3B Bargaining versus S7M Maturity in practice

Both look like "uses AI for some things, not others" in a single conversation.

**S3B signal:** ask about a domain they have walled off. Then describe a hypothetical model release that gets noticeably better at that domain. Watch the reaction. If the boundary moves quickly under the hypothetical without the person testing it first — S3B is likely.

**S7M signal:** the same person will say *"I'd need to test that before changing how I use it."* The boundary is held on evidence, not on identity.

The cleaner observation: ask which tasks they *won't* delegate to AI and why. If the reason maps to their professional identity domain (*"that's my core craft"*), the boundary is identity-drawn. If the reason maps to AI's reliability (*"it hallucinates too much in this context"*), the boundary is competence-drawn.

## S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance

Both look like careful AI evaluation.

**S2T signal:** the person updates their view based on both positive and negative AI outputs. They can describe times AI impressed them *and* times it failed. Their stated view of AI has moved since they started testing.

**S2D signal:** the person focuses on failures. When a capability is mentioned, they know an exception. When AI succeeds, they attribute it to their prompting skill or to a low-stakes task. Their stated view of AI has not moved despite significant testing.

**Cleanest probe:** *"Tell me about a time AI did something that made you reconsider your view."* An S2T person will have an answer. An S2D person will either not have one or will describe something that confirmed their negative view.

## S2P versus S2D dressed in ethical language

See [states/S2P-pre-emptive-non-adoption.md](states/S2P-pre-emptive-non-adoption.md) for the full diagnostic frame. Three signals over time, none reliable on a single observation:

1. **Cross-domain consistency.** Does the objection apply across domains, including those where identity is not at stake?
2. **Evidence response.** Does the position hold when the stated concern is demonstrably addressed?
3. **Emotional register.** Anxiety underneath suggests S2D. Moral conviction or contempt suggests S2P.

Most real cases are mixed. Use the frame to understand the mixture, not to classify it.

## Common manager mistakes by state

A short table.

| State | What not to do |
| --- | --- |
| S1 Initial Encounter | Skip with a mandate; force a stance before appraisal |
| S2T Trust Evaluation | Interrupt the testing with another mandate or tool change |
| S2D Defensive Resistance | Argue with positive evidence; pressure to "see the value" |
| S3E Ego Shock | Bring data; reassure the person they are still good at their job; rush them |
| S3B Bargaining | Push them to expand AI use; treat their boundary as a personality flaw |
| S3X Structural Displacement | Apply identity-work interventions to a structural problem |
| S5 Understanding | Add metrics that turn maturity into S6R; promote without checking S3E history |
| S6E Enthusiastic Overuse | Reward output volume; let leaderboards run; promote the loudest voice |
| S6D Dependent Overuse | Praise AI-augmented output without acknowledging the person's contribution |
| S6R Driven Overuse | Add productivity tracking; raise the efficiency target |
| S6S Social Overuse | Make AI use publicly visible; name and shame non-users |
| S7M Maturity | Move their boundary by managerial decree |
| S7I Identity Expansion | Take credit for the new identity; treat the AI dependency as a weakness |
| S7E Ethical Integration | Dismiss the ethical reflection as overthinking; force them into evangelism |
| S7B Burnout | Treat the withdrawal as failure; force re-engagement before recovery |
| S7V Evangelism | Burn them out by making them the only voice |
| S2P Pre-emptive Non-Adoption | Treat the values position as ignorance; argue capability instead of values |

The general rule: most damage comes from applying the wrong-state intervention.

## Observation limits

These heuristics are conversation starters, not diagnostic tools. Most real cases are mixed. Use the observation guide to orient a conversation, not to classify someone.

Self-report is contaminated once someone has read the model. If they know what state they are supposed to be in, they will report it. Behavioural observation over multiple weeks is more reliable than a single conversation.
