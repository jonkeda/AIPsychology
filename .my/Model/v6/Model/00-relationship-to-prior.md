# Relationship to Prior Frameworks

This model is an extension of prior frameworks, not a replacement. Knowing what it borrows and what it adds clarifies when to use it instead of, or alongside, its predecessors.

## Technology Acceptance Model (Davis, 1989)

TAM holds that adoption is driven by perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use. It is a snapshot model: it explains whether someone will adopt, not what happens to their psychology over time when adoption is complicated by identity stakes. This model picks up where TAM stops — it describes the psychological arc that follows the initial adoption decision.

## Diffusion of Innovations (Rogers)

Rogers describes how innovations spread through populations over time, from early adopters through late majority and laggards. That is a population-level model. This one is individual-level: it describes one person's trajectory, not a population curve. The two are compatible. Rogers tells the reader where someone sits in the adoption distribution; this model tells them what is happening psychologically to that person at that position.

## Transtheoretical Model / Stages of Change (Prochaska and DiClemente)

TTM describes behavior change as moving through pre-contemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, and maintenance, with relapse possible at any stage. This model borrows the relapse-is-normal framing and the multi-state structure. It differs in that the states here are not motivational stages toward a goal — someone in S3B Bargaining is not "pre-contemplating" S7M Maturity; they are in a legitimate stable position that may persist for years. Progression through the model is not the goal.

## Gartner Hype Cycle

The Hype Cycle describes a technology's trajectory through inflated expectations, disillusionment, and eventually a plateau of productivity. It maps loosely onto this model: S6E Enthusiastic Overuse resembles the Peak of Inflated Expectations, the S3E Ego Shock and S6D Dependent Overuse period resembles the Trough of Disillusionment, and S7M Maturity resembles the Plateau. Gartner is a population-level technology-maturity model; this is an individual-level psychological model. They describe similar shapes from different vantage points.

## V4 and V5 of this model

V5 renamed V4's S1.5 Trust Evaluation and S2 Defensive Resistance to S2T and S2D respectively, added mechanism anchors, refined dropout into two severity axes, and added a limits-of-operationalization section.

V6 makes the following changes against V5:

- Restores the **Mechanism anchors** heading from V5 but changes the epistemological framing: the anchors are **conceptual vocabulary**, not tested causal claims.
- Renames the V5 S6 sub-states for stable mnemonics: S6A → S6E (Enthusiastic), S6B → S6D (Dependent), S6C → S6R (Driven by pressure), S6D → S6S (Social).
- Renames the V5 S7 sub-states: S7A → S7M (Maturity), S7B → S7I (Identity Expansion), S7C → S7E (Ethical Integration), S7D → S7B (Burnout), S7F → S7V (Evangelism).
- Renames bare S3 to S3E (Ego Shock) for consistency.
- Adds four structural elements: an S2T → S2D edge, a direct S3E → S5 edge, a new state S3X (Structural Displacement), and a new state S2P (Pre-emptive Non-Adoption).
- Strips grief / loss / mourning language from S3E Ego Shock.
- Tightens defensiveness scope to identity-threatened domains.
- Spells out state coexistence rules.
- Adds an explicit scope statement, populations file, healthiness frame, usage guide, and observation guide.
- Commits explicitly to a descriptive stance. Testability paths are signposted, not claimed as achieved.

V6 does **not** add transition probabilities, dwell times, or any numerical intensity formula.
