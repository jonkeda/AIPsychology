## Q1 — Reality vs measurability

**You're right; the two reviews drift between these without flagging it.** Some examples from my own text:

- Saying S2 vs S1.5 has "no observable test in a single encounter" is a *measurability* statement. It doesn't mean the two states are the same; it means I can't tell them apart with a snapshot. They might still be genuinely different states in the person's head.
- Saying "the binary dropout split is wrong" is closer to a *reality* claim — I'm asserting most real dropouts are mixed. That's a claim about what exists, not just about what we can see.
- Saying the S5 dual-component stability claim is "unfalsifiable" is mostly a measurability claim dressed as a reality claim. The thing might be true; I just have no instrument.

**Implication for the model and reviews.** A state shouldn't be *deleted* because it's hard to measure. It should be *marked*. Specifically:

- Keep states that are conceptually distinct even if current measurement is weak.
- Tag each state with a measurability grade: snapshot-observable / longitudinal / self-report-only / not-currently-measurable.
- Don't promote unmeasurable states into "falsifiable" claims — that's the V4 mistake.

This is a real fix. I'll add it as a recommendation in the V5 plan (Section 8 below) rather than treating the falsifiability review as if measurement difficulty equals nonexistence.

## Q2 — Creeping complexity

**Yes, the model is getting heavier with each revision.** Headline trajectory:

| Version | New constructs added | Net direction |
|---|---|---|
| V2 → V3 | Trust Evaluation stage, Bargaining stage, Delegation Depth axis, External Trigger layer, Social Context layer, Practical/Emotional dropout split, Influence/Evangelism end state, Cultural Variability section, Subtype 6D | Bigger |
| V3 → V4 | "States" framing, S1.5 promoted, S6 split into 4 first-class states, Identity Stakes axis, Task Delegation axis (renamed), Falsifiability markers, Adoption Ceiling (optional), Removed 7E | Bigger |
| V4 → V5 (proposed) | Two-axis dropout, transition probabilities, mechanism anchoring, "Limits of operationalization" section, study protocols | Bigger again |

**The risk is real.** A model that explains everything explains nothing. Each construct must earn its keep in one of three ways:

1. **Explanatory power** — predicts an outcome the simpler model gets wrong.
2. **Operational use** — lets a practitioner intervene differently.
3. **Falsifiability** — makes a prediction that could be wrong.

Anything that does none of these should be cut.

**Specifically on the binary dropout → two-axis proposal:** I should withdraw that as automatic. It only earns its keep if practitioners actually *act* differently on a "60% practical, 40% emotional" reading vs a binary one. If the answer is "no, I still pick the more dominant intervention," then the binary is fine and the axis is overengineering.

**Recommendation: hold the line at V4's complexity. V5 should add no more than one new construct, and only if it removes two.**

## Q3 — Complete first, then dumb down?

**No. That's the wrong direction.**

The "complete it, then simplify" approach has two failure modes:

- **Fossilization.** Once a construct is in writing for a while, it gathers attachment. People defend it. The simplification phase never actually happens.
- **Over-fitting.** A "complete" model fitted to all observed cases describes the past well and predicts the future poorly. Every edge case becomes a new branch.

**The opposite discipline works better:** keep the model as small as possible, and add a construct only when something the model currently can't explain forces it. That's how scientific models actually evolve, and it matches how good software architecture evolves too.

**Concretely for this model:**

- Don't add S1.5 *and* the two-axis dropout *and* mechanism anchoring *and* dynamics in V5. Pick one.
- Use what's missing to drive the next version, not what could be added.
- Resist the urge to "fill out" the model. The cultural variability section already shows the failure mode — it adds words without adding constraints.

**Operational from day one.** Every state in the model should be defensible on the question: *what behavior would I see in a real person that puts them in this state, and not in any other?* If the answer needs a study no one is going to run, the state is research-grade only. That's allowed, but it should be marked, not advertised as if it were practitioner-ready.

## Q4 — Can we guess transition probabilities?

**Short answer: no, and the temptation to is the most dangerous thing about going to V5.**

**Why guessing is worse than blank.** Numbers carry false authority. "S3 → S5: 0.4" reads like data even when it isn't. Once a number is in the model it gets cited, copied, and built upon. Removing it later is harder than not adding it.

**What's actually measurable, and how:**

| Quantity | Measurable how | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Time spent in each state ("dwell time") | Repeated short surveys + interview classification, or log-based proxies (usage telemetry against state markers) | Medium — needs a panel |
| Transition rates (which → which) | Same panel, before/after each model release | Medium |
| Triggering events | Pre-registered diary plus dated workplace/release events | Low if subjects already journal |
| Probability of regression after a release | Telemetry of usage drop in the 2–4 weeks following named release events | Low for orgs with logs |
| Identity Stakes (per domain) | Standard self-report scale (e.g., role-identity centrality, Likert) | Low — questionnaire |
| Dropout classification | Reversibility test (provide a working tool; do they restart?) plus exit interview | Medium |

**What you can honestly include without measurement:** qualitative anchors. "Most," "often," "rare," "uncommon under low stakes" — these are useful and don't pretend to be data. Strong recommendation: V5 may include qualitative frequency anchors, but **no numerical probabilities until a study produces them**.

**On dwell times specifically.** I claimed in the diff review that V4 should add transition probabilities. Withdraw that for V5; promote it to "V6 if data exists." V5 can add **the structure for** transition probabilities — labeled edges with placeholders — without committing to values. That gives future studies a place to plug in numbers without forcing fabricated ones now.
