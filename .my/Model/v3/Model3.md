# The AI Psychological Adaptation Cycle — Version 3

A model of how people psychologically adapt to AI, incorporating identity dynamics, emotional response, usage depth, social context, and external disruption.

Version 3 adds to Version 2:

- A **trust-evaluation phase** for rational (non-defensive) assessment
- A **bargaining phase** where people negotiate partial adoption
- A **delegation-depth axis** that modulates psychological intensity
- **External triggers** (model releases, mandates, media) as phase accelerators and regressors
- A **social dimension** (peer influence, cultural framing, normalization)
- **Practical friction** as a distinct dropout mechanism alongside emotional dropout
- **Influence / Evangelism** as a new end state
- Explicit **cultural variability** acknowledgment

---

## Two Axes

Version 3 operates on two simultaneous axes:

**Vertical axis — Emotional Phase:** The psychological stage a person occupies (Stages 0–7 below).

**Horizontal axis — Delegation Depth:** How much cognitive authority is being handed to AI.

| Delegation Level                     | Example                                   | Psychological Weight                           |
| ------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| **D1. Information**            | "Summarize this article"                  | Low — no identity threat                      |
| **D2. Task execution**         | "Write this email for me"                 | Moderate — skill relevance begins             |
| **D3. Creative delegation**    | "Draft this design / strategy / argument" | High — core identity engaged                  |
| **D4. Autonomous partnership** | "Make decisions with me"                  | Very high — agency and identity merge with AI |

A person's emotional intensity at any stage depends on their delegation depth. Someone at Stage 3 (Ego Shock) using AI at D1 feels mild discomfort. The same person at D3 or D4 feels existential crisis. The phases below interact with this axis throughout.

---

## External Trigger Layer

Phase transitions are not purely internal. **External events** can accelerate, delay, or reverse movement through stages:

- **Model releases** — A capability jump can send someone from Stage 4 back to Stage 2 or 3
- **Workplace mandates** — Forced adoption compresses Stages 0–1 and can skip trust evaluation
- **Media narratives** — "AI will replace you" amplifies Stage 2–3; "AI is your creative partner" accelerates Stage 4–5
- **Peer adoption** — Watching colleagues adopt reduces Stage 1 duration; watching them struggle reinforces it
- **AI failures** — A high-profile hallucination or error can trigger regression from any stage
- **Regulatory changes** — New laws or bans can freeze adoption or force re-evaluation

These triggers are marked with ⚡ where they most commonly intervene in the stages below.

---

## Social Context Layer

Adoption does not happen in isolation. Three social forces shape phase transitions:

- **Social contagion** — Peer behavior accelerates or inhibits movement. If your team adopts, your Stage 1 collapses under social pressure. If no one around you uses AI, your Stage 5 feels isolating.
- **Cultural framing** — Techno-optimistic cultures compress the early stages. Cultures with strong craft identity (artisans, academics, writers) amplify Stages 2–3.
- **Normalization** — A social process distinct from individual understanding. A person can reach Stage 4 internally but still feel like an outlier if their environment hasn't normalized AI use. Full stabilization (Stage 7) requires both personal reframing *and* social normalization.

## The Stages

### Stage 0: Pre-Exposure

**"AI is irrelevant to me."**

No meaningful encounter with AI. No emotional reaction. No opinion.

⚡ Collapsed by: workplace mandates, peer adoption, media saturation.

**Path forward:** Exposure triggers either Stage 1 or, for low-identity users, direct movement to Stage 1A.

---

### Stage 1: Dismissal / Minimization

**"AI won't work. It's overhyped."**

A protective stance. People underestimate AI to avoid cognitive and emotional effort.

**Branching:**

- **1A. Low-identity users** — minimal skill-identity attachment. They experience curiosity, not threat. Jump to Stage 4 (Trust Evaluation) or Stage 5 (Understanding) directly. They may never pass through Stages 2, 3, or 3B.
- **1B. High-identity users** — strong attachment to skills AI can replicate. Move into emotional resistance (Stage 2).

⚡ Accelerated by: a compelling demo, a peer's success story, a workplace mandate.
⚡ Reinforced by: a bad first experience, a respected peer's dismissal, anti-AI media.

---

### Stage 2: Defensive Resistance (Fear / Anger / Rationalization)

**"Fine, I'll try it — but I'm going to find flaws."**

An ego-defense phase. People feel threatened, so they nitpick, criticize, downplay strengths, and highlight weaknesses. This is about identity protection, not objective evaluation.

**Transitions:**

- → **Stage 3 (Ego Shock)** — if AI performs surprisingly well and defenses collapse
- → **Stage 3B (Bargaining)** — if defenses hold partially; person negotiates limited adoption
- → **Stage 1 (Dismissal)** — regression if AI performs poorly or is misused

⚡ A new model release or capability demonstration can push someone from Stage 2 directly into Stage 3.

**Dropout risk:** Some people leave here for **practical friction** — not because of identity threat, but because the tool gave bad outputs, broke their workflow, or required too much prompt engineering effort. Practical dropout is distinct from emotional resistance and should not be confused with it.

---

### Stage 3: Ego Shock / Identity Disruption

**"If AI can do this… what does that make me?"**

The emotional core of the model. The intensity scales with delegation depth:

| Delegation Level | Experience at Stage 3                                   |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| D1 (Information) | Mild surprise — "It's useful"                          |
| D2 (Task)        | Discomfort — "It does this faster than me"             |
| D3 (Creative)    | Identity crisis — "It can do what I trained years for" |
| D4 (Autonomous)  | Existential — "What is my role now?"                   |

People may experience anxiety, grief, loss of identity, fear of obsolescence, existential discomfort.

Most common among creatives, knowledge workers, experts, and people with strong skill-based identities.

**This is the most psychologically intense stage.**

⚡ Can be re-triggered from later stages by a model release that leaps past current capabilities, or by watching a junior colleague outperform with AI.

**Transition:** → Stage 3B (Bargaining) or → Stage 5 (Understanding), depending on whether the person can tolerate the full identity confrontation or needs to negotiate first.

---

### Stage 3B: Bargaining (New in V3)

**"I'll use it — but only for X."**

A coping mechanism that delays full identity confrontation. People adopt AI partially while setting boundaries that preserve their sense of competence:

- "I'll use it for first drafts, but I always do the final version."
- "It can handle the boilerplate; the real thinking is mine."
- "I use it for code, not for writing — writing is *me*."
- "I'll let it suggest, but I decide."

Bargaining is **not a failure state**. It's a stabilization strategy. Many people stay here for months or years and are productive. But it is inherently fragile — each time AI crosses one of the self-imposed boundaries, the bargain is renegotiated or collapses into Stage 3.

**Transitions:**

- → **Stage 5 (Understanding)** — gradual acceptance that boundaries are artificial
- → **Stage 3 (Ego Shock)** — a capability jump breaks the bargain
- → **Stage 2 (Defensive Resistance)** — regression if a failure validates the boundary

⚡ New model releases are the primary destabilizer of bargaining. Each capability improvement forces renegotiation.

---

### Stage 4: Trust Evaluation (New in V3)

**"Let me test this carefully before I commit."**

A rational assessment phase distinct from emotional resistance. People deliberately test AI on low-stakes tasks, evaluate reliability, and calibrate expectations.

This stage can be entered from:

- **Stage 1A** (low-identity users who skip the emotional phases)
- **Stage 3B** (after bargaining resolves into openness)
- **Stage 5** (looping back after Understanding to re-evaluate a new tool or model)

Trust Evaluation is characterized by:

- Systematic testing on known problems
- Comparing AI output to personal output
- Assessing consistency and reliability
- Evaluating fit with existing workflow

Not all hesitation is fear. This phase captures the people who are cautious without being threatened — the careful adopters that V2 conflated with emotional resisters.

**Transitions:**

- → **Stage 5 (Understanding)** — trust established
- → **Practical dropout** — tool doesn't meet quality or reliability standards
- → **Stage 2 (Defensive Resistance)** — if testing surfaces a threatening capability

---

### Stage 5: Cognitive Reframing / Understanding

**"It's powerful, but it's still just a tool. I can work with this."**

The emotional fog clears. People understand limitations, see where human judgment matters, learn effective prompting, and integrate AI into their mental model of work.

This is the first **stable** phase — but stability requires both:

- **Internal reframing** — "I understand what AI is and isn't"
- **Social normalization** — "People around me use this; it's unremarkable"

If internal reframing outpaces social normalization, people may feel like outliers despite personal comfort. If social normalization outpaces internal reframing (e.g., a workplace mandate), people may use AI compliantly without genuine understanding.

**Transitions:**

- → **Stage 6 (Overcompensation)** — enthusiasm tips into overuse
- → **Stage 7 (Stabilization)** — direct path for measured adopters
- → **Stage 3 (Ego Shock)** — regression via external trigger

⚡ A model release or capability demonstration can reset understanding and send people back to earlier stages.

---

### Stage 6: Overcompensation / Overreliance

**"This is incredible — I want to use it for everything."**

The pendulum swings past balanced use. This phase has multiple subtypes with distinct psychological drivers:

**6A. Enthusiastic Overuse** — Dopamine-driven experimentation. Feels like a creative superpower. The person is energized, productive, and uncritical.

**6B. Anxiety-Driven Dependence** — "I don't trust my own abilities anymore." The person uses AI as a crutch because ego shock (Stage 3) eroded confidence.

**6C. Efficiency Obsession** — "I must automate everything." Driven by productivity pressure, not emotion. Common in high-performance work cultures.

**6D. Social Overcompensation** (New in V3) — "Everyone else is using it; I need to keep up." Driven by peer pressure and fear of being left behind, not personal enthusiasm.

This phase is not inherently destructive — but it is unstable.

**Transitions:**

- → **Stage 7 (Stabilization)** — the pendulum settles
- → **Stage 7D (Burnout)** — overuse exhausts
- → **Stage 3 (Ego Shock)** — overreliance reveals a dependency that triggers new identity questions

---

### Stage 7: Stabilization — Multiple End States

People eventually settle into a long-term pattern. These end states are not mutually exclusive — a person can occupy more than one simultaneously, and can shift between them over time.

**7A. Maturity (Healthy Integration)**

"I know when to use AI and when not to."

Balanced, thoughtful, sustainable. The person has clear boundaries based on understanding, not fear.

**7B. Identity Expansion**

"AI extends what I can be."

People develop new creative or cognitive identities with AI as a partner. They do things they couldn't do before and define themselves partly through this expanded capability.

**7C. Ethical / Philosophical Integration**

"What does AI mean for society, creativity, or humanity?"

A reflective, meaning-making stage. The person thinks about bias, intellectual property, creative authenticity, and the societal implications of widespread AI adoption.

**7D. Burnout / Withdrawal**

"I need a break from this."

Overuse or sustained intensity leads to exhaustion. The person steps back — temporarily or permanently. This is a legitimate outcome, not a failure.

**7E. Regression Loop**

The cycle is not linear — it loops. A person in any stable end state can be pushed back to earlier stages by:

- A major model release (external trigger)
- A capability jump that breaks their mental model
- A high-profile AI failure that re-triggers fear
- A workplace change (new mandate, new role, new peers)
- A personal experience (AI outperforms them on something they valued)

Regression is normal, not pathological. Most people cycle through the model more than once as AI evolves.

**7F. Influence / Evangelism** (New in V3)

"Let me show you what this can do."

The person moves beyond personal use into shaping others' adoption. They teach, mentor, advocate, write about AI, lead organizational adoption, or build communities. This is a distinct trajectory — not just Maturity with a platform, but a different relationship where the person's identity becomes partly defined by their role as an AI guide.

Common among people who passed through ego shock and came out the other side — their experience of the full cycle makes them effective at helping others through it.

---

## Dropout Mechanisms (New in V3)

People can exit the cycle at any stage. V3 distinguishes two fundamentally different dropout types:

### Emotional Dropout

Driven by identity threat, ego shock, fear, or burnout. The person stops using AI because it feels psychologically unsafe.

- Most common at Stages 2, 3, and 7D
- Can be mitigated by social support, gradual exposure, and low-stakes initial tasks
- Often temporary — people re-enter when pressure or curiosity resurfaces

### Practical Dropout

Driven by tool failures, workflow friction, bad outputs, prompt engineering fatigue, or cost. The person stops using AI because it doesn't work well enough.

- Most common at Stages 2, 4, and 6
- Not an emotional event — it's a usability judgment
- Can be mitigated by better tools, better training, and better defaults
- Often permanent unless the tool improves significantly

Confusing these two types leads to wrong interventions: you can't fix practical dropout with psychological support, and you can't fix emotional dropout with better documentation.

## Cultural Variability (New in V3)

The model's emotional intensity and phase sequencing vary across cultural contexts:

- **High craft-identity cultures** (artisans, academics, literary traditions) — amplified Stages 2, 3, and 3B. Bargaining may be prolonged.
- **Techno-optimistic cultures** — compressed Stages 0–2. People reach Understanding faster but may hit Overcompensation harder.
- **Collectivist cultures** — social dimension is stronger. Peer adoption and normalization carry more weight than individual reframing. Stage 5's social component dominates.
- **Hierarchical work cultures** — workplace mandates overrid

- **Economic-anxiety cultures** — Stage 3 (Ego Shock) is primarily about job loss, not identity. The existential flavor differs.

The model's core sequence holds across cultures, but the **intensity, duration, and dominant flavor** of each stage shifts.
