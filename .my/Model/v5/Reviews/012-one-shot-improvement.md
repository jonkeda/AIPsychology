# One-Shot Improvement — Model V5

## TL;DR

- Consolidate the four S6 overuse states (S6A Enthusiastic Overuse, S6B Dependent Overuse, S6C Driven Overuse, S6D Social Overuse) into a single S6 state, and add explicit transition markers for S6→S7A Maturity.
- The four variants collapse to one root mechanism (resource depletion); the distinction obscures what matters.
- V5 leaves practitioners blind at the highest-risk point: they see someone burning out but the model offers no framework for intervention.
- The model's biggest leverage point is the transition from unsustainable engagement (S6) into either stability (S7A Maturity) or burnout (S7D Burnout). V5 fragments S6 into four sub-states but leaves the exit criteria silent. Consolidating S6 and adding behavioral markers for S6→S7A Maturity would make the model prescriptive at the exact point where intervention is possible.

## Findings

1. The four S6 variants collapse to one mechanism (resource depletion), yet the distinction creates false **precision** and makes it harder for practitioners to identify where someone actually is.

2. V5 is **silent** on exiting S6 into stability (S7A Maturity) versus burnout (S7D Burnout) — the highest-leverage intervention point in the model.

3. Practitioners must distinguish four S6 states that all collapse to one mechanism, creating unnecessary **cognitive load**.

4. The Social Context layer (social contagion, cultural framing, normalization) is **undersized** relative to its importance in shaping transition rates and S5 Understanding stability.

5. The model lacks a **dropout feedback loop**: when multiple people drop out for similar reasons, what organizational responses prevent the next cohort from dropping?

6. S3B Bargaining exit criteria are not **operationalized** with behavioral markers practitioners can observe.

7. The "Limits of operationalization" section admits what is untestable but offers **no alternative**. Practitioners need specific observables or questions to place someone in S3B Bargaining versus S7A Maturity.

## Recommendations

1. **Consolidate S6 and add exit markers.** Merge the four S6 variants into "S6 — Unsustainable Engagement," unified under resource depletion. Add a subsection "S6 Exit Markers — When S6 Becomes S7A Maturity vs. S7D Burnout" with three observable behavioral signals that predict which end state someone lands in. For example: "rejects new use cases without testing them," "rebuilds independent judgment on familiar tasks," "reports that pausing AI use no longer feels threatening." This single change addresses findings 1, 2, and 3 simultaneously.

2. **Develop the Social Context layer.** Expand the three-bullet list into a full sub-model with explicit causal chains showing how organizational or peer-group context shapes entry rates and stability of S5 Understanding.

3. **Add a dropout feedback loop.** When multiple people drop out for similar reasons, specify what organizational responses prevent the next cohort from dropping. Dropout clusters by reason; the model should explain how to learn from patterns to change the field conditions that caused them.

4. **Operationalize S3B Bargaining exit criteria.** Replace the current heuristic with observable behavioral markers — for example, "person runs the same benchmark test twice" or "person explicitly documents the scope boundary." Specify what practitioners should observe or ask when distinguishing S3B Bargaining from S7A Maturity.

5. **Replace "Limits of operationalization"** with a practical measurement guide. For each snapshot-observable and longitudinal-observable claim, specify what to measure or ask.
