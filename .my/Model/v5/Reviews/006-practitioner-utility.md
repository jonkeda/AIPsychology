# Practitioner Utility Review — Model V5

## TL;DR

- You can identify only S0 Baseline, S6A Enthusiastic Overuse, and D-levels from a single conversation. Everything else needs longitudinal data or direct self-report.
- You can influence S1 Initial Encounter → S2T Trust Evaluation/S2D Defensive Resistance (by controlling Identity Stakes framing), S2D Defensive Resistance → S3B Bargaining (by helping them set boundaries), and the four S6 states (by changing environmental drivers: remove metrics, rebuild confidence, revisit goals, shift peer context).
- The mechanism anchors help only for S3 Ego Shock and S6. Below S3 Ego Shock they describe brain hardware you can't move. For S7 end states they explain nothing actionable.
- The two-axis dropout (0–10 practical / emotional) is less overhead than it looks. The reversibility test is the only operationalization in the model. The graded reading cuts decision time roughly in half versus V4's binary guess.
- The reversibility test is executable as a diagnostic question in a 1:1, not as a controlled experiment. Five minutes, high signal.
- Biggest gap: no intake protocol, no state-identification flowchart, no scripts for interventions in S3B Bargaining or S6A Enthusiastic Overuse/B/C/D, no way to assess Identity Stakes in a conversation without 20 minutes of probing.
- V5 is a working theory that is not yet a working tool. The model explains the landscape clearly and the mechanism anchors make intervention different for S3 Ego Shock and S6. The two-axis dropout and reversibility test are real improvements over V4. But you still cannot diagnose someone's state in five minutes, and you cannot hand it to a manager and expect them to use it without a diagnostic protocol. Use it Monday morning as a frame for thinking about blockers and as a checklist for what questions to ask. Do not use it as a diagnostic instrument without adding the missing intake questions and decision trees.

## Findings

### 1. Which states can I reliably identify from one conversation?

Three, maybe four: S0 Baseline, S6A Enthusiastic Overuse, S6B Dependent Overuse, and D-level.

- **S0 Baseline versus non-S0 Baseline**: five seconds. "Do you use AI?"
- **S6A Enthusiastic Overuse versus S7D Burnout**: two minutes of usage questions. Pulling AI into everything with energy = S6A Enthusiastic Overuse. Stopped almost completely = S7D Burnout. The volume gap is huge.
- **D-level**: observable in real time. "Summarize this" = D1. "Help me decide between two strategies" = D3–D4.

Everything else is opaque or requires longevity:

- **S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance**: Both look like testing. The difference is asymmetric updating — they dismiss positive evidence and overweight negatives. Visible across three or four tool tries over weeks, not in one conversation. "It made a typo, so it is unreliable" while ignoring four hours saved is S2D Defensive Resistance flavor, but you can't confirm in one shot.
- **S1 Initial Encounter versus S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance**: You can separate S1 Initial Encounter (first encounter) temporally, but in a single conversation you are guessing.
- **S3 Ego Shock versus S5 Understanding**: Both will say "I get it now." One is panicked and recalibrating. One has integrated. You can surface this with "How does this change your role?" but the internal state is self-report only.
- **S7A Maturity versus S7B Identity Expansion**: S7A Maturity says "I use it for X, not for Y." S7B Identity Expansion says "I use it for X and now I can do things I couldn't before, which is part of who I am now." You need: "Has using AI changed what you think you are capable of?"
- **S7C Ethical Integration**: They have to tell you explicitly. "I am thinking a lot about what AI means for creativity" or "I am worried about what this does to junior people." Without that cue, no signal.

The model is honest about this. The Limits of operationalization section says snapshot-observable states are three (S0 Baseline versus non-S0 Baseline, S6A Enthusiastic Overuse versus S7D Burnout, D-level). Plan for 2–3 check-in conversations before you are confident about S3 Ego Shock versus S5 Understanding or S3B Bargaining versus S7A Maturity. You are triangulating, not diagnosing.

### 2. Which transitions can I actually influence?

Five buckets:

**S1 Initial Encounter → S2T Trust Evaluation or S2D Defensive Resistance.** You cannot change Identity Stakes (a property of how much the person cares about the domain). You can control framing before the person forms their initial stance. Present AI as "a tool for things you don't want to do anyway" and low-stakes people lean S2T Trust Evaluation. Present it as "capability in your craft domain" and high-stakes people lean S2D Defensive Resistance faster. Slow the S2D Defensive Resistance transition by separating tool demo from capability threat ("Here is what this does operationally" versus "Here is what this means for your role"), giving them early evidence of control, and naming the discomfort early ("This might feel identity-threatening; that is normal").

**S2D Defensive Resistance → S3B Bargaining.** You can help someone set boundaries that feel stable. Ask "What would you be willing to try it for?" and help them define a specific, measurable scope. The mechanism is cognitive dissonance reduction (managing the discomfort of contradictory beliefs by compartmentalizing) — they can hold "AI is powerful but I control how much of my domain it touches." Works best when you help them pick a domain where AI is genuinely useful but not identity-central, you commit to not expanding scope without their agreement, and you check back after three model releases because the bargain destabilizes when capability crosses their boundary.

**S2D Defensive Resistance or S3B Bargaining → S5 Understanding.** You cannot force this. You can create conditions. S5 Understanding requires internal reframing (coherent mental model) and social normalization (AI use unremarkable in their context). Accelerate internal reframing by teaching how and when the tool fails, giving them calibration feedback, and walking through a failure together. Affect social normalization by making AI use visible among respected peers (through example, not pressure), treating adoption as "how we work now" without fanfare, and protecting them from public failure while learning.

**S5 Understanding → S6A Enthusiastic Overuse.** Hard to prevent. Short-circuit it by making the reward predictable (standardized templates, known limitations), removing leaderboards or AI-usage metrics, and asking "What problem were you solving when you started using AI so heavily?" If the answer is "none, I was just experimenting," move them to a defined project where AI has a bounded role.

**S5 Understanding or S6 → S3 Ego Shock.** You cannot prevent capability jumps. You can name the regression as normal ("Your mental model broke because the capability shifted. This happens."), support boundary re-evaluation, and help them notice what skills are still theirs.

**S3 Ego Shock, S6B Dependent Overuse, S6C Driven Overuse, S6D Social Overuse → S7A Maturity or S7C Ethical Integration.** The interventions differ by driver, which is why S6 is four parallel states:
- S3 Ego Shock: "What is the core of what you do that AI cannot replicate or should not touch?"
- S6A Enthusiastic Overuse: "Where did you lose judgment? Let us rebuild it."
- S6B Dependent Overuse: "What made you doubt yourself? How do we rebuild that?"
- S6C Driven Overuse: "What were you trying to achieve before efficiency became the goal?"
- S6D Social Overuse: "What does this tool actually help you do?"

**S6A Enthusiastic Overuse → S7B Identity Expansion.** Notice what the person is newly capable of, ask them to teach others (externalize the new skill), integrate the new capability into their role definition.

Most of your leverage is in the S1 Initial Encounter → S2D Defensive Resistance → S3B Bargaining → S5 Understanding path and in S6 management. Almost no leverage in S7 because S7 is stable — your job there is support and mentorship, not intervention.

### 3. Do the mechanism anchors help me intervene differently?

They help for S3 Ego Shock and S6. They do not help for anything else.

Below S3 Ego Shock, the mechanisms are early-stage cognition (novelty appraisal in S1 Initial Encounter, calibration under uncertainty in S2T Trust Evaluation, identity-protection cognition in S2D Defensive Resistance). These describe how the brain decides whether something is relevant and threatening. Knowing S2D Defensive Resistance is identity-protection cognition tells you positive evidence will not convince them — change the frame of what is at stake — but the intervention is framing, not mechanism-specific.

**S3 Ego Shock is where they matter.** Anchored to self-efficacy collapse and terror management. The intervention is not "here is evidence that AI is not as good as you think"; it is "I see you are confronting your own replaceability. That is real discomfort and it does not disappear by argument." Then ask "What is the core of your identity that is not threatened?" The mechanism anchor changes the tone of intervention from "persuade them the threat is overstated" to "help them integrate the threat into a revised self-concept."

**S3B Bargaining is partly mechanism-dependent.** Anchored to dissonance reduction via scope limitation. Help them solve it cleanly: pick a real scope where AI is useful and safe, make scope explicit, do not pressure them to expand it.

**S6 mechanisms are highly actionable.** The four S6 states have different mechanisms and need different interventions:
- **S6A Enthusiastic Overuse** (variable-reward reinforcement): remove unpredictability. Standardize templates. Kill metrics.
- **S6B Dependent Overuse** (self-efficacy displacement): rebuild their judgment. Have them evaluate AI outputs against their own thinking, not against AI outputs.
- **S6C Driven Overuse** (goal-substitution): revisit the superordinate goal. "You wanted more time for thoughtful work, not maximum output. Have you checked whether you got that?"
- **S6D Social Overuse** (normative social influence): change the context or peer reference group.

Without these anchors, you would guess.

**S7 mechanisms are explanatory, not prescriptive.** S7 people are self-regulating; your job is support, not intervention.

### 4. The two-axis dropout — does it change intervention?

Yes, substantially. And the reversibility test makes it executable.

| Scenario | V4 guess | V5 graded | Intervention |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| High practical (8), low emotional (2) | "Practical, give them better tools" | "Yes, tool problem. Urgency: high." | Treat as blocker. Find or build a better tool. Timeline: weeks. |
| Low practical (2), high emotional (8) | "Emotional, psychological support" | "Yes, identity threat. Urgency: exists but slower." | Treat as identity work. No tool will fix this. Timeline: months. |
| High both | "Unclear" | "Mixed. Tool failed in an identity spot." | Fix the tool first. Once it works, emotional severity often drops because the rationalization clears. Then identity work if needed. |
| Low both | "Just not interested" | "Confirmed: not interested." | Do not intervene. |

The reversibility test makes this operational. "You said it kept missing context. If I could get you a version that remembered context, would you try again?" Listen to the answer and tone:
- "Yes, absolutely" — practical-driven.
- "Maybe, but honestly I am just tired of the whole thing" — emotional-driven.
- "I could try but I do not know if it matters" — mixed, leaning emotional.

The continuous scale forces you to be honest about how much is each. It prevents the false choice of "I cannot fix the tool, so it must be emotional." Maybe it is 50/50 and you can fix half of it.

### 5. Is the reversibility test executable in a real workplace?

It is executable as a diagnostic question. It is not executable as a controlled experiment.

Five minutes in a 1:1:
1. "I heard you stopped using ChatGPT because [their cited reason]. What would the tool need to do to make that reason go away?"
2. Listen for "It would need to [specific technical thing]" versus "I do not know, I am just over it" versus "Maybe, but honestly I am burned out."
3. Note the tone. Practical (engineering problem) versus emotional (motivational problem) versus mixed (tired and stuck).
4. Optional: if you have a workaround on hand, offer it only if practical severity was driving the exit.

Real obstacles: people often have no clear cited reason ("It just did not work for me"); time has passed; they may not want to re-engage. The test is a triage question, not a controlled experiment. Use it.

### 6. What is missing to make this a working diagnostic and intervention tool?

In order of urgency:

**1. Intake protocol (CRITICAL).** A short structured conversation to narrow down states. The model gives you state definitions but no intake questions. Suggested decision tree, five minutes:
- "How long have you been using AI for work?" (places them post-S0 Baseline)
- "How much of your core work could AI do?" (signals Identity Stakes without psychoanalyzing)
- "When you tried it, what was the first reaction?" (S1 Initial Encounter flavor)
- "Are you testing it carefully, or nitpicking it?" (S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance)
- "If you use it now, what stops you from using it more?" (S3B Bargaining boundaries versus S5 Understanding maturity versus dropout)

**2. Mechanism-specific intervention scripts (HIGH).** For each of S3 Ego Shock, S3B Bargaining, S6A Enthusiastic Overuse, S6B Dependent Overuse, S6C Driven Overuse, S6D Social Overuse: "If the person is here, ask this." Just good questions. Example for S3 Ego Shock:
- "Help me understand what this threatens about your role."
- "If AI can do X, what can you do that AI cannot?"
- "What is the core of your identity that is separate from this skill?"

Example for S6B Dependent Overuse:
- "When you use AI, do you notice yourself checking its output against your own thinking, or against AI's thinking?"
- "What made you stop trusting your own judgment?"

**3. Identity Stakes assessment shortcut (HIGH).** Three questions, 90 seconds:
- "How many years did you train to get good at this?"
- "When you do this skill, how much of your self-concept is tied to doing it well?"
- "If someone else did this task faster with AI, what would that say about you?"

The third question is the real one. Defensive answer = high stakes. "Nothing, it is just a tool" = low stakes.

**4. Calibration guide for D-level assessment (MEDIUM).**
- D1: "I am using AI to summarize, extract, or organize information I already had."
- D2: "I am delegating a skill execution task but I am reviewing and editing the output."
- D3: "I am asking AI to ideate, strategize, or draft something new that I then refine."
- D4: "I am asking AI to help me decide things, or I am using autonomous AI."

Practitioner question: "For the work you have stopped using AI for, were you mostly delegating specific tasks (D2) or asking it to think with you (D3–D4)?"

**5. S3B Bargaining boundary-setting template (MEDIUM).**
- "What domain or task type would you be willing to try AI for?"
- "Why does this feel safe?"
- "Let us be explicit: you are using AI for [X], not for [Y]. Agreed?"
- "When a new AI release comes out, we check whether it changes what is safe in [X]. Agreed?"
- "Check-in: Does this boundary still feel right?"

**6. Longitudinal tracking for S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance and S3B Bargaining versus S7A Maturity (MEDIUM).**

For S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance: ask the same question twice. First time, listen for openness versus defensiveness. Second time (two weeks later, after they have tried it): did they change their view based on evidence (S2T Trust Evaluation) or find new reasons to dismiss (S2D Defensive Resistance)?

For S3B Bargaining versus S7A Maturity: when a new AI capability crosses their boundary, do they re-evaluate based on evidence ("Let me test this carefully") or move the boundary quickly without testing (S3B Bargaining mode)?

**7. End-state stabilization guide (LOW).**
- S7A Maturity: feedback on calibration. Do not second-guess their boundaries.
- S7B Identity Expansion: celebrate the expansion. Ask them to teach others.
- S7C Ethical Integration: take their ethical questions seriously. Do not dismiss them.
- S7F Evangelism: clear the path for mentoring.

V5 is 80% of a working tool. The missing 20% is the operational part: the questions, the trees, the templates.

## Recommendations

1. **Build the intake protocol first.** Five-minute conversation that narrows states. Prerequisite for everything else.

2. **Add mechanism-specific intervention scripts.** For S3 Ego Shock, S3B Bargaining, S6A Enthusiastic Overuse, S6B Dependent Overuse, S6C Driven Overuse, S6D Social Overuse: the questions to ask and the mechanisms they unlock.

3. **Add Identity Stakes assessment shortcut.** Three questions, 90 seconds.

4. **Clarify D-level definitions with examples.** Add the real-world signal: "What does delegating a design vision look like versus delegating a design task?" (D3 versus D2.)

5. **Add S3B Bargaining boundary-setting template.** Explicit agreement, check-in protocol, re-negotiation trigger. Makes S3B Bargaining usable as a stable holding state.

6. **Document what to track longitudinally.** For practitioners who cannot diagnose S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance or S3B Bargaining versus S7A Maturity in a snapshot, what should they notice over time? When should they re-assess?

7. **Drop or measure the S7F Evangelism prerequisite.** Either gather longitudinal data to test it or reframe as "typically more stable if they have been through S3 Ego Shock; we do not yet know if it is required."

8. **Test the reversibility test.** Use it with 50 actual dropouts and see if the tone-based diagnosis matches the outcome. Report back.
