# Missing Populations and Edge Cases — Plain-Language Version

## What this review is about

The model V5 describes how people react when they meet AI for the first time and have to figure out what to do about it. The story it tells: you have a sense of what you're good at, AI shows up and threatens that, you panic, you negotiate, eventually you settle into a stable relationship with the tool.

That story works for some people. This review lists the people it doesn't work for — groups the model either ignores or quietly assumes don't exist.

## TL;DR

- **AI-native users** (kids and teens). The model says everyone starts with a moment of "wait, this thing threatens who I am." A 16-year-old hasn't decided yet who they are. There's nothing to threaten.
- **Trades and manual work.** The model talks like everyone is a designer or a programmer. An electrician's worry about AI is "will this take my paycheck," not "is this replacing my creative soul."
- **People with cognitive disabilities.** The model assumes you can already do the thing AI does, and now you have to deal with feeling replaced. For someone using AI as a workaround for dyslexia or memory problems, AI isn't competition — it's a ramp into a building they couldn't enter before.
- **Companionship use.** People who talk to AI for emotional support don't fit anywhere in the model. The whole framework assumes you're trying to get work done.
- **AI researchers.** They already know how the tool works. They skip the testing-and-flailing part.
- **People whose income depends on staying human-only.** A bespoke writing service. A high-end tutor. For them, panic isn't a phase to integrate. It's their permanent business reality.
- **People who reject AI on principle before ever using it.** The model only has a path for ethical objections that show up after you've tried the tool.
- **The Adoption Ceiling is missing.** V4 had a concept for "I understand AI fine, I just don't want to use it for *that*." V5 dropped it. That gap matters.

## Findings

### 1. AI-native users (people who first meet AI before age 18)

The model insists on a starting moment called Initial Encounter — the first time AI feels new and threatening. A teenager who's grown up with AI doesn't have that moment. A 16-year-old hasn't yet decided that writing or coding is a core part of who they are. When they use AI to write something, there's no Ego Shock (the panicky reaction when a tool starts doing the thing you thought made you *you*). There's no "you" yet, not in that domain.

The model says threat causes shutdown. This user has nothing to defend, so they skip the shock entirely and may land directly in a stable, comfortable place without ever going through the painful middle.

**Fix:** Add an alternate path for users who meet AI before they've formed a domain identity. Send them straight to Understanding (the calm state where you know what the tool does and doesn't do) or Identity Expansion. Or just say plainly that the model only describes adults who already have a domain identity.

### 2. Non-knowledge work (trades, manual labor, service)

The model uses knowledge-worker vocabulary throughout: skill, craft, expertise, domain. Those words fit a designer or a programmer. An electrician has skills too — they're physical, hands-on, diagnostic. The model's Task Delegation framework (information lookup, task execution, creative work, autonomous partnership) assumes the worker is the one analyzing, deciding, or creating.

Here's the split the model doesn't make: in knowledge work, AI shock lands as identity threat ("the thing I was good at is being copied"). In trades, it lands as economic threat ("I'm going to be unemployed"). Those are different problems with different shapes. The model treats them as one.

**Fix:** Either define a separate Task Delegation frame for non-knowledge work, or add a note explaining how the levels and stakes map differently. At minimum, flag that shock in these domains is about money, not identity.

### 3. People with cognitive disabilities

The model assumes every user can take in new information, fold it into their existing mental model, and accurately judge their own limits. Neither assumption holds for everyone.

A user with an intellectual disability, a working-memory deficit, or a processing-speed impairment might use AI as a prosthetic — to fill a gap, not to compete with an existing skill. A person with dyslexia using AI to clean up their writing isn't in shock. They're using a ramp.

The model assumes all AI use is **capability-matching**: you can already do the thing, and now AI does it too, so you feel replaced. It has no state for **capability-enabling**: AI opens up a domain you couldn't enter on your own.

**Fix:** Add an optional path into Understanding for accessibility use, or say plainly that the model doesn't apply to users for whom AI is assistive technology.

### 4. Emotional and companionship use

The whole model assumes the use case is productivity. Someone using AI as a chatbot friend or virtual mentor doesn't fit any state in the diagram. Identity Stakes don't apply. Task Delegation doesn't map. Shock doesn't trigger. Understanding requires the user to build an accurate mental model of the tool — but if the use is relational, the entire point is *not* to treat it as a tool that needs calibration.

**Fix:** Either say openly that emotional-companionship use is out of scope, or add a separate fork from Initial Encounter that describes what relationship-formation with AI looks like.

### 5. AI researchers

The model assumes users are outsiders to the technology. An AI researcher already has an accurate mental model of what AI can and can't do. They don't spend time testing because they already know the failure modes. They don't enter Defensive Resistance because they're in technical-assessment mode, not ego-defense mode. A researcher might jump straight into Ego Shock, or skip it and land in Ethical Integration.

**Fix:** Add a note that high-expertise users skip Defensive Resistance and may go directly to Shock or Ethical Integration. Or flag that domain expertise reshapes the usual sequence.

### 6. People whose livelihood depends on **not** adopting AI

A skilled service provider whose whole pitch is "human-only" — a bespoke writing service, a high-end designer, a personalized tutor — faces permanent economic threat from AI. For them, shock is not a temporary crisis to integrate and move past. It's the permanent state of their market.

They can't withdraw, because their livelihood is on the line. Bargaining doesn't work because the whole point is they cannot use AI without destroying their business. They stay in Defensive Resistance forever, not because of ego defense, but because economic survival demands it.

**Fix:** Add a note that Defensive Resistance can be a permanent stable end state for users with an economic reason to stay there, and that this is different from identity-defense. Or scope the model explicitly to users who have a real choice about whether to adopt.

### 7. People who reject AI on principle before ever testing it

The model's path for ethical objection runs: Initial Encounter → Defensive Resistance → Shock → Ethical Integration (or drop out). But some users reject AI on principle *before* trying it. They decide it's unethical and choose not to engage at all. Someone who says "I don't use AI because it scraped my copyrighted work" hasn't gone through Defensive Resistance or Shock. The model describes Ethical Integration as something that happens *after* engagement, never before.

**Fix:** Add an explicit path from Initial Encounter directly to Ethical Integration for users with principled objections that come before any tool use.

### 8. The Adoption Ceiling gap

V4 had a concept called the **Adoption Ceiling** — a personal limit on how far a user could go in the model, based on their values, cognitive capacity, or emotional tolerance. V5 dropped it. That creates a blind spot.

Picture a manager at the calm Maturity state, using AI to summarize emails. Low stakes. Comfortable. Now the company mandates AI for hiring decisions and performance reviews. The manager crashes back into Shock — not because they don't understand AI, but because the emotional weight of higher-stakes delegation is more than they've integrated. They understand AI completely. They've simply chosen not to delegate those decisions. The model's "stable at Understanding" state doesn't account for choice or values.

**Fix:** Bring back the Adoption Ceiling, but tie it to the person, not to the tool. A user might have a delegation ceiling of 2 (can't absorb higher-stakes decisions without falling apart) even when they're at Maturity overall. Push them above their ceiling and they regress in a predictable way.

## Recommendations

1. **Say who the model is for.** Put a scope statement at the top of V5. Suggested wording:

> This model describes adaptation of knowledge workers and creatives who encounter AI after building identity in their domain. It applies to users whose first encounter with AI happens in adulthood and whose stakes are identity-based. It does not describe AI-native users, non-knowledge-work domains, emotional-relational use, users with cognitive disabilities requiring AI as assistive technology, users already expert in the technology, or users facing permanent economic threat from adoption.

2. **Bring back the Adoption Ceiling** as a delegation ceiling (level 1, 2, 3, or unbounded) tied to each person, separate from their state:

> A user in Understanding or Maturity is stable only within their delegation ceiling. Forcing delegation beyond their ceiling triggers regression to Shock, even with full understanding. The ceiling is independent of state and may be modified only through sustained skill-building or readiness work.

3. **Add an AI-native pathway** for users whose first real exposure to AI comes before or alongside their formation of domain identity:

> Users whose AI exposure comes before they form domain identity may bypass Initial Encounter and enter Understanding or Identity Expansion directly. No identity threat is present because identity is still forming. This pathway is increasingly common.

4. **Add a non-knowledge-work subsection** explaining how states and delegation work differently outside knowledge work:

> In non-knowledge domains, Task Delegation maps differently: Information-level (diagnostic tools), Task Execution (automation of routine decisions). Creative and Partnership often don't apply. Shock manifests as economic threat (replacement risk) rather than identity threat (skills being replicated), making Bargaining less common and Defensive Resistance more durable.

5. **Add an accessibility / assistive-tech note** to the Understanding state:

> This model assumes the user's baseline capability exists and AI is capability-matching (replicating existing skill). Users relying on AI as assistive technology bypass Shock because the tool provides access, not competition. This use case requires separate analysis.

6. **Mark economic dependence as out of scope.** Add a bullet to the scope statement:

> Users whose livelihood depends on human-only service provision face permanent Shock and cannot exit the model through integration. Economic dropout is the only option. This population is out of scope.

7. **Add a pre-emptive ethical rejection path** from Initial Encounter directly to Ethical Integration:

> Some users perform rapid ethical appraisal at Initial Encounter and move directly to Ethical Integration or non-adoption without testing. This is distinct from post-engagement Ethical Integration and represents ethical commitment prior to capability assessment.
