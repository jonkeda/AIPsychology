# Answers — Model V5

Answers to the questions in [1-Question.md](1-Question.md), grounded in [Model5.md](../Model/v5/Model5.md). Plain-English. Written to be useful, not academic. Where the model is silent or the answer is opinion, that is flagged.

---

## A. Meaning

### A.1 What does the model mean?

The model is a map of how a person's relationship with AI changes over time. It is a directed graph of **states** — labeled snapshots of how someone is currently relating to AI — with rules about how they move between those states.

Read it as three things stacked together:

1. **A vocabulary.** Names for recognizable patterns (Ego Shock, Bargaining, Maturity, Burnout). Once you know the names, you can say "she is in S2D Defensive Resistance about her writing but S5 Understanding about her email" and another reader knows what you mean.
2. **A graph of transitions.** Arrows that describe which states tend to follow which. Not deterministic — these are tendencies, not laws.
3. **Two modulating axes.** Identity Stakes (how much the person's self-image is tied to the skill AI is touching) and Task Delegation Level (how much authority they hand to AI in a single interaction). These two dials change the *intensity* of whatever state the person is in.

What the model is **not**:

- Not a stage theory. People do not march from S1 Initial Encounter to S7 in order. They loop, regress, and occupy multiple states at once.
- Not a diagnosis. There is no test that places someone in a state. It is a frame for thinking, not a clinical instrument.
- Not a value judgment. S7D Burnout (burnout) and S3B Bargaining (bargaining) are not failures. They are legitimate places to be.

### A.2 In which states can people be in at the same time?

The model explicitly allows multi-occupancy in two places:

- **The four S6 overuse states** can co-occur. Common pairs: S6A Enthusiastic Overuse + S6C Driven Overuse in startup cultures (enthusiastic plus driven), S6B Dependent Overuse + S6D Social Overuse in compliance-heavy cultures (dependent plus socially compelled).
- **The S7 end states** can co-occur. Common pairs: S7A Maturity + S7C Ethical Integration (mature use plus reflective stance), S7B Identity Expansion + S7F Evangelism (expanded identity plus mentoring others).

The model also allows different states **across different domains**. The same person can be in S5 Understanding about AI for travel planning and S2D Defensive Resistance about AI for their professional craft. Identity Stakes is per-domain, not per-person.

What the model does **not** explicitly allow (and review 007 flagged this gap):

- A person in S6 and S7 at the same time. The graph implies you exit S6 when you enter S7. Regression can push you back, but you do not occupy both at once.
- S3B Bargaining and S7A Maturity in the **same domain**. Both look like bounded use, but they are different psychologically. The model treats them as alternatives. Across domains, fine.
- S2T Trust Evaluation and S2D Defensive Resistance at the same time in the same domain. They are a fork, not a pair.

If you read the model carefully you will find a few cases where the rule is not stated. Use the principle: same domain plus same time plus same psychological mechanism conflict = pick one. Across domains or across time = both fine.

## B. Healthyness

The model does not use the word "healthy" anywhere. Below is a reading of the model that maps onto a healthy/unhealthy frame, with the caveat that this is interpretation, not direct claim.

### B.1 Which states are healthier?

In rough order from healthier to less healthy (as long-term residences):

1. **S7A — Maturity.** Calibrated, bounded, evidence-driven adoption. The cleanest "healthy" state in the model.
2. **S7B — Identity Expansion** and **S7C — Ethical/Philosophical Integration.** Both are stable end states with growth or reflection built in.
3. **S5 — Understanding.** Functional and stable. Less integrated than S7A Maturity but not pathological.
4. **S3B — Bargaining.** Bounded use that defers full identity confrontation. The model explicitly says this is "not a failure state — many people remain productive here for years."
5. **S7F Evangelism — Influence / Evangelism.** Healthy when grounded in real S3 Ego Shock experience; collapses to S6A Enthusiastic Overuse or S6D Social Overuse flavor when not.
6. **S6A — Enthusiastic Overuse.** Productive but uncritical. Healthy short-term, costly long-term.
7. **S6C — Driven Overuse** and **S6D — Social Overuse.** Sustainable only as long as the external pressure does not exceed personal capacity.
8. **S2D — Defensive Resistance** and **S6B — Dependent Overuse.** Both involve the person fighting their own judgment. Sustainable but corrosive.
9. **S3 — Ego Shock.** Healthy as a transition. Unhealthy as a residence.
10. **S7D — Burnout / Withdrawal.** A legitimate recovery state, not a failure. But living there permanently means the cost outran the value.

### B.2 Which levels of healthiness are there?

The model does not define levels. A reasonable framework, layered onto the model:

- **Level 1: Sustainable.** The person can stay in this state indefinitely without psychological cost. S7A Maturity, S7B Identity Expansion, S7C Ethical Integration, S5 Understanding, S3B Bargaining for many people.
- **Level 2: Sustainable with cost.** The person can stay but pays a tax. S6A Enthusiastic Overuse, S6C Driven Overuse, S6D Social Overuse, S7F Evangelism.
- **Level 3: Transitional only.** Healthy as a passage, harmful as a residence. S3 Ego Shock, S2T Trust Evaluation, S2D Defensive Resistance, S1 Initial Encounter.
- **Level 4: Recovery.** A sign that something else broke. S7D Burnout.

### B.3 Are there healthy states?

Yes. S7A Maturity is the cleanest. S7B Identity Expansion and S7C Ethical Integration are healthy in different flavors (creative expansion, ethical reflection). S5 Understanding is healthy and stable. S3B Bargaining is healthy for the right person in the right phase.

### B.4 What does a healthy state look like?

For an individual, a healthy state has these markers:

- **Calibrated use.** The person knows when AI helps and when it does not, and routes work accordingly.
- **Stable identity.** Their sense of who they are and what they are good at is not under active threat from the tool.
- **No reactive override.** Decisions about whether to use AI come from evidence, not from peer pressure, panic, or the need to defend a self-image.
- **Recoverable from regression.** A new model release can knock them back temporarily, but they have the resources to climb back.
- **Sustainable energy budget.** They are not running so hot that burnout is the next step.

In the model, S7A Maturity is the canonical example. The person has a working internal map of where the tool fails, and they steer around those places.

### B.5 What is a healthy state for an organisation?

The model is about individuals. The organisational reading is interpretation, not direct claim.

A healthy organisation, mapped onto this model, would look like:

- **A wide spread, not a forced cluster.** People are at different states for legitimate reasons. The org tolerates S3B Bargaining for long-tenure experts and S6A Enthusiastic Overuse for new hires without pretending everyone should be in S7A Maturity.
- **Few people stuck in S2D Defensive Resistance, S3 Ego Shock, or S6B Dependent Overuse.** These are corrosive long-term and signal that the org's framing is creating unnecessary identity threat.
- **No incentives that drive S6C Driven Overuse or S6D Social Overuse.** Productivity metrics that count AI use, leaderboards, public AI dashboards — these manufacture S6C Driven Overuse and S6D Social Overuse.
- **Recovery slots available.** People are allowed to be in S7D Burnout without it being career-ending.
- **Real S7F Evangelism voices.** The internal evangelists have actually been through S3 Ego Shock themselves, not just been promoted because they were enthusiastic early.
- **Domain-aware.** The org recognizes that the same person can be in S5 Understanding for one task and S3 Ego Shock for another, and does not force a single stance.

A frequently unhealthy pattern: an org rolls out AI with a mandate, compresses everyone through S0 Baseline→S2D Defensive Resistance→S5 Understanding by force, and ends up with a workforce that looks compliant but is actually full of S2D Defensive Resistance and S3B Bargaining underneath, with a thin layer of loud S6A Enthusiastic Overuse people on top.

## C. Usage

### C.1 What can the model be used for?

- **Recognition.** Naming what is happening to you or someone else. "Oh, I am in S3 Ego Shock about my design work" is a less stuck place than "I just feel terrible about AI."
- **Vocabulary in a 1:1.** A manager and a report can talk about adoption without the conversation collapsing into pro-AI or anti-AI.
- **Mapping interventions to states.** The right thing to do for someone in S3 Ego Shock (sit with the discomfort, find the unthreatened core) is the wrong thing for someone in S6A Enthusiastic Overuse (kill the metrics, slow them down).
- **Triage of dropouts.** The two severity axes plus the reversibility test give you a way to ask "is this fixable with a better tool, or is this identity work?"
- **Anticipating regression.** The model predicts that capability jumps will push S5 Understanding and S7 people back to S3 Ego Shock. Knowing this lets you prepare instead of being surprised.
- **A frame for HR or org-design conversations.** See section H.

### C.2 What can I do with the model?

Concretely, on a Monday morning:

- **Hold a 1:1** and use the states as questions. "When did this last feel okay? What changed?" instead of "what's wrong with you and AI?"
- **Audit your team's distribution.** Roughly, where is everyone? Are people clustered in a worrying state?
- **Write a personal note** about which state you are in for which domain. Useful self-reflection.
- **Spot manipulation.** When someone says "everyone is using AI now," recognize S6D Social Overuse pressure and choose your response.
- **Pace a rollout.** Knowing that compressing S1 Initial Encounter–S2D Defensive Resistance too fast leaves people in undigested S2D Defensive Resistance, plan a rollout that gives space.

### C.3 What can it not be used for?

- **Diagnosis.** There is no validated test that places someone in a state. Snapshot-observable states are limited (review 006 flagged: only S0 Baseline, S6A Enthusiastic Overuse versus S7D Burnout, and D-level are reliably visible in one conversation). Self-report contaminates everything once the subject reads the model.
- **Prediction of who will end up where.** The model has no transition probabilities. It tells you which transitions are *legal*, not which are *likely*.
- **Quantitative measurement.** No formula, no numbers, no scores. The dropout severity axes are 0–10 but the model itself admits inter-rater agreement would be loose (review 007).
- **Universal claims.** Cultural variability is acknowledged as hypothesized, not measured. Treat any claim about "most people" as qualitative.
- **Therapy in the clinical sense.** See section D.
- **Performance management.** Using the model to grade or rank people would be a category error and a source of harm.

### C.4 What is the value, in money, in happiness, or other measures?

The model has not been tested. Any value claim is a guess. Reasonable guesses:

**In money** (organisational):

- **Reduced wasted rollout cost.** If the model helps you avoid forcing S5 Understanding from S2D Defensive Resistance and ending up with an underground S2D Defensive Resistance workforce, you save the cost of the second rollout. Very rough order-of-magnitude: tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars for a mid-sized engineering org.
- **Reduced regretted attrition.** People who quit because of a botched AI rollout cost roughly 6–12 months of their salary to replace. Even a 10% improvement in retention during rollout is real money.
- **Better dropout triage.** The reversibility test, if validated, separates fixable practical dropouts from identity-driven dropouts. Stops you spending tool budget on identity problems.

These are speculation, not measurement.

**In happiness** (individual):

- **The recognition effect.** Having a name for a feeling reduces the feeling's grip. People who can say "I am in S3 Ego Shock" cope better than people who can only say "I feel terrible about AI."
- **Reduced shame.** The model frames S3 Ego Shock, S3B Bargaining, and S7D Burnout as legitimate, not failures. That alone is worth something to people who think their reluctance means they are "behind."
- **Less harmful interventions.** A manager who knows the model is less likely to say the wrong thing to someone in S3 Ego Shock.

**Other measures:**

- **Common vocabulary** for cross-team conversations.
- **Better questions** in research (the studies in review 003 would not exist without the frame).
- **Reduced epistemic loneliness** for people who feel weird about AI in a workplace where everyone is performing enthusiasm.

The honest summary: the model's value is in conversations and decisions, not in dollars saved. Until it is tested it should be treated as a useful frame, not a measured intervention.

## D. Therapy or help

### D.1 How can the model be used for therapy?

Strictly speaking it cannot, because it is not a therapeutic instrument. It can support therapy as a frame:

- A therapist working with someone in distress about AI can use the state vocabulary to organize what the person is reporting.
- The S3 Ego Shock mechanism anchors (self-efficacy collapse, terror management) point at known therapy approaches: identity work, confronting replaceability discomfort, rebuilding domain confidence.
- The S6B Dependent Overuse mechanism (eroded self-trust from over-outsourcing) points at exposure-style work: gradually doing things without AI to rebuild judgment.

The model is not a treatment protocol. It is a map a therapist could use to orient a session.

### D.2 Should it be used for therapy?

Not as a primary instrument. Reasons:

- It has not been clinically validated.
- It conflates ordinary adaptation discomfort (most S3 Ego Shock cases) with clinical-level distress (a minority of cases). Treating ordinary adaptation as a clinical condition pathologizes normal reactions.
- It does not include risk markers — when does S3 Ego Shock distress warrant clinical referral versus self-resolve?
- Practitioners using it as a therapeutic instrument without training would do harm.

It is a reasonable supporting frame for a trained therapist who already understands what the model is and is not.

### D.3 What if it is just a model to relate reality to?

That is the right framing. The model is a map for the territory, not the territory itself. Used this way:

- It is a vocabulary, not a diagnosis.
- It points at recognizable patterns, not deterministic predictions.
- It makes it easier to notice things ("I am in S6C Driven Overuse, that is why I am exhausted") that would otherwise be unnamed.
- It lets two people compare notes about what they are experiencing.

This is the model's strongest use. Most of section C is about this.

### D.4 What is the work of practitioners?

Practitioner here meaning: managers, coaches, HR professionals, internal facilitators, mentors, AI rollout leads. Not therapists.

Their work, mapped to the model:

- **Identifying** which states people in their care are in (with all the caveats in C.3).
- **Choosing the right intervention** for the state. The model claims interventions differ by state — review 006 lists what works and what does not.
- **Avoiding state-wrong interventions.** Trying to argue someone out of S3 Ego Shock with evidence does not work; it makes things worse. Trying to coach an S6A Enthusiastic Overuse person to "be more critical" without removing the variable rewards driving the behavior is shouting at the tide.
- **Pacing rollouts.** Designing the rollout so people have space to move through S2T Trust Evaluation or S2D Defensive Resistance, not skipping them.
- **Naming what is happening.** Sometimes the entire intervention is "this is called Ego Shock; it is normal; here is what it usually looks like."
- **Knowing their limits.** When something looks clinical, they refer to a therapist instead of trying to handle it themselves.

The model gives practitioners a frame and a vocabulary. It does not give them a script.

## F. Testable or descriptive

### F.1 What is the value of it being testable?

If the claims are testable, you can:

- Update the model when reality contradicts it.
- Use it to make falsifiable predictions in a planning context.
- Defend its use in settings where unsupported claims would not be tolerated (research, regulated workplaces, formal education).
- Fund and run studies that improve it.

Testability is what separates a model from a story.

### F.2 What is the value of it being descriptive?

A descriptive model still has value:

- It names things people recognize.
- It organizes scattered observations into a coherent picture.
- It supports conversations and reflection.
- It generates hypotheses that a future testable version could check.

Descriptive models become testable later. That is the usual progression. V5 is mostly descriptive with a few falsifiable corners (S3B Bargaining versus S7A Maturity heuristic, dropout reversibility test, S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance asymmetric updating).

### F.3 How trustworthy is it without being tested?

Modestly. As of V5:

- **Trust the vocabulary.** The states map to recognizable patterns most readers will identify in themselves and others.
- **Trust the rough graph.** The transitions map to plausible trajectories grounded in known psychology (cognitive dissonance, self-efficacy, calibration, identity-protective cognition, generativity).
- **Do not trust specific claims.** "Most people cycle more than once," "S7F Evangelism requires prior S3 Ego Shock," "S5 Understanding needs both internal and social acceptance" are explicitly marked conjecture. The model is honest about this.
- **Do not use it for high-stakes decisions yet.** Hiring, firing, performance reviews, clinical referral. The model has not earned that level of trust.

The model is an organized intuition with literature anchors. That puts it above a personal opinion and below a validated framework.

### F.4 What is the minimum that it should be tested?

Review 003 lists eight specific studies. Reasonable minimum subset before claiming the model is empirically grounded:

1. **Cycling through the model more than once.** 60 people, 18 months. Confirms or refutes the regression claim.
2. **S5 Understanding dual-component stability.** 50 people, 12 months. Tests whether internal coherence and social normalization are both required.
3. **S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance asymmetric updating** as a controlled task. 80 people. Tests the model's most central distinction.
4. **The reversibility test for dropouts.** 50 real cases. Tests whether the practical-versus-emotional split actually predicts what it claims.

Together those would cover the central structure. Cost is roughly $200–250k. Without these, the model is a frame, not a tested claim.

### F.5 How can it be tested that someone is in a given state?

Currently, weakly. Snapshot-observable states (one conversation, no history) are limited:

- **S0 Baseline** (no AI use) — observable directly.
- **S6A Enthusiastic Overuse** versus **S7D Burnout** — observable via volume of AI use.
- **D-level** — observable in real time from what the person is asking AI to do.

Other states need triangulation:

- **S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance** requires observing how the person updates when given evidence over time. The asymmetric-updating signal is real but takes weeks to detect.
- **S3 Ego Shock** is reportable by the person but easily masked. The mechanism anchors (self-efficacy collapse, existential discomfort) are private experiences.
- **S5 Understanding versus S3B Bargaining** requires watching what happens around model releases. Does the boundary move on evidence or on pressure?
- **S7A Maturity versus S3B Bargaining** also requires longitudinal observation across capability jumps.
- **S6A Enthusiastic Overuse, S6B Dependent Overuse, S6C Driven Overuse, S6D Social Overuse** are distinguishable by *driver* (enthusiasm versus anxiety versus pressure versus conformity), which usually requires asking the person and trusting the answer.

The honest answer is that V5 cannot reliably place someone in a state in five minutes. Three or four conversations across weeks gets you closer.

### F.6 What if a state is not easily observable? Is it still valid to exist?

Yes, if:

- It explains observed behavior better than the alternative of not having it.
- It makes a prediction that distinguishes it from neighboring states, even if that prediction needs longitudinal data.
- The mechanism behind it is grounded in known psychology.

S3B Bargaining and S7A Maturity are the canonical example. Both look like "bounded AI use." The model claims they are different states because:

- They have different mechanism anchors (dissonance reduction by scope limitation, versus metacognitive calibration of failure modes).
- They predict different behavior under capability jumps (S3B Bargaining moves boundaries reactively, S7A Maturity moves them on evidence).
- They have different upstream paths (S3B Bargaining comes from S2D Defensive Resistance via S3 Ego Shock, S7A Maturity comes from S5 Understanding via measured experience).

A state that fails all three tests — no observed-behavior payoff, no distinguishing prediction, no mechanism — is a name without content and should be removed (review 011 applies this test to S0 Baseline and recommends deletion).

A state that passes the first and third but fails the second is in a grey zone. It is worth keeping descriptively, but the model should mark it as not currently distinguishable from a neighbor.

The S3B Bargaining versus S7A Maturity pair passes all three. Hard to observe, still valid.

## G. Predictability

### G.1 What can the model predict?

What the model claims to predict (qualitatively, no numbers):

- **Direction of motion under triggers.** A capability jump pushes S5 Understanding and S7 people back toward S3 Ego Shock. A workplace mandate compresses S0 Baseline–S2D Defensive Resistance. Peer adoption shifts can push people from S5 Understanding to S6D Social Overuse.
- **Which interventions are wrong.** Arguing with someone in S2D Defensive Resistance using positive evidence about AI does not work. Removing variable rewards from S6A Enthusiastic Overuse reduces the compulsive behavior. Trying to coach someone out of S3 Ego Shock with evidence is wrong intervention.
- **Failure modes from upstream conditions.** S6C Driven Overuse in productivity-tracked cultures. S6D Social Overuse in compliance-heavy cultures. S6B Dependent Overuse downstream of unresolved S3 Ego Shock.
- **End-state collapse patterns.** S7F Evangelism without prior S3 Ego Shock collapses under pressure. Sustained S6A Enthusiastic Overuse or S6C Driven Overuse runs into S7D Burnout.
- **Where dropouts come from.** Practical-flavored dropouts cluster around D2 task delegation and tool-failure events. Emotional dropouts cluster around high-stakes domains and capability jumps.

What the model does **not** predict:

- Specific people's specific transitions.
- How long someone will stay in a state.
- The probability of any given transition.
- Cultural specifics outside the loose "high-craft-identity versus techno-optimistic" sketch.
- Numerical anything.

### G.2 How can it predict burnout?

Burnout in the model is **S7D Burnout**, reachable from any S6 state and from sustained S7B Identity Expansion or S7F Evangelism intensity. The mechanism anchor is resource depletion under sustained high-effort engagement.

Predictive markers from the model:

- **Sustained S6A Enthusiastic Overuse** without exit to S7A Maturity. Variable-reward engagement is sticky and depletes regulatory resources over time.
- **S6B Dependent Overuse**. Anxiety-driven AI use is more depleting than enthusiastic use because every interaction carries identity threat plus cognitive load.
- **S6C Driven Overuse in tracked-productivity environments.** No natural ceiling on "more efficient." The person runs until they fall over.
- **S6D Social Overuse under persistent peer pressure.** The mismatch between actual interest and required performance is exhausting.
- **High-intensity S7B Identity Expansion or S7F Evangelism sustained without recovery.** Identity expansion and evangelism are high-effort end states that look stable until they are not.
- **Workload spikes layered on any of the above.** Burnout draws from the same regulatory budget as the rest of life.

Practical use: if someone you know is in S6A Enthusiastic Overuse or S6C Driven Overuse and the variable rewards or productivity pressure are not coming off, S7D Burnout is the next state. The model does not say in how many weeks. Watch for energy decline, fewer creative outputs, and rising irritability.

What the model cannot predict:

- A specific date.
- Whether the burnout will be temporary (return to S5 Understanding or S7A Maturity) or permanent (dropout).
- Individual resilience differences.

It is useful as an early-warning frame, not as a forecast.

## H. Organisational use

### H.1 What is the value of the model for a software organisation?

A software org rolls out AI tools, sees uneven adoption, faces resistance from senior engineers, and watches some early enthusiasts burn out. The model gives the org a way to think about what is actually happening:

- The senior engineers are not "anti-AI." They are in S2D Defensive Resistance about their core craft and S5 Understanding or S7A Maturity about peripheral tasks. Same person, two states.
- The early enthusiasts are in S6A Enthusiastic Overuse and trending toward S7D Burnout. Productive now, expensive later.
- The quiet middle is in S3B Bargaining for many tasks. They are productive and stable. Leaving them alone is the right move.
- The forced-mandate compliance is creating a layer of underground S2D Defensive Resistance that will surface later as attrition or sabotaged rollouts.

That diagnosis would be invisible without the model. With the model, the org can plan rollouts, support, and tooling differently for different groups instead of treating "the workforce" as one population.

### H.2 How can it help personnel (individuals)?

For an individual employee:

- **Self-recognition.** "I am in S3 Ego Shock about my design work" is a more useful self-description than "I hate AI now."
- **Permission.** S3B Bargaining and S7D Burnout are legitimate states. Knowing that reduces shame.
- **Vocabulary in feedback.** The person can tell their manager "I am in S2D Defensive Resistance right now and I do not think pushing harder will help" without sounding like they are refusing.
- **Personal pacing.** Recognizing they are running into S6C Driven Overuse lets them slow down before burnout.
- **Domain awareness.** Realizing they are in different states for different tasks lets them prioritize where the real friction is.

### H.3 How can it help managers?

- **Diagnose, do not push.** Ask which state the person is in before deciding what to do.
- **Match intervention to state.** Different states need different help (review 006 lists scripts).
- **Avoid wrong interventions.** Do not bring evidence to an S2D Defensive Resistance conversation. Do not remove tools from an S5 Understanding person to "get them to S3 Ego Shock first." Do not promote an enthusiastic S6A Enthusiastic Overuse person to be the team's AI mentor (S7F Evangelism needs prior S3 Ego Shock).
- **Spot S6 risks.** Variable rewards (S6A Enthusiastic Overuse), anxiety (S6B Dependent Overuse), productivity pressure (S6C Driven Overuse), peer pressure (S6D Social Overuse) all need different management.
- **Plan for regression.** A capability jump will push S5 Understanding and S7 people back. Build that into the cadence.
- **Use the dropout axes.** When someone disengages, ask whether it is practical or emotional before throwing tools at it.

### H.4 How can it help HR?

- **Better rollout design.** Compressing S0 Baseline → S5 Understanding by mandate creates underground S2D Defensive Resistance. HR can flag that pattern and design rollouts that allow movement through S2T Trust Evaluation or S2D Defensive Resistance before insisting on S5 Understanding.
- **Better retention diagnostics.** When AI rollouts cause attrition, HR can ask whether people quit because the tool failed (practical), because they felt threatened (emotional), or because the org's framing forced them through unwanted states (organisational).
- **Better support programs.** Coaching for S3 Ego Shock, recovery time for S7D Burnout, peer groups for S3B Bargaining, structured AI literacy for S2T Trust Evaluation. Different programs for different states.
- **Avoid measuring the wrong thing.** AI usage metrics manufacture S6C Driven Overuse and S6D Social Overuse. If HR is asked to track adoption, the model warns that high adoption does not mean healthy adoption.
- **Identify good internal evangelists.** S7F Evangelism voices grounded in real S3 Ego Shock experience are credible. S6A Enthusiastic Overuse voices promoted to evangelist roles collapse.

### H.5 How can it help with what *not* to do when people are in a certain state?

A short table of common manager mistakes by state:

| State                              | What not to do                                                                                                             |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **S1 Initial Encounter**     | Skip it with a mandate; force a stance before the person has appraised                                                     |
| **S2T Trust Evaluation**     | Interrupt the testing with another mandate or tool change                                                                  |
| **S2D Defensive Resistance** | Argue with positive evidence; pressure the person to "see the value"                                                       |
| **S3 Ego Shock**             | Bring data; reassure the person they are still good at their job (sounds patronising); rush them                           |
| **S3B Bargaining**           | Push them to expand their AI use; take their boundary as a personality flaw                                                |
| **S5 Understanding**         | Add metrics that turn maturity into S6C Driven Overuse; promote them into evangelism without checking S3 Ego Shock history |
| **S6A Enthusiastic Overuse** | Reward output volume; let leaderboards run; promote the loudest voice                                                      |
| **S6B Dependent Overuse**    | Praise the AI-augmented output without acknowledging the person's contribution                                             |
| **S6C Driven Overuse**       | Add productivity tracking; raise the efficiency target                                                                     |
| **S6D Social Overuse**       | Make AI use publicly visible; name and shame non-users                                                                     |
| **S7A Maturity**             | Move their boundary by managerial decree                                                                                   |
| **S7B Identity Expansion**   | Take credit for the new identity; treat the AI dependency as a weakness                                                    |
| **S7C Ethical Integration**  | Dismiss the ethical reflection as "overthinking"; force them into evangelism                                               |
| **S7D Burnout**              | Treat the withdrawal as failure; force re-engagement before recovery                                                       |
| **S7F Evangelism**           | Burn them out by making them the only voice; ignore that S7F Evangelism is high-effort                                     |

The general rule: most damage comes from applying the wrong-state intervention. The model's main organisational value is helping managers stop doing that.

## I. Premise

### I.1 Does the model have the premise that AI is good?

No. The model is neutral about whether AI use is good. It describes how people relate to AI, including legitimate non-use (S0 Baseline, S7C Ethical Integration minimal-use variant, S7D Burnout, dropout). The S3 Ego Shock (Ego Shock) state is the model's most prominent acknowledgment that adapting to AI is psychologically costly.

The model does have a softer assumption: that AI is a meaningful enough technology that adapting to it is a real psychological process worth modeling. That is closer to "AI matters" than "AI is good."

What the model does **not** assume:

- That everyone should use AI.
- That refusing AI is a failure.
- That AI is harmless.
- That the path through the model has a "correct" endpoint.

### I.2 Should everyone be in an S7 end state?

No. The model lists S7 end states as **stable resting places**, not as targets. Several reasons not everyone should aim for S7:

- **S3B Bargaining is also a legitimate long-term residence.** The model says many people remain productive there for years. That is fine.
- **S5 Understanding is stable on its own.** A person who reaches S5 Understanding and stays there is not failing.
- **S7D Burnout is sometimes the right answer.** Permanent withdrawal can be a healthy choice when AI use does not pay off for the person.
- **S7F Evangelism requires prior S3 Ego Shock.** Not everyone has been through S3 Ego Shock (or should be pushed into it just to qualify for S7F Evangelism).
- **S0 Baseline may be valid.** Some domains and roles do not require AI engagement at all. The model lists S0 Baseline mostly for completeness, but the absence of meaningful encounter is not a failure.

Treating "S7" as a goal misses that the model is descriptive, not aspirational. Pushing everyone toward S7A Maturity is itself an intervention that creates S6C Driven Overuse and S6D Social Overuse pressures. The right organisational stance is: let people land where they land, intervene when the state is corrosive (sustained S2D Defensive Resistance, S3 Ego Shock, S6B Dependent Overuse), and respect the rest.

### I.3 What if people do not want to or cannot progress through the states?

The model accommodates both, though imperfectly:

**Do not want to progress.** Many people stop at S3B Bargaining or S5 Understanding and stay there. The model treats this as legitimate. The risk is organisational: if the org forces movement, the person ends up in S2D Defensive Resistance-with-a-mask, which is worse than honest S3B Bargaining.

**Cannot progress.** The model is silent on this and review 005 (missing populations) flagged it. People who genuinely cannot move past a state because of:

- Cognitive disability that makes AI tools unusable.
- Domain mismatch where AI does not help.
- Age, training, or life circumstances that make the cost of adaptation too high.
- Economic precarity that punishes the experimentation S2T Trust Evaluation requires.
- Personal philosophical commitments (some S7C Ethical Integration cases).

The model does not give these people a clear home. They sit between S0 Baseline and dropout, neither of which describes them well. This is a real gap. Practitioners should treat the model as a description of *most* trajectories, not all, and recognize that "stuck" is not always a problem to solve.

For an org, the practical posture is: do not assume every employee will move. Plan for legitimate non-progression. Do not measure adoption as if it is the only variable that matters. Some of the most productive people in the org are in S3B Bargaining for their core domain and will be there for years. That is fine.

## K. State Descriptions

### K.1 What should be added to the descriptions?

Most state descriptions in V5 cover what the state *is*, what it *feels like*, and what its *mechanism* is. What is often missing:

- **Snapshot markers.** What can an outside observer notice in one conversation? Currently rare. Most states describe internal experience without external indicators.
- **Self-recognition prompts.** A short list of "if you find yourself thinking X or doing Y, you may be in this state." Useful for the practical-use case in section C.
- **Common confusions.** Which state does this state look like from outside, and how can the person tell? S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance, S3B Bargaining versus S7A Maturity, S6A Enthusiastic Overuse versus S7B Identity Expansion, S6B Dependent Overuse versus S3 Ego Shock — all of these need explicit "how do I tell" notes.
- **Exit signals.** What does it look like when someone is transitioning out of this state? Currently the model lists destinations but not transition behavior.
- **Failure modes specific to the state.** What goes wrong if someone stays here too long? Already present for S6 and S7D Burnout, missing for S3B Bargaining, S5 Understanding, and the S7 states.
- **Wrong interventions to avoid.** Section H.5 above is a sketch. The state descriptions themselves should each carry one.
- **Sample voice.** A few quoted lines a person in this state typically says out loud. The state intros have one ("AI is not a thing in my life right now"); add three or four per state for recognizability.
- **Edge cases or boundary conditions.** When does this state not apply? A state that "applies always" is suspicious.	

### K.2 How can the state descriptions become meaningful?

Meaningful here means: a reader can use the description to recognize the state in themselves or others.

- **Add concrete examples.** A vignette per state: a named character, a specific situation, what they do, what they say, what they think. The current descriptions are abstract; vignettes make them stick.
- **Add domain variations.** What does S3 Ego Shock look like for a copywriter versus a software architect versus a sales engineer? Same state, different surface.
- **Add "you might also see" lists.** Behavioral markers, language patterns, decision patterns. Things a manager or peer could notice without reading the person's mind.
- **Add a pair of contrasts.** "This state versus the state it is most often confused with." Makes the boundaries operational.
- **Add a recovery or transition sketch.** What does it look like to move out of this state in a healthy way? Most descriptions have transitions listed but not described.
- **Trim the academic reference list.** Mechanism anchors are valuable but currently dominate. The plain reader cares about the experience first, the mechanism second.
- **Consistent structure.** Each state should follow the same layout: voice quote, plain description, mechanism anchor (one sentence), markers, transitions, common confusions, wrong interventions. Currently the structure varies.

If V6 does this for every state, the model goes from "interesting frame for the reader who already gets it" to "diagnostic vocabulary the engineer and the academic can both use." That is the target audience for the model documents (per the model writing-style guide).
