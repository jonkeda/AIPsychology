## A. Organisational

### A.1 What to do if people stay in S2D Defensive Resistance?

First, understand why they are staying. S2D Defensive Resistance is not stubbornness — it is identity-protection cognition. The person is evaluating AI in a way that defends their self-concept, discounting positive evidence and amplifying negative evidence. Pushing harder with evidence or pressure does not work. It confirms the threat.

**Why people stay in S2D Defensive Resistance longer than expected:**

- The domain is high Identity Stakes. The longer someone has trained in a skill, the more identity capital is at stake. A 20-year expert in a craft is slower through S2D Defensive Resistance than a new hire. This is not irrationality — it is proportionate self-protection.
- The org is framing AI as a replacement, not a tool. If the message is "AI will do what you do, faster," high-stakes people have every reason to defend. If the message is "AI can take the parts of your job you hate," the identity threat drops.
- There is no safe experimentation space. S2D Defensive Resistance requires room to try the tool and find flaws. If mistakes are visible, public, or career-relevant, the person will never open up enough to exit the state.
- They have been burned by hype before. People who watched earlier technology waves over-promise are quicker to pattern-match the current threat as manageable, and slower to update when AI actually is different. Their priors are empirically calibrated, not paranoid.

**What works, concretely:**

1. **Change the frame before changing the tool.** Do not present AI as "here is what it can do." Present it as "here is what it is bad at, and here is how you catch it." S2D Defensive Resistance people need to feel like the superior judge, not the threatened party. Let them be the expert evaluator. Confirm their skepticism as useful.

2. **Separate the domain.** Find a task in a low Identity Stakes domain for this person — not their core craft, but something adjacent or administrative. Let them form their first AI habits there. Once the cognitive dissonance (the discomfort from holding contradictory beliefs about AI) is not identity-threatening, their evaluation becomes S2T Trust Evaluation-style (fair) rather than S2D Defensive Resistance-style (defensive).

3. **Give them a win they own.** Find one place where AI genuinely helps them. Not helps the team, helps them. Let them discover it, not be told about it. Self-discovered evidence is not discounted the way presented evidence is.

4. **Name the state, not the person.** In a 1:1, you can say: "I think this might feel threatening because it touches work you are very good at. That is a normal response. We are not asking you to stop being the expert — we are asking you to test whether AI is useful for the parts of the work you do not love." This externalizes the resistance to the situation rather than the person.

5. **Wait for a capability demonstration that is too large to rebut.** The model's ⚡ marker on S2D Defensive Resistance says it is pushed to S3 Ego Shock by "a model release that overruns the current rebuttal." Sometimes the right answer is: let the next model release do the work. You cannot argue someone out of S2D Defensive Resistance. A demonstration they cannot explain away might do it faster than six months of meetings.

6. **Do not measure their AI usage.** Leaderboards, adoption metrics, and productivity dashboards make the identity threat structural. If the org watches and scores who uses AI, S2D Defensive Resistance people will entrench further rather than risk public failure.

**What does not work:**

- Presenting positive evidence about AI capabilities. This is the mechanism of S2D Defensive Resistance — the person discounts it.
- Calling it out as a problem in performance reviews.
- Assigning a champion or buddy who is in S6A Enthusiastic Overuse. Their enthusiasm reads as naïveté to someone in S2D Defensive Resistance and confirms the threat.
- Mandating a timeline for adoption. Mandates compress S0 Baseline → S1 Initial Encounter mechanically, but they cannot force S2D Defensive Resistance → S5 Understanding. They are more likely to produce compliance theatre (the person performs adoption) on top of unchanged S2D Defensive Resistance underneath.

**When to accept it:**

If a person has high Identity Stakes, has been in S2D Defensive Resistance for more than six months, and has a stable performance record, the honest answer may be: let them be in S3B Bargaining. They will use AI for some things, not others, and remain productive. That is a legitimate long-term position. The alternative — forcing someone into S5 Understanding before they are ready — tends to produce S6B Dependent Overuse or S6D Social Overuse on the surface with S2D Defensive Resistance still running underneath.

## B. Questionnaires

### B.1 How can questionnaires reliably be used to find in what state people are?

Questionnaires can support state identification, but cannot do it alone. The limits are real. Here is how to use them without overshooting what they can deliver.

**What questionnaires can do:**

- **Narrow the search.** A short questionnaire can rule out several states and point toward two or three candidates. That is faster than a 30-minute interview starting from scratch.
- **Capture self-report on usage behavior.** "How often do you use AI tools for core work?" and "What was the last task you delegated to AI?" give behavioral data that correlates with D-level (Task Delegation Level) and rough state brackets.
- **Screen for Identity Stakes.** "How central is [domain] to your professional identity?" and "If a colleague used AI to do a task you are known for, how would that feel?" are not precise instruments but they triangulate.
- **Track change over time.** A monthly questionnaire does not diagnose a state at a point in time — but it can detect movement. If someone's answers about AI openness shift between month 3 and month 6, that is a signal worth following up.
- **Surface outliers.** A team questionnaire quickly shows who is at the extremes (very high AI integration, very low AI use with strong negative feeling) and who is in the middle. The extremes are where intervention is most useful.

**For greater reliability:**

- Use behavioral questions, not attitude questions. "How many times did you use AI for a task you considered important this week?" is more reliable than "How do you feel about AI?" Attitude questions are gamed more easily and suffer more from social desirability bias.
- Anchor to specific domains, not AI in general. "For your design work specifically" gives different results from "for any work" because Identity Stakes is per-domain.
- Ask about the past, not intentions. "What did you do?" is more reliable than "What would you do?" or "Do you believe AI is useful?"
- Use the asymmetric-updating signal indirectly. Ask: "Think about a time when AI produced a weak output. How did you explain it?" and "Think about a time when AI produced a strong output. How did you explain it?" S2D Defensive Resistance people attribute weak outputs to the tool's fundamental nature and strong outputs to luck or prompting skill. S2T Trust Evaluation people update in both directions.
- Keep it short and unannounced. Long questionnaires trigger performance anxiety and strategic answers. Five to eight questions, embedded in an existing check-in, produces more honest data.

### B.2 Which states cannot be found by using questionnaires?

Several. The states that require longitudinal observation or internal experience are not reachable by self-report at a point in time.

**States that questionnaires cannot reliably distinguish:**

| State | Why it resists questionnaire identification |
| --- | --- |
| **S3B Bargaining** versus **S7A Maturity** | Both report "I use AI for some things, not others." The difference is how boundaries move under capability pressure — a longitudinal observation, not a snapshot answer. |
| **S2T Trust Evaluation** versus **S2D Defensive Resistance** | Both report "I am testing AI carefully." The difference is asymmetric updating, which only appears across multiple evidence encounters over time. |
| **S6A Enthusiastic Overuse** versus **S7B Identity Expansion** | Both report high use and positive feeling. The difference is whether a new identity has formed, which the person may not be conscious of. |
| **S6B Dependent Overuse** | The person will typically not report "I no longer trust my own judgment." They will report high AI use and frame it positively or neutrally. The anxiety is often invisible in self-report. |
| **S7C Ethical Integration** | The person may report ethical concerns but not distinguish whether these are a passing thought or a stable end state. Co-occurrence with S7A Maturity or S7B Identity Expansion is common but hard to detect without depth. |
| **S6D Social Overuse** | The person will typically report "I use AI because it helps" — the social pressure is not consciously framed as the driver. The true driver only appears if you ask "What would happen if your team stopped using it?" |

**States that questionnaires can detect, with caveats:**

- **S0 Baseline**: trivially detected ("Do you use AI tools for work?").
- **S7D Burnout**: "I have stepped back significantly from AI use in the last three months" is reportable.
- **D-level**: "What types of tasks do you currently use AI for?" with a structured scale.
- **S6A Enthusiastic Overuse**: high volume self-report combined with low critical evaluation self-report.
- **Dropout**: former users can be detected by usage questions.

### B.3 What are the negative parts of questionnaires for this model?

**1. The model describes internal states; questionnaires capture behavior and attitude.**
The states are defined by mechanism (identity-protection cognition, self-efficacy collapse, metacognitive calibration). A questionnaire measures what someone reports about their behavior and feelings, which is a proxy, not the mechanism. You can see the surface, not the engine.

**2. Self-report is contaminated once the subject knows the model.**
The model document itself is the contamination vector. A person who has read Model5 can answer strategically, mapping their self-presentation to the state they want to be seen as in. S7A Maturity is the aspirational state; everyone looks like S7A Maturity on a questionnaire once they know S7A Maturity is the "good" end state. The model explicitly flags this: self-report "is contaminated by self-report once a subject has read this model."

**3. Social desirability bias applies universally.**
People will underreport S2D Defensive Resistance (it sounds stubborn), S6B Dependent Overuse (it sounds weak), S7D Burnout (it sounds like giving up), and S6D Social Overuse (it sounds like conformism). They will overreport S7A Maturity and S7F Evangelism (both sound positive and competent).

**4. Point-in-time questionnaires miss the process.**
The model is a state machine with transitions. A questionnaire sees a snapshot. The clinically relevant things — does this person update fairly across evidence, do their boundaries move reactively or on evidence, is this person trending toward S7D Burnout — are process questions that cannot be answered by a snapshot.

**5. Questionnaires cannot detect the S3 Ego Shock underlying S6B Dependent Overuse or S3B Bargaining.**
S3 Ego Shock is not always visible to the person while they are in it, and even less visible in self-report. Someone in S6B Dependent Overuse because of an unresolved S3 Ego Shock episode will answer high-use, moderately positive questions. The S3 Ego Shock substrate is invisible.

**6. Domain specificity is hard to operationalise across teams.**
Because Identity Stakes is per-domain, a team questionnaire that does not anchor to a specific domain produces average answers across heterogeneous domains. "How do you feel about AI?" averages a person's low-stakes domains (neutral) with their high-stakes domains (defensive) and produces a misleading middle answer.

**What questionnaires should be used as:**
A triage and tracking tool, not a diagnostic instrument. Use them to spot who to talk to in depth, to detect movement over time, and to flag outliers. Do not use them to place people in specific states for HR or management decisions.

## C. AI Coaches

### C.1 How could we design an AI Coach for this model?

An AI coach for this model has one core job: help a person understand which state they are in, what that state means, and what they might do next. It is not therapy. It is a guided reflection tool grounded in the model.

**Design constraints from the model itself:**

- The coach cannot diagnose states from a single conversation. Most states require longitudinal data. The coach should make this explicit rather than pretending to deliver a diagnosis.
- The coach must not reveal state labels too early. Once someone knows "you are in S2D Defensive Resistance," they will react to the label rather than the content. Surface the experience first, the label second.
- The coach should never push toward a specific state as a target. S7A Maturity is not the goal for everyone. S3B Bargaining is legitimate. The coach's job is to support, not to graduate.
- The coach must not be used for performance management data. If the org uses the coach's records to assess employees, the tool becomes untrustworthy and all reflection becomes strategic.

**Conversation structure:**

A session has three phases:

**Phase 1 — Surface the experience.** Open-ended questions about what has been happening with AI recently. Not "what state are you in?" but "tell me about the last time using AI felt wrong" or "what would have to change for you to use it more?" Listen for: emotional register (anxiety versus enthusiasm versus indifference), domain specificity, Identity Stakes signals, evidence-updating patterns.

**Phase 2 — Reflect and name.** Once the experience is on the table, introduce the relevant state vocabulary in plain language. Not "you are in S2D Defensive Resistance" but "what you are describing sounds like identity protection — where using the tool feels like it is threatening something important about your expertise. Does that match?" Let the person accept or reject the framing.

**Phase 3 — Explore options.** Not "here is what you should do" but "here are two or three things people in this situation have found useful — which sounds most relevant to where you are?" Present options from the model's intervention vocabulary (naming the state, finding a low-stakes domain, identifying what AI is bad at, setting explicit boundaries).

**Domain model the coach needs:**

- The state graph with all transitions.
- The mechanism for each state in plain language.
- A set of questions that surface Identity Stakes and D-level without using those terms.
- A decision tree that maps question responses to candidate state brackets (not single states).
- Intervention menus per state bracket.
- A memory layer that persists across sessions, because longitudinal data is required for the most important distinctions.

**Session frequency:**
Monthly check-ins plus on-demand "something happened" sessions triggered by the person. The monthly cadence is what allows the coach to detect movement over time. A one-time session is triage, not coaching.

**What the AI coach should refuse to do:**
- Assign a definitive state label after one session.
- Recommend therapy (refer to a human for that).
- Report conversation content to the org without explicit consent.
- Encourage faster progression through the model.
- Tell someone they "should" be in S7A Maturity.

### C.2 How can we let an LLM identify states reliably?

The question is incomplete in the source file, so this is an answer to the most likely intended question: how to get an LLM to identify which state a person is in from a conversation.

**What the LLM can do well:**

- **Detect surface behavioral markers.** Usage volume, task types, emotional valence in language, whether the person is framing AI as a tool versus a threat versus a crutch.
- **Detect asymmetric updating in real time.** If the person dismisses a positive AI example and latches on to a negative one, the LLM can notice the asymmetry and flag S2D Defensive Resistance-flavor.
- **Detect D-level from task descriptions.** "I ask it to summarize" is D1; "I ask it to draft my strategy docs and I ship them" is D3–D4.
- **Surface Identity Stakes from language.** "My craft," "twenty years of expertise," "what I trained for" are high-stakes signals. "Email summaries," "admin work," "stuff I hate doing" are low-stakes signals.
- **Maintain session context.** Track what the person said in session 1 and compare it to session 3. Has their framing changed? Are boundaries moving?

**What the LLM cannot do reliably:**

- **Distinguish S3B Bargaining from S7A Maturity** without multiple sessions across at least one model release. Both look like "bounded use" in a single conversation.
- **Detect S6B Dependent Overuse** unless the person explicitly reports lost confidence. The anxiety is usually masked.
- **Distinguish S6C Driven Overuse from S6A Enthusiastic Overuse** without asking about the driver, which the person may not be self-aware enough to report accurately.
- **Detect social desirability bias.** The LLM has no baseline for what the person's unperformed self sounds like.

**Practical architecture for state identification:**

Rather than asking "what state is this person in?" ask the LLM to:

1. Classify each person's statements against a set of binary signals: high/low Identity Stakes, high/low usage, positive/negative affect, fair/asymmetric evidence updating, internal/external pressure driver.
2. Accumulate those signals across sessions.
3. Map accumulated signals to a candidate state bracket (not a single state). For example: "high Identity Stakes + low usage + asymmetric updating → candidate: S2D Defensive Resistance or S3B Bargaining."
4. Generate clarifying questions to narrow the bracket further.
5. Never report a state label to the org. Report only to the person, and frame it as a hypothesis, not a diagnosis.

**Calibration and validation:**
The LLM's state assignments should be calibrated against human expert judgments on a dataset of transcripts before deployment. Without calibration data, the LLM will produce confident-sounding labels that are unreliable. Start with a pilot cohort where both a human expert and the LLM classify the same conversations. Measure agreement. Iterate on the question set and classification rules until agreement is acceptable.

A realistic expectation for a well-designed first version: reliable identification of S0 Baseline, S7D Burnout, D-level, and rough Identity Stakes level. Unreliable for S2T Trust Evaluation versus S2D Defensive Resistance, S3B Bargaining versus S7A Maturity, and all S6 sub-states except S6A Enthusiastic Overuse. That is still useful — it narrows the space significantly.
