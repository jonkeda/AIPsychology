## A. States

### A.1 Do S3B Bargaining and S7A Maturity feel different — and how should we change the descriptions?

Yes — the observation is correct, but the cleanest way to describe the difference is not D-level. It is *which domain* the boundary is drawn around.

The model already distinguishes S3B Bargaining (where someone makes a deal with themselves to use AI for some things but not others) from S7A Maturity (bounded adoption with calibrated scope) by *how the boundary moves* — reactive under pressure in S3B, evidence-based in S7A. That distinction is real but incomplete. The missing piece is *where* the boundary sits.

**S3B Bargaining draws the boundary at the identity-threatened domain.**
The whole point of the bargain is to resolve the dissonance between "I am skilled at X" and "AI is also skilled at X." The resolution is: keep AI out of X, let it have everything else. The graphic designer in S3B Bargaining may happily use AI at D3 Creative Delegation to draft a strategy document, while refusing it at D2 Task Execution for anything that touches design. The boundary is identity-shaped, not D-level-shaped. Inside the protected domain, even small uses feel wrong; outside it, even large uses feel fine.

**S7A Maturity draws the boundary at the competence line, regardless of domain.**
The S7A Maturity person has a calibrated model of what AI is good at and what it is bad at. They will use AI inside their core identity domain when AI is reliable enough there, and they will refuse AI outside their core domain when AI is not reliable enough there. The level of identity engagement does not determine the boundary — the quality of AI on the specific task does.

Why the user's intuition that "S3B = lower-level tasks" looks right in practice: identity-threatened domains usually contain the higher-D work for that person (their core craft is what they trained for, which is usually D3 Creative Delegation or D4 Autonomous Partnership work). So S3B people end up with AI confined to their lower-D tasks *because* those happen to be outside the identity domain — not because S3B inherently caps at low D-level. The two correlate, but the operative axis is Identity Stakes.

**The two states feel different from the inside:**

- S3B feels like protecting something important. The restriction has emotional weight — this is the stuff I won't let AI touch.
- S7A feels like routing. The restriction is pragmatic — AI is not good enough at this yet, or the cost of checking its work exceeds the benefit.

**Proposed description changes:**

For S3B Bargaining, add: *The boundary in S3B Bargaining is drawn around the identity-threatened domain. AI is allowed everywhere except where it would touch the skill the person built their self-concept around. Because identity-threatened domains tend to be the person's higher-D work, S3B users often end up using AI mostly for lower-D tasks — but the operative axis is Identity Stakes, not Task Delegation Level.*

For S7A Maturity, add: *Unlike S3B Bargaining, the boundary in S7A Maturity is drawn at the competence line, not at the identity line. The S7A user will use AI inside their core domain when capability evidence supports it, and refuse AI outside their core domain when capability evidence does not. The question is "Is AI reliable enough at this specific task?" not "Does this task feel too important to delegate?"*

### A.3 Economic dependence — people whose livelihood depends on human-only provision

**The model's current position:**
The model explicitly leaves this population out of scope. The Cultural Variability section briefly notes that in "economic-anxiety cultures" S3 Ego Shock is primarily about job loss rather than identity, but it does not model what happens to people for whom AI genuinely eliminates the economic basis of their work — not just threatens it psychologically, but makes it unviable.

**Why the user is right to push back:**
The AI Adaptation Cycle is built on the assumption that integration is possible. The cycle ends at S7 end states — mature use, identity expansion, ethical reflection, evangelism. These all assume the person finds a viable place in a world with AI. For the population whose livelihood depends on human-only provision (a translator whose clients paid for human tone; a paralegal whose work is document review; a medical transcriptionist), the cycle's endpoint is not available. There is no S7A Maturity position where "I use AI for some things and not others" and remain employed. The AI does their job, period.

For this population, S3 Ego Shock is not resolved by S3B Bargaining or S5 Understanding, because the problem is structural, not psychological. A better mental model of AI does not make the economic problem go away.

**What the model gets wrong about this population:**
It conflates two different problems:

1. *Psychological adaptation* — how someone changes their relationship to AI when their professional identity is under threat
2. *Economic displacement* — how someone survives when the economic demand for their skill disappears

The model is a good description of problem 1. It is not a model of problem 2 at all. The risk is that someone reads the model, applies it to an economically displaced worker, and concludes the worker needs psychological support to get to S7A Maturity — when what they actually need is retraining, economic support, and a different job.

**What should happen for this population:**

The adaptation cycle still applies during the transition period. A person losing their livelihood to AI goes through S1 Initial Encounter, S2D Defensive Resistance, and S3 Ego Shock just like everyone else. The cycle describes the psychological experience correctly. What is different is the *viable endpoint*.

For economically displaced workers, the useful endpoints are:

- **Occupational transition** — the skills transfer to adjacent work (the translator becomes an AI output editor; the paralegal moves into legal strategy work AI cannot replicate). This is a form of S7A Maturity in a new domain.
- **Principled non-adoption** — the person leaves the field rather than integrating AI. This is not failure; it is a legitimate resolution.
- **Advocacy and political action** — some displaced workers become activists for regulation, labor protection, or economic redistribution. This is structurally outside the integration cycle entirely — it is a stable non-adoption position with external advocacy attached. It is not S7F Evangelism (which is integration-side cheerleading); it is a different track that the model does not currently describe.

**The user's broader point — all knowledge workers may become economically dependent:**
This is the consequential version of the same question. The strong form ("all knowledge workers will be displaced") is speculative — it assumes AI continues a capability trajectory that has not yet played out, and it ignores that many roles will become AI-augmented rather than AI-replaced. The weaker form is harder to argue with: the share of knowledge work that is genuinely AI-collaborative rather than AI-replaceable is unknown, and the model is silent on what happens if that share is small. A V6 model should:

- Add a structural-displacement track alongside the adaptation track
- Note explicitly that S7A Maturity assumes viable human-AI collaborative work remains in the person's domain — and that this is not guaranteed at the population level
- Be honest about what the model cannot tell you: it cannot predict whether your specific economic position is salvageable. It can only describe how you will experience the process of finding out

**Opinion, flagged:** The current out-of-scope carve-out was probably reasonable when the model was first written. It is less reasonable now. The model should address economic displacement explicitly in V6, even if only to say clearly: "this model describes psychological adaptation; economic displacement is a different problem that requires different interventions."

### A.3.1 Options for V6: how to handle economic dependence

The question is not whether V6 should address this — it should — but *how much* of it should be inside the model versus alongside it. Here are the realistic options, from minimal to maximal, with what each one buys and what it costs.

#### Option 1 — Sharper out-of-scope statement (minimal)

Keep the model focused on psychological adaptation. Add an explicit boundary section at the top:

> This model describes psychological adaptation to AI for people who have a viable economic position in a world with AI. It does not model economic displacement, retraining, or labor-market transition. Readers using this model for displaced workers should treat it as describing the psychological half of their situation only.

**What it buys:** Honesty. Stops the model from being misapplied to people whose problem is structural.
**What it costs:** Nothing structural changes. The model still leaves the most consequential population unaddressed.
**When this is the right choice:** If V6 is meant to be a focused refinement of V5 rather than a scope expansion. Cheap, defensible, immediately useful.

#### Option 2 — Add an "Economic Viability" modulator (light)

Treat economic viability as a third modulator alongside Identity Stakes and Task Delegation Level. Three values: *viable* (the person's role survives in some AI-collaborative form), *contested* (it's unclear whether the role survives), *non-viable* (the role does not survive).

This modulator changes which transitions and end states are reachable:

- *Viable* — full model applies, all S7 end states reachable.
- *Contested* — model applies but S3 Ego Shock is amplified and S3B Bargaining is unstable (the boundary keeps moving because the underlying economic facts are moving).
- *Non-viable* — S5 Understanding and S7A Maturity in the original domain are not reachable. S3 Ego Shock resolves only via occupational transition or principled exit, not via integration.

**What it buys:** A structural way to mark which paths are open without adding new states. Reuses the existing axis machinery.
**What it costs:** Economic viability is hard to assess in real time. Marking someone as *non-viable* is a strong claim and easily gets it wrong. It also makes the model feel deterministic in ways psychological models usually avoid.
**When this is the right choice:** If V6 wants to extend the existing structure rather than add a parallel one. Probably the best ratio of insight to disruption.

Add one new state and one new exit path. The state lives between S3 Ego Shock and the existing dropout, and represents the position of someone who has reached S3 *and* who has concluded the economic position is not recoverable.

Working name: **S3X — Structural Displacement** (the X marks "exit from the integration cycle, for economic rather than psychological reasons").

From S3X, three resolutions are possible:

- → **Occupational transition** (a new domain, possibly entering a new instance of the cycle at S0 Baseline)
- → **Exit from knowledge work** (no further engagement with the cycle)
- → **Adversarial advocacy** (the activist track — stable non-adoption with external advocacy attached)

**What it buys:** Explicit place in the state graph for displaced workers. Makes the cycle complete without claiming everyone integrates.
**What it costs:** Adds a state, which the V5 design philosophy resisted. Requires defining when someone is "in" S3X versus still in S3 Ego Shock — a hard distinction in practice.
**When this is the right choice:** If the model needs to be useful as a coaching or HR instrument that can label the position of every member of a workforce, including those leaving.

#### Option 4 — Companion model: AI Economic Displacement Cycle (maximal)

Write a second document. The existing model stays focused on psychological adaptation; a companion model describes the economic-displacement trajectory (warning signs, transition periods, viability assessment, retraining pathways, advocacy positions). Cross-link them. Most real situations involve both, so practitioners use them together.

**What it buys:** Both problems get the depth they deserve. Neither model gets distorted by trying to cover the other.
**What it costs:** Two models to maintain. Clear scope boundary needed between them. Risk that readers use only one and miss the other half of the picture. Significantly more work.
**When this is the right choice:** If displacement is going to be a major focus and there is appetite for a second document.

#### Option 5 — Reframe the whole model around viability (heavy)

Restructure V6 so the first question is no longer "how do you adapt?" but "is your position viable, contested, or non-viable?", and the adaptation cycle branches off only in the viable and contested cases. Non-viable cases get a different cycle (the displacement track from Option 4 folded in).

**What it buys:** The model leads with the most consequential question. No reader can apply it without first confronting whether their economic position survives.
**What it costs:** Major restructure. Loses the clean psychological focus that V5 worked to establish. Risk of the model becoming about labor economics rather than psychology.
**When this is the right choice:** Probably not now. This is what V7 might do if Option 2 or Option 3 turns out to be insufficient.

#### Recommendation

**Option 1 plus Option 2.** Add the explicit out-of-scope statement at the top so the model cannot be misapplied. Add Economic Viability as a third modulator so the model can describe what changes when viability is contested or absent. This adds one axis, no new states, and keeps the model's psychological focus intact.

Defer Option 3 (the Structural Displacement state) to V7, conditional on the modulator approach proving insufficient. Defer Option 4 (the companion model) until there is an identified audience asking for it. Skip Option 5.

**One specific addition either way:** the model should say plainly that S7A Maturity, S7B Identity Expansion, and S7F Evangelism all assume the existence of viable AI-collaborative work in the person's domain. This is currently silent in V5 and is the load-bearing assumption that the economic-dependence question exposes.

### A.4 Ethical rejection — is it close to S2D Defensive Resistance, and should there be a pre-emptive ethical rejection path?

**On the observation that ethical rejection is close to S2D Defensive Resistance:**

The user is largely right. Most instances of ethical rejection in practice use the same cognitive machinery as S2D Defensive Resistance (where someone uses identity-protection cognition to evaluate AI defensively). The surface features are almost identical:

- Both discount positive evidence about AI capability
- Both focus on AI's failures and harms rather than its benefits
- Both use language that sounds like principled evaluation but functions as motivated reasoning
- Both tend to appear most strongly in people with high Identity Stakes in domains AI can replicate

The Luddite comparison is historically accurate. The original Luddites were not ideologically anti-technology — they were skilled textile workers whose economic position was being destroyed. The ethical framing ("this machinery is destroying livelihoods") was simultaneously true and self-interested. Modern ethical rejection of AI has the same structure: the ethical concerns raised (labor exploitation, bias, environmental cost, authenticity) are real concerns, and they are also frequently more intensely held by people whose professional identity AI threatens than by people with no skin in the game.

The mechanism that makes them look similar: in both S2D Defensive Resistance and identity-driven ethical rejection, the person is protecting a valued self-concept. In S2D Defensive Resistance they protect their professional competence. In identity-driven ethical rejection they protect their identity as an ethical agent — "I am someone who does not exploit workers" or "I am someone who values authentic human work." Both are identity protection. The difference is which part of the identity is under threat.

**Where they genuinely differ:**

Genuine ethical rejection is not S2D Defensive Resistance. It exists. The distinguishing signals are imperfect but real:

1. **Cross-domain consistency.** S2D Defensive Resistance is stronger inside the person's identity domain and absent outside it. Genuine ethical rejection tends to apply across domains — someone who refuses AI on labor-exploitation grounds applies that refusal to their own work *and* to others'. This signal is weak on its own, because people also know their own domain better and will naturally have more articulated views there even when their objection is genuinely ethical. It only becomes diagnostic when combined with the other two signals.
2. **Evidence response.** Show a person in S2D Defensive Resistance an AI that performs their core task perfectly, and the dissonance increases — they need stronger defenses, not weaker ones. Show a genuine ethical objector the same demonstration, and their objection holds steady or strengthens, but for a different reason: the better the AI works, the worse the ethical concern they are raising (more displaced workers, more biased outputs at scale, more authentic-seeming fakes). In both cases capability evidence does not break the position, but the *reason* it doesn't break is different.
3. **Emotional register.** S2D Defensive Resistance has anxiety underneath it — fear of obsolescence. Principled ethical rejection tends to have a different flavor: moral conviction, sometimes grief, sometimes contempt. The threat appraisal is not "this threatens me" but "this threatens something I care about."

None of these signals is reliable on a single observation. They are diagnostic only over time and in combination. In practice most instances are mixed: someone can simultaneously be in S2D Defensive Resistance (protecting their competence) and making genuine ethical arguments that are also true. The mix doesn't make either component invalid.

**On the pre-emptive ethical rejection path:**

The proposal is: add a path from S1 Initial Encounter directly to Ethical Integration (or non-adoption) for people who do rapid ethical appraisal before testing the tool.

**Opinion: yes, this path should be added, but the name and endpoint need care.**

The current model has S7C Ethical / Philosophical Integration as the reflective end state for mature users who remain engaged with AI but think carefully about it. The proposed path is different: it describes people who reach an ethical conclusion at first encounter, without capability testing. That is meaningfully distinct from S7C, which is a destination after extended use.

The proposed path should be distinguished as follows:

- **Pre-testing ethical commitment** — the person appraises AI at first encounter, decides the ethical costs outweigh the benefits for them, and declines to engage further. This is not S7C (which requires use and reflection); it is a clean exit from the cycle at S1.
- The mechanism is different from S2D Defensive Resistance: it is **moral identity prioritization**, not capability-threat defense. The person is not trying to protect their professional skill — they are acting in accordance with their established values.
- It is also distinct from emotional dropout, which is a response to a negative experience. Pre-emptive ethical commitment happens before any negative experience.

**What the model should add:**

A branch from S1 Initial Encounter → *Pre-commitment non-adoption* (working name) for people who:

1. Decline on ethical or values grounds before meaningful capability testing
2. Apply that position consistently across domains, not just in identity-threatened areas
3. Would not change their position if the ethical concern were demonstrably addressed (for example: if someone says "I won't use AI because it exploits training data," and you show them a model trained only on licensed data, and they still won't use it — the stated reason was a proxy for a deeper position)

This is a legitimate and stable outcome. It should be in the model. The model should also note the diagnostic difficulty: from outside, pre-commitment non-adoption and S2D Defensive Resistance using ethical language look almost identical. The distinction only becomes visible over time (consistency across domains, response to addressed objections, emotional register).

**On the proposed quote in the question:**

> Some users perform rapid ethical appraisal at Initial Encounter and move directly to Ethical Integration or non-adoption without testing. This is distinct from post-engagement Ethical Integration and represents ethical commitment prior to capability assessment.

This is accurate. The last sentence — "represents ethical commitment prior to capability assessment" — is the key one. It distinguishes this path from S7C (post-engagement) and from S2D Defensive Resistance (capability-driven defense). Add it. But the destination should be labeled *pre-commitment non-adoption* or similar, not "Ethical Integration" — the latter implies a stable reflective relationship with a tool being used; this path ends without use.
