# Practitioner Utility Review — Model V5

## TL;DR

- You can identify only S0, S6A, and D-levels snapshot. Everything else needs either longitudinal data or what people tell you directly.
- You can influence S1→S2T/S2D (by controlling Identity Stakes framing), S2D→S3B (by helping them set boundaries), and the four S6 states (by changing environmental drivers: remove metrics, rebuild confidence, revisit goals, shift peer context).
- The mechanism anchors help *only* for S3 and S6. Below S3 they describe brain hardware you can't move. For S7 end states they explain nothing actionable.
- The two-axis dropout (0–10 practical / emotional) is less overhead than it looks. The reversibility test is the only operationalization in the model. If you use it, the graded reading cuts decision time in half vs. V4's guessing.
- The reversibility test is executable as a diagnostic question in a 1:1, not as a controlled experiment. Takes five minutes, high signal.
- Biggest gap: no intake protocol, no state-identification flowchart, no scripts for interventions in S3B or S6A/B/C/D, no way to assess Identity Stakes in a conversation without 20 minutes of probing.

## Verdict

V5 is a **working theory that is not yet a working tool**. The model explains the landscape clearly and the mechanism anchors make intervention different for S3 and S6. The two-axis dropout and reversibility test are real improvements over V4. But you still cannot walk into a room with this and diagnose someone's state in the first five minutes, and you cannot hand it to a manager or team lead and expect them to use it without a diagnostic protocol. Use it Monday morning as a *frame* for thinking about blockers and as a checklist for what questions to ask. Do not use it as a diagnostic instrument without adding the intake questions and decision trees that are currently missing.

## Findings

### 1. Which states can I reliably identify in a real person from observable behavior in one conversation?

Three, maybe four: **S0, S6A, S6B, D-level**. That's it.

- **S0 vs. non-S0**: Five seconds. "Do you use AI?" Done.
- **S6A vs. S7D**: Two minutes of usage questions. If they're pulling it into everything with energy, S6A. If they've stopped almost completely, S7D. The volume gap is huge.
- **D-level**: Observable in real time. If they say "summarize this," that's D1. If they're asking AI to help decide between two strategies, that's D3-D4.

Everything else is opaque or requires longevity:

- **S2T vs. S2D**: Both look like "testing." The difference is asymmetric updating — they dismiss positive evidence and overweight negatives. You won't see this in one conversation; you see it across three or four tool tries over weeks. If someone says "Well, it made a typo, so it's unreliable" while ignoring that it saved them four hours, that's S2D flavor, but you can't confirm it.
- **S1 vs. S2T vs. S2D**: You can separate S1 (first encounter) from the others temporally, but in a single conversation? You're guessing. If someone is in their first two weeks with AI, assume they're in S1 or one of the S2s. You won't know which without the next interaction.
- **S3 vs. S5**: Both people will say "I get it now." One is panicked, recalibrating their sense of their own value. One has actually integrated it into their working model. You can surface this with careful questions — "How does this change your role?" — but the internal state is self-report only. They might not even be honest with themselves about it.
- **S7A vs. S7B**: S7A says "I use it for X, not for Y." S7B says "I use it for X and now I can do things I couldn't before, which is part of who I am now." Both look like bounded adoption. The difference is *identity expansion* vs. *stewardship of existing identity*. You will not hear this distinction in a casual conversation. You need: "Has using AI changed what you think you're capable of?" and then listening to whether they're claiming new capability (S7B) or just managing existing capability better (S7A).
- **S7C**: Reflective stance. They have to tell you explicitly. "I'm thinking a lot about what AI means for creativity" or "I'm worried about what this does to junior people." Without that cue, you have no signal. It's almost always co-present with S7A or S7B, so treat it as optional in first diagnosis.

The model is **honest about this** — the "Limits of operationalization" section says snapshot-observable states are three (S0 vs. non-S0, S6A vs. S7D, D-level) and I've expanded it slightly to include the two S2 states as "separable by context" even if not internally distinguishable. Everything else is longitudinal-only or self-report-only.

**What this means for you:** You cannot do a 15-minute intake and say "Here's where this person is." You can narrow the field. You can say "Probably S2D given what they're saying about the tool" or "Likely in S3 range given the emotion level." You're triangulating, not diagnosing. Plan for 2–3 check-in conversations before you're confident about S3 vs. S5 or S3B vs. S7A.

### 2. Which transitions can I actually influence, and how?

Five buckets of actionable transitions:

**S1 → S2T or S2D (branch point):** You cannot change Identity Stakes (that's a property of how much the person cares about the domain). But you *can* control the *framing* of what AI is before the person forms their initial stance. If you present AI as "a tool for things you don't want to do anyway," low-stakes people lean S2T. If you present it as "capability in your craft domain," high-stakes people lean S2D faster. You can slow the S2D transition for high-stakes people by: (a) separating the tool demo from the capability threat ("Here's what this does operationally" vs. "Here's what this means for your role"), (b) giving them early evidence of control ("You're not using this alone; you're using it with your judgment"), (c) naming the discomfort early ("This might feel identity-threatening; that's normal and we can work with it").

**S2D → S3B (bounded adoption as a holding state):** You can help someone set boundaries that feel stable. This is not a permanent fix; the model treats S3B as a coping mechanism. But S3B is where many people stay productive for years. If someone is stuck in defensive resistance, asking "What would you be willing to try it for?" and then helping them define a *specific, measurable scope* can move them to bargaining. The mechanism is cognitive dissonance reduction — they can hold "AI is powerful but I control how much of my domain it touches." This works best when: (a) you help them pick a domain where AI is genuinely useful but not identity-central, (b) you commit to not expanding that scope without their agreement, and (c) you check back in after three model releases because the bargain destabilizes when capability crosses their boundary.

**S2D or S3B → S5 (Understanding):** You cannot force this, but you can create conditions. S5 requires two components: internal reframing (they've built a coherent mental model) and social normalization (using AI is unremarkable in their context). You can accelerate internal reframing by: (a) teaching them how and when the tool fails (reduces magical thinking), (b) giving them calibration feedback ("You used this for X, which is a good use; you tried it for Y, which is where it tends to hallucinate"), (c) walking through a failure together so they see it as a tool property, not a personal failing. You can affect social normalization by: (a) making AI use visible among peers they respect (not through pressure, through example), (b) treating AI adoption as part of "how we work now" without fanfare, (c) protecting them from public failure while they're learning.

**S5 → S6A (overuse due to dopamine):** Hard to prevent, but you can short-circuit it. The mechanism is variable-reward reinforcement — the unpredictability is what hooks engagement. If someone slides into S6A, the intervention is: (a) make the reward *predictable* (standardized templates, known limitations), (b) remove leaderboards or AI-usage metrics, (c) ask "What problem were you solving when you started using AI so heavily?" If the answer is "none, I was just experimenting," move them to a defined project where AI has a *bounded role*, not unlimited access.

**S5 or S6 → S3 (regression from external trigger):** You cannot prevent model releases or capability jumps. You can: (a) name the regression as normal ("Your mental model broke because the capability shifted. This happens."), (b) support them through re-evaluating their boundaries (S3B again), (c) help them notice what skills are still theirs (S3's terror management is about replaceability; you can shift the frame to "replaceability is different from irrelevance").

**S3, S6B, S6C, S6D → S7A or S7C (settling into stable states):** You can support *reflection* and *calibration*. For S3 people, ask: "What's the core of what you do that AI can't replicate or shouldn't touch?" For S6A people, ask: "Where did you lose judgment? Let's rebuild it." For S6B people, ask: "What made you doubt yourself? How do we rebuild that?" For S6C people, ask: "What were you trying to achieve before efficiency became the goal?" For S6D people, ask: "What does this tool actually help *you* do?" The interventions differ by driver, which is why S6 is four parallel states, not one.

**S6A → S7B (enthusiasm becomes identity expansion):** You can nurture this by: (a) noticing what the person is newly capable of, (b) asking them to teach others (externalize the new skill), (c) integrating the new capability into their role definition ("You're now a writer-with-AI," not "You're a writer who uses AI sometimes"). The model notes this is *possible*, not inevitable.

**What this means for you:** Most of your leverage is in the S1→S2D→S3B→S5 path and in the S6 management. You have almost no leverage in S7 because S7 is stable — your job there is support and mentorship, not intervention. You have *some* leverage in S2D through framing and boundary-setting. Your real power is in S3 (acknowledging the threat) and S6 (changing the driver).

### 3. Do the mechanism anchors help me intervene differently, or are they only useful for academic credibility?

**They help for S3 and S6. They don't help for anything else.**

Below S3, the mechanisms are early-stage cognition (novelty appraisal in S1, calibration under uncertainty in S2T, identity-protection cognition in S2D). These describe how the brain decides whether something is relevant and threatening. You can *frame* the threat differently, but you can't move the mechanism itself. Knowing that S1 is novelty appraisal doesn't change your intervention — you still get to "show them the tool and see what they think." Knowing that S2D is identity-protection cognition is more useful (it tells you that positive evidence won't convince them; you have to change the frame of what's at stake), but even then the intervention is framing, not mechanism-specific.

**S3 is where they matter.** S3 is anchored to self-efficacy collapse and terror management. This tells you: the person isn't being irrational, they're being *existential*. The intervention is not "here's evidence that AI isn't as good as you think"; it's "I see you're confronting your own replaceability. That's real discomfort and it doesn't disappear by argument." You can then ask: "What is the core of your identity that isn't threatened?" or "What will you still be good for that AI isn't?" The mechanism anchor changes the *tone* of the intervention from "persuade them the threat is overstated" to "help them integrate the threat into a revised self-concept." That's real.

**S3B is partly mechanism-dependent.** It's anchored to cognitive dissonance reduction via scope limitation. Knowing this means: they are not being irrational, they are solving a dissonance problem. The intervention is "help them solve it cleanly" by: (a) picking a real scope where AI is useful *and* safe for their identity, (b) making that scope explicit, (c) not pressuring them to expand it. The mechanism tells you the person will feel relief when they've compartmentalized successfully. That's actionable.

**S5 mechanisms matter for diagnosis, not intervention.** S5 is schema integration plus social normalization. Knowing this tells you to ask two questions: "Does this fit your working model?" and "Does your team treat this as normal?" If the answer to either is no, they're not in S5 yet. But the intervention is the same either way: support on one dimension that's still unresolved.

**S6 mechanisms are highly actionable.** The four S6 states have different mechanisms and you need different interventions:
- **S6A** (variable-reward reinforcement): Remove unpredictability. Standardize templates. Kill metrics.
- **S6B** (self-efficacy displacement): Rebuild their judgment. Have them evaluate AI outputs against their own thinking, not against AI outputs. Don't let them outsource the outsource.
- **S6C** (goal-substitution): Revisit the superordinate goal. "You wanted more time for thoughtful work, not maximum output. Have you checked whether you got that?"
- **S6D** (normative social influence): Change the context or the reference peer group. If the whole team is doing it, one person's rebalancing is easier.

These are genuinely different interventions, and the mechanism anchors tell you which one you need. Without them, you'd guess.

**S7 mechanisms are explanatory, not prescriptive.** S7A is metacognitive calibration (fine, helpful to know they're self-aware about failure modes). S7B is tool incorporation into identity (interesting, doesn't change what you do — you celebrate the expansion). S7C is meaning-making (acknowledge that they're doing philosophical work). S7F is generativity (recognize that mentoring is their stabilizing role). None of this changes your intervention. S7 people are self-regulating; your job is not to intervene, it's to support.

**What this means for you:** The mechanism anchors earn their keep in S3, S3B, and S6. They are genuinely useful. For S2T, S2D, and S5, they explain what's happening but don't change your intervention. For S7, they're interesting but optional. The model is honest that S0, S1, and S5's social-normalization component are currently conjecture or unmeasured. Don't expect them to support intervention yet.

### 4. The two-axis dropout (practical × emotional severity) — does this change my intervention vs. V4's binary split? If yes, how? If no, the change is overhead.

**Yes, it changes intervention, substantially. And the reversibility test makes it executable.**

V4 binary: "Is this emotional or practical dropout?" You had to guess or you lost the person trying to figure it out.

V5 continuous: Two independent 0–10 scales.

| Scenario | V4 guess | V5 graded | Intervention difference |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| High practical (8), low emotional (2) | "Practical, give them better tools" | "Yes, tool problem. Urgency: high." | Treat as blocker. Find or build a better tool. Timeline: weeks. |
| Low practical (2), high emotional (8) | "Emotional, psychological support" | "Yes, identity threat. Urgency: exists but slower." | Treat as identity work. No tool will fix this. Timeline: months, with the right conversation structure. |
| High both (7 practical, 7 emotional) | "Unclear, could be either" | "Mixed. Tool *failed in an identity spot*." | **Fix the tool first.** Once the tool works, the emotional severity often drops because the rationalization clears. Then do identity work if needed. Timeline: weeks for tool, months for recalibration. |
| Low both (2 practical, 2 emotional) | "They're just not interested" | "Confirmed: not interested." | Don't intervene. |

**The reversibility test makes this operational.** The model proposes: "Provide the dropout with a tool that demonstrably solves their cited failure. If they restart, practical severity was high. If they don't, emotional severity was driving the exit."

This is *genius* because it's testable in a conversation: "You said it kept missing context. If I could get you a version that remembered context, would you try again?" Listen to their answer *and tone*. "Yes, absolutely" (practical-driven). "I mean, maybe, but honestly I'm just tired of the whole thing" (emotional-driven). "I could try but I don't know if it matters" (mixed, leaning emotional).

**Execution in real workplace:**

- You do not need a perfect replacement tool. You need to know what solving their practical problem would look like. This can be a conversation: "What would have to change about the tool for you to use it again?"
- If the answer is a real technical fix (context window, speed, accuracy), practical severity is high.
- If the answer is vague ("I don't know," "It doesn't feel right," "I'm just over it"), emotional severity is high.
- If the answer is "The tool works fine, but honestly I'm exhausted from trying to adopt it," mixed with heavy emotional.

**How this changes intervention:**
- V4: "Is it emotional or practical?" → send them therapy or send them a FAQ.
- V5: "How much practical? How much emotional?" → Design a specific intervention. If practical is 8/10, your timeline is tool-dependent. If emotional is 8/10, your timeline is identity-work-dependent and slower. If both are high, fix the tool, *then* see if the emotional part persists.

**Is it overhead?** No. You're asking the same questions either way. The continuous scale just forces you to be *honest* about how much is each. It also prevents the false choice of "Well, I can't fix the tool, so it must be emotional." Maybe it's 50/50 and you can fix half of it, which makes room for the emotional work.

**What this means for you:** The two-axis model is not overhead. It's a better diagnosis. The reversibility test is practical and doable. Use it in 1:1s: "If X changed, would you try again?" The *tone* of the answer tells you the emotional-practical split as clearly as any scale score would.

### 5. The reversibility test — is it actually executable in a real workplace, or does it require resources practitioners don't have?

**It's executable as a diagnostic question. It's not executable as a controlled experiment.**

**What the model proposes:** "Provide the dropout with a tool that demonstrably solves their cited failure. If they restart, practical severity was high. If they don't, emotional severity was driving the exit."

**What you actually have:**
- Five minutes in a 1:1 to ask a diagnostic question: "You said the tool did X. If I could get you a version that didn't do X, would you pick it up again?"
- Listening to the answer: tone, hesitation, specificity of their response.
- No need for a second tool. No need for them to actually try it.
- Signal: clear and fast.

**What you don't have:**
- Time to integrate a new tool into their workflow and wait for them to settle.
- Budget to build a custom tool for one person.
- Certainty that a new tool won't introduce three new problems.

**Execution:**

You are *not* running an experiment. You are *gathering signal*. In a conversation:

1. "I heard you stopped using ChatGPT because [their cited reason]. What would the tool need to do to make that reason go away?"
2. Listen for: "It would need to [specific technical thing]" vs. "I don't know, I'm just over it" vs. "Maybe, but honestly I'm burned out."
3. Note the tone. Practical (engineering problem) vs. Emotional (motivational problem) vs. Mixed (tired and stuck).
4. *Optional:* If you have a workaround on hand ("There's a plugin that does this; it's not perfect but would you try it?"), offer it *only if* the reversibility test said practical severity was driving the exit. If emotional was high, offering a new tool is placebo and wastes time.

**Real obstacles you'll face:**

- **They don't have a clear "cited reason."** "It just didn't work for me" is a common answer. Your follow-up: "What didn't work? What did you try?" Dig until you get a specific failure or until it becomes clear there's no specific failure and it's just "not vibing."
- **Confounding:** Time has passed. They may have solved the problem elsewhere, taken a new role, or just have different capacity. The reversibility test still works ("Would X change your mind?") but be honest with yourself that the answer is hypothetical.
- **They don't want to re-engage:** This is information. Emotional severity is very high or they've moved on. Don't push.

**Is it worth doing?** Yes. It takes five minutes. It's more honest than guessing "probably emotional" and walking away. It's more actionable than "probably practical" and sending them to IT.

**What this means for you:** The reversibility test is *not* a controlled experiment in the scientific sense. It's a triage question. It's highly executable. Use it.

### 6. What is missing that I would need to make this a working diagnostic + intervention tool?

**Missing pieces in order of urgency:**

**1. Intake Protocol (CRITICAL)**
You need a short structured conversation to narrow down states before you're deep in back-and-forth diagnostics. Right now, the model gives you state definitions but no intake questions. Suggested outline:

- "How long have you been using AI for work?" (→ places them post-S0)
- "How much of your core work could AI do?" (→ signals Identity Stakes without psychoanalyzing)
- "When you tried it, what was the first reaction? (Useful? Threatening? Confusing?)" (→ S1 flavor)
- "Are you testing it carefully, or nitpicking it?" (→ S2T vs. S2D signal, requires listening)
- "If you use it now, what stops you from using it more?" (→ S3B boundaries vs. S5 maturity vs. dropout)

Build a decision tree, not a full questionnaire. Goal: five minutes, narrow the field from "one of 17 states" to "probably S2D or S3B range."

**2. Mechanism-Specific Intervention Scripts (HIGH)**
For each of S3, S3B, S6A, S6B, S6C, S6D, you need: "If the person is here, here's what to ask." Not a therapy protocol. Just good questions.

Example for S3 (currently missing):
- "Help me understand what this threatens about your role."
- "If AI can do X, what can *you* do that AI can't?"
- "What's the core of your identity that's separate from this skill?"

Example for S6B (currently missing):
- "When you use AI, do you notice yourself checking its output against your own thinking, or against AI's thinking?"
- "What made you stop trusting your own judgment?"

These change the intervention from "Here's how to use the tool better" to "Here's why you're stuck and how we unlock it."

**3. Assessment Protocol for Identity Stakes (HIGH)**
The model defines Identity Stakes as "how much the person's self-concept depends on skills AI can replicate in that domain." But no way to assess it in under 5 minutes without priming them.

Proposed shortcut:
- "How many years did you train to get good at this?"
- "When you do this skill, how much of your self-concept is tied to doing it *well*?"
- "If someone else did this task faster with AI, what would that say about you?"

The third question is the real one. If the answer is defensive, Identity Stakes are high. If the answer is "Nothing, it's just a tool," stakes are low.

**4. Calibration Guide for D-Level Assessment (MEDIUM)**
D1–D4 is defined but there's no guidance on how to ask about it or infer it. You're supposed to observe it, but real conversation is messier than the definitions.

Proposed additions:
- D1: "I'm using AI to summarize, extract, or organize information I already had."
- D2: "I'm delegating a skill execution task (write, code, design) but I'm reviewing and editing the output."
- D3: "I'm asking AI to ideate, strategize, or draft something new that I then refine." (Fuzzy boundary with D2.)
- D4: "I'm asking AI to help me decide things or I'm using autonomous AI." (Rare in most roles.)

Many people drift between D1 and D2 in a single day. Your question: "For the work you've stopped using AI for, were you mostly delegating specific tasks (D2) or asking it to think with you (D3–D4)?"

**5. S3B Boundary-Setting Template (MEDIUM)**
S3B is where people are *productive* even though it's a coping mechanism. The model describes it but doesn't help practitioners *use* it as a holding state.

Template:
- "What domain or task type would you be willing to try AI for?"
- "Why does this feel safe?" (Listen for: "It's not my core skill" or "No one depends on it being perfect" or "I still own the decision.")
- "Let's be explicit: you're using AI for [X], not for [Y]. Agreed?"
- "When a new AI release comes out, we check whether it changes what's safe in [X]. Agreed?"
- "Check-in: Does this boundary still feel right, or is it time to expand it or contract it?"

This prevents the person from feeling gaslit ("You said you were trying it"; "Yeah, but differently") and helps them notice when the boundary needs re-negotiation.

**6. Longitudinal Tracking for S2T vs. S2D and S3B vs. S7A (MEDIUM)**
The model acknowledges these distinctions are longitudinal-only. There's no guidance on what to notice over time.

For S2T vs. S2D:
- Ask the same question twice: "What do you think of [new capability]?"
- First time, listen for openness vs. defensiveness.
- Second time (two weeks later, after they've tried it), listen: Did they change their view based on evidence (S2T), or did they find new reasons to dismiss it (S2D)?

For S3B vs. S7A:
- When a new AI capability comes out that crosses their boundary, notice: Do they re-evaluate based on new evidence ("Let me test this carefully"), or do they move the boundary quickly without testing (S3B mode)?

**7. End-State Stabilization Guide (LOW)**
S7A, S7B, S7C, S7F are stable; you're not trying to move them. But you should know how to support them:
- **S7A:** Feedback on calibration. "Your use case analysis was exactly right." Don't second-guess their boundaries.
- **S7B:** Celebrate the expansion. Notice the new capability. Ask them to teach others.
- **S7C:** Take their ethical questions seriously. Don't dismiss them as over-thinking. They are doing real meaning-making work.
- **S7F:** Clear the path for mentoring. Their influence is valuable; make space for it.

**What this means for you:** V5 is 80% of a working tool. The missing 20% is the *operational* part: the questions, the trees, the templates. Build those and V5 becomes something you can hand to a manager. Without them, V5 is a great frame but a practitioner is still improvising on every conversation.

## Recommendations

**For V6 — before building anything else:**

1. **Build the intake protocol.** Five-minute conversation that narrows states. This is prerequisite for everything else.

2. **Add mechanism-specific intervention scripts.** For S3, S3B, S6A, S6B, S6C, S6D: "If you diagnose this state, here are the questions to ask and the mechanisms they unlock." Not therapy; just good questions grounded in why the state behaves the way it does.

3. **Add Identity Stakes assessment shortcut.** Three questions, 90 seconds. Goal: signal whether stakes are high or low in this person × domain pair.

4. **Clarify D-level definitions with examples.** The current definitions are correct but abstract. Add the real-world signal: "What does delegating a design vision look like vs. delegating a design task?" (D3 vs. D2.)

5. **Add S3B boundary-setting template.** Explicit agreement, check-in protocol, re-negotiation trigger. This makes S3B usable as a stable holding state, not just a description.

6. **Document what to track longitudinally.** For practitioners who can't diagnose S2T vs. S2D or S3B vs. S7A in a snapshot, what should they notice over time? When should they re-assess?

7. **Drop or measure the S7F prerequisite.** The model claims stable S7F requires prior S3 passage. Either gather longitudinal data to test this or reframe it as "typically more stable if they've been through S3; we don't yet know if it's required."

8. **Test the reversibility test.** It's elegant and executable. Use it with 50 actual dropouts and see if the tone-based diagnosis matches the outcome (whether they restart or not). Report back.

**Priority:** Intake protocol first. Without it, practitioners are improvising. With it, the whole model becomes actionable.
