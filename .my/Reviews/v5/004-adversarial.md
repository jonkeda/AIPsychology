# Adversarial Critique — Model V5

## TL;DR

- **Mechanism anchors add no explanatory power.** They are labels (e.g., "identity-protection cognition") attached to behaviors the model already described. None of the anchors' predictions have been tested against the model's claims. Relabeling a behavior as an anchor is not the same as explaining it.

- **The "Limits of operationalization" section is a confession, not an advance.** It admits that V4's core distinctions — S2T vs S2D, S3B vs S7A, the dual-component S5 — cannot be measured. These weren't newly discovered limits in V5; they were always there. V5 is acknowledging a structural unfalsifiability problem that V4 papered over.

- **V5 is V4 with humbler claims and longer footnotes.** The state structure is identical. The axes are identical. The transitions are identical. The major additions are: admissions of unmeasurability, mechanism labels without tested predictions, and upgraded caveats to the status of "heuristic."

- **The dropout two-axis system is dimensionally empty.** A 2D plane with no metric, no empirical way to score positions, and an "operational test" that is circular: if the person won't use a "better tool," conclude it was emotional severity — but that just means you failed to make a tool they wanted to use.

- **Identity-protection cognition is too broad to be falsifying.** The model anchors S2D to Kahan's motivated-reasoning work, but Kahan's own research shows that motivated reasoning explains nearly all belief-resistant behavior. Attaching the same mechanism to S2D, S3, and S3B doesn't distinguish them; it just says "people protect their identity in all three states." The mechanism anchor predicts everything and therefore predicts nothing.

- **Regression as a property, not an exception, makes falsification impossible.** The model now says "any state can regress to any earlier state from internal or external triggers." But if regression is always available as an explanation, no observation falsifies the model. Did a person stay in S2D for years? They must not have experienced a trigger. Did they leave immediately? Trigger happened. The framework explains every outcome post-hoc.

## Verdict

V5 is intellectually more honest than V4. It makes fewer claims and admits which ones are unmeasurable. That is not the same as making a better model. The core problem persists: the model is a taxonomy of observed behaviors dressed in mechanism-anchor language, with no mechanism that actually constrains which behaviors are possible. Adding citations to cognitive science and admitting you can't measure something do not turn folk psychology into science.

## The Case That V5 Is Cosmetic

### 1. Mechanism anchors are descriptive relabeling, not explanatory anchoring

The model anchors S2D to "identity-protection cognition" (citing Kahan and Festinger). That is the label for the already-described behavior: asymmetric updating, discounting positive evidence, amplifying negative evidence. Adding the name of a psychological mechanism does not explain *why* that behavior happens or predict *what* the person will do next.

An anchor would have explanatory power if it narrowed the space of possible behaviors. For example, if "identity-protection cognition" predicted that S2D people would accept *any* evidence framed as protecting their identity (even if it contradicts earlier positions), the anchor would be falsifying. But the model doesn't make that prediction. S2D is still just "asymmetric updating." The mechanism label is decoration.

**Operative text**: "Mechanism anchor: **identity-protection cognition**..." (S2D section) and identical pattern across all states.

**The fatal gap**: Not a single mechanism anchor has a tested prediction in the model. No section says "if S2D is driven by identity protection, then we should observe X behavior that differs from S2T in way Y." The anchors are one-way labels, not two-way constraints.

### 2. "Limits of operationalization" admits the model was never falsifiable

The new section lists what *cannot* be measured: S2T vs S2D (both look like testing, but only S2D shows asymmetric updating "only visible across multiple evaluations"), S3B vs S7A (the heuristic requires longitudinal observation across model releases), the dual-component S5 (internal reframing and social normalization are both unobservable).

These are not new limits. They were always there. V4 claimed S2T and S2D were distinguishable states; V5 admits the only way to distinguish them is to watch someone's behavior change across multiple model releases. That is not a measurement; that is a post-hoc narrative. You observe someone's choices, decide whether the updates were "asymmetric," and then retroactively assign the state.

**Operative text**: "S2T vs S2D — both look like 'user testing the tool' in a snapshot. Asymmetric updating on positive evidence is the operative signal, only visible across multiple evaluations." and "S3B vs S7A — the heuristic in S3B requires watching boundary-movement behavior across multiple model releases."

**The structural problem**: If the only way to distinguish two states is to watch outcomes across time and judge whether the person's behavior matches the state's definition, the states are not separate claims about reality; they are interpretive frames applied after the fact. A state with no snapshot test is a narrative label, not a falsifiable construct.

### 3. The dual-component S5 is post-hoc unfalsifiable

S5 requires both "internal reframing" and "social normalization." Neither is measurable. The note says so: "the dual-component requirement is currently conjecture. No instrument is specified for either component, and any observed discomfort can be attributed to mismatch."

This is the classic move: define a success state that has two independent unmeasurable components. Outcome follows the model? Person had internal reframing. Outcome contradicts it? Lacked social normalization. The model explains every path.

**Operative text**: "Stability has two components, both required: **Internal reframing** — personal mental model is coherent. **Social normalization** — surrounding environment treats AI use as unremarkable. Note: the dual-component requirement is currently conjecture."

**Why it matters**: A person who rejects AI after reaching S5 is explained as "lacking social normalization," not as a model failure. A person who embraces it but seems unhappy is "lacking internal reframing." The model survives every outcome.

### 4. The dropout two-axis plane is mathematically hollow

V5 replaces V4's binary dropout split (emotional vs practical) with a 2D plane (Practical severity 0–10, Emotional severity 0–10). But the model provides:

- No definition of how to score a position on the plane
- No empirical data on what the distribution of dropouts looks like
- No prediction of what region of the plane predicts what recovery outcome

The "operational test" is offered as an operationalization: provide a better tool; if they restart, practical severity was high; if not, emotional severity was. But this is circular. If someone doesn't restart after you provide what *you* think is a better tool, you conclude the problem was emotional. But the only evidence that it was "better" is that it's better according to your judgment, not theirs. You have not measured anything; you have assigned a label post-hoc.

**Operative text**: "Provide the dropout with a tool that demonstrably solves their cited failure. If they restart, practical severity was high. If they don't, emotional severity was driving the exit..."

**The gap**: There is no independent measure of "better tool" and no prediction of the distribution of dropouts across the plane. The plane is a decorative 2D space with no explanatory content.

### 5. Identity-protection cognition is universal, therefore not distinguishing

The model anchors multiple states to identity-protection mechanisms: S2D to "identity-protection cognition," S3 to "self-efficacy collapse," S3B to "cognitive dissonance reduction," and S7C to "meaning-making." Kahan's work (cited under S2D) shows that motivated reasoning — protecting one's identity from disconfirming evidence — is a robust cross-domain phenomenon.

But if identity protection explains S2D, S3, and S3B, it does not distinguish them. All three states show identity-protective behavior; the model calls them different states. Why is the S2D person's asymmetric updating a different state from the S3 person's existential discomfort? Both are identity-protective. Both are anchored (eventually) to the same mechanism. The mechanism label obscures rather than clarifies why these are separate states.

The model's answer is implicit: the *intensity* differs. But intensity is not measured and not predicted by the mechanism; it is modulated by the two axes (Identity Stakes × Task Delegation Level), which are orthogonal to the mechanism anchor.

**Operative text**: S2D, S3, and S3B all use identity-protective mechanisms as anchors, yet are claimed to be separate states. S5 also references identity-related anchors ("schema integration").

**Why it matters**: A mechanism anchor that applies to five different states is not constraining the model; it is naming a universal human behavior and calling it an explanation.

### 6. The S7F-requires-S3 claim is flagged as untested but constrains the model anyway

The model speculates: "Most stable when the person has previously passed through S3. Lived experience of Ego Shock is what makes them credible to others currently in it; without that experience, the advocacy tends to be naive (S6A flavor) or compelled (S6D flavor) and collapses under the first hostile question."

Then immediately: "Note: the 'S3 prerequisite for stable S7F' claim is currently conjecture."

If it is conjecture, it should not shape the model's structure. If it is true, it should be a structural requirement, not a footnote. Instead, V5 presents it as both: a constraint on what "stable S7F" means *and* a conjecture. This allows the model to absorb counterexamples: a successful S7F evangelist who never passed through S3 would simply be "unstable S7F" or "S6D flavor," not a refutation of the model.

**Operative text**: "Most stable when the person has previously passed through S3... Note: the 'S3 prerequisite for stable S7F' claim is currently conjecture."

**The problem**: Making a non-falsifiable claim and flagging it as uncertain is not the same as removing it from the model. It remains a constraint that shapes interpretation of observations.

### 7. The S3B/S7A heuristic is retroactively applied

The model offers a heuristic to distinguish S3B (bounded adoption under ego threat) from S7A (bounded adoption after mature integration): "In S3B, boundaries are renegotiated **under capability pressure**. When a new model release crosses one of the self-imposed lines, the line moves quickly without documented testing. In S7A, boundaries move **only after measured evidence**."

But whether a person is in S3B or S7A determines how you interpret the same behavior. Suppose a person has bounded adoption and a new model is released. They don't change their boundary. In S3B, you interpret this as "they're awaiting the next threat that will shatter the boundary." In S7A, you interpret this as "they tested and the evidence wasn't compelling." The same non-action means opposite things depending on which state you assigned them to first.

The model admits this: "This is a **heuristic**, not a measured fact. It requires longitudinal observation across multiple model releases, and is contaminated by self-report once a subject has read this model."

**Operative text**: "Heuristic distinction from S7A... This is a heuristic, not a measured fact."

**Why this matters**: A distinction that is contaminated by the subject's self-report and requires longitudinal observation to apply is not a property of the states; it is a post-hoc narrative applied after watching the outcome. The heuristic does not predict; it interprets.

### 8. Mechanism anchors do not constrain the model's transitions

Compare the mechanism anchors to a genuine structural constraint. A real constraint would say: "If a person is in S2D (identity-protective cognition), then they cannot directly transition to S5 (schema integration) without passing through S3 (identity disruption)."

The model makes no such prediction. S2D can transition to S5 Understanding, or to S2T, or to S3, or to dropout. Knowing the mechanism anchor tells you nothing about which transition happens. The anchor is a label for the *current* state, not a predictor of the *next* state. It adds no falsifying power.

**Operative text**: See S2D transitions: "→ S5 Understanding — trust established", "→ S2D Defensive Resistance", "→ Practical-flavored Dropout". No mechanism-based constraint on which transition occurs.

**The question this leaves**: What is the mechanism anchor *for*? It is not constraining transitions. It is not predicting new observations. It is naming a behavioral pattern that was already described. This is hypothesis decoration, not hypothesis anchoring.

### 9. Regression as a property removes the falsifiability of the state structure

The model now claims: "Regression is a **property of every transition**, not a destination. Any state in S5–S7 can be pushed back to earlier states by: (list of triggers)."

If any state can transition to any earlier state, the core state structure becomes untestable. Observed behavior: person stayed in S2D for two years and left abruptly. Does this falsify the model's claim that S2D typically leads to S3 or S5? No — the person just experienced a trigger (a model release, a workplace change, a personal underperformance) at year two.

Observed behavior: person jumped from S0 to S5 without passing through S1, S2T, S2D, or S3. Falsifies the model? No — "possible for low stakes with prior conceptual familiarity" (S1 section).

The model has provided an exit for every observed path. The state structure is now a taxonomy of possible observations, not a falsifiable model of a causal process.

**Operative text**: "Regression is a **property of every transition**, not a destination. Any state in S5–S7 can be pushed back to earlier states by: A major model release that breaks the current mental model..."

**Why it matters**: A model in which any path is possible is not wrong; it is empty. It explains everything post-hoc and predicts nothing in advance.

### 10. V5 downgrades falsifiability claims from V4 without reconceptualizing

The introduction states: "Several V4 claims are downgraded from 'falsifiable' to 'heuristic' where the model lacks a workable measurement protocol."

This is presented as a refinement. In fact, it is an admission that V4's falsifiability claims were aspirational. V5 is not a better model; it is a more accurate model of V4's actual status. But the solution to "this claim is not falsifiable" is not "flag it as a heuristic and keep it." It is "remove it or develop a measurement protocol."

**Operative text**: "Several V4 claims are downgraded from 'falsifiable' to 'heuristic'..." and the Limits of operationalization section.

**The deeper problem**: The model inherits its entire state structure from V4. The only changes are: added mechanism labels (which don't constrain), admitted measurement limits (which were always there), and refactored the dropout split into a 2D plane (which is unmeasurable). None of these changes address the underlying issue: the model has no testable predictions.

## Minimum Changes To Defeat This Critique

The critique can be defeated *or* the model can be honest. These are the minimum changes to do either.

### Path 1: Make the model testable (honesty track)

**1. Pick a single state transition and make a falsifiable prediction about its mechanism anchor.**

Example: S2D is anchored to "identity-protection cognition." Propose a behavioral test: people in S2D, when presented with evidence of AI capability in their domain, will rate the evidence as lower-quality/less-relevant/more-biased than people in S2T rating identical evidence. Propose to measure this. Run an experiment. Let the results determine whether the anchor predicts behavior or just names it.

Currently: no such test exists. The anchor is one-way. Until a two-way test exists, remove the anchor and call the state what it is: "asymmetric updating when identity is at stake."

**2. Operationalize the dual-component S5.**

"Internal reframing" and "social normalization" are unmeasured. Pick one or both. For "internal reframing," propose a measure: ask the person to explain what AI can and cannot do, and score the explanation against a rubric of accurate claims. For "social normalization," propose a measure: ask peers whether AI use is treated as normal in the environment, or measure adoption rate in the social cohort.

Do this before claiming that S5 requires both components. If you can't measure them, remove the claim or rewrite S5 to require only what you can observe.

**3. Test the S3B/S7A heuristic on a real longitudinal dataset.**

Gather a cohort, observe them across three model releases, note whose boundaries move under capability pressure (S3B signature) and whose move only after their own testing (S7A signature). Report the signal-to-noise ratio. If the heuristic is reliable, publish it. If not, either refine it until it is or replace it with a measurable distinction.

Currently: the heuristic is "contaminated by self-report" — your own words. That is admitting the distinction doesn't survive empirical scrutiny.

**4. Replace the dropout 2D plane with a measured scale or remove it.**

If practical and emotional severity are truly independent, gather data on real dropouts. Create a 2×2 contingency table of outcomes (restart / no restart) vs (high practical / low practical) and (high emotional / low emotional). Publish the cell counts. If the cells are non-empty and the patterns are distinct, keep the plane. If not, return to the V4 binary split (which at least admits it is crude).

The "operational test" (offer a better tool and see if they restart) is not a measurement; it is an intervention study. Run it properly: compare tool-offer to control, measure both groups' restart rates, then explain the difference. If the difference is captured by the 2D plane, show the contingency table.

### Path 2: Simplify and admit limits (minimal-honesty track)

**1. Stop claiming mechanism anchors add explanatory power.**

Remove all mechanism-anchor sections or rewrite them as "related literature" with no claim that the mechanism constrains the model. Change:

```
Mechanism anchor: identity-protection cognition (Kahan, Festinger)
```

To:

```
Related cognition literature: This state resembles patterns described in identity-
protection research (Kahan's work on motivated reasoning). This connection is noted
for conceptual grounding only; the model does not test whether mechanism X predicts
transition Y.
```

This is honest. It says the state is conceptually related to a mechanism without claiming the mechanism explains the behavior.

**2. Remove all "conjecture" claims that constrain the model.**

The S3 prerequisite for stable S7F (flagged as conjecture) currently shapes how you interpret S7F observations. Either test it or remove it. If you test it and find it false, S7F needs to be redefined. If you test it and find it true, move it from "Note" to "Structural requirement." Do not leave constraints as untested conjectures.

**3. Reduce the S3B/S7A distinction or mark it as non-operational.**

Change the text to: "S3B and S7A are conceptually distinct (defensive posture vs. measured integration), but currently lack an operational test that survives longitudinal data without post-hoc interpretation. Until such a test exists, do not attempt to classify individual cases into one or the other."

This is honest about the model's limits.

**4. Replace the dropdown 2D plane with an observed typology.**

Stop proposing that practical and emotional severity are independent axes. Instead, gather data on how dropouts actually look. Report: "N% of dropouts cite practical failures, M% cite emotional exhaustion, P% report both." Don't pretend you can measure a 2D plane with no metric.

### Path 3: Keep the model as a descriptive taxonomy (reframing track)

Stop calling the model falsifiable. Reframe it as:

> "This is a taxonomy of observed AI-adoption patterns, not a causal model with testable predictions. It organizes behaviors you may recognize in yourself or others. Each state is labeled with a plausible cognitive mechanism, but the model does not yet test whether those mechanisms predict the observed transitions. The model is useful as a thinking tool (a frame for understanding your own experience) and as a vocabulary for discussing adoption patterns (a shared language for teams). It is not useful for prediction, intervention design, or causal inference."

This is honest and defensible. Many useful conceptual frameworks are purely descriptive. The model does not have to be testable to be useful. But it cannot claim to be falsifiable and simultaneously admit that none of its core distinctions are measurable.

## Recommendations

**Recommendation 1 (strongest): Run an empirical study before V6.**

Pick two state transitions (S1 → S2T vs S1 → S2D; S2D → S3 vs S2D → S3B) and gather behavioral data on the actual signals. Do subjects in S2T actually show symmetric updating and subjects in S2D show asymmetric updating? Can you measure it? Can you predict which transition a person will take?

Run this study. Publish the results. If the transitions are real, V6 can anchor the model to tested mechanisms. If not, V6 needs to restructure the states.

**Recommendation 2 (essential): Make a choice between Paths 1 and 3.**

You cannot have it both ways. Either develop testable predictions (Path 1, honest-hard-track) or admit the model is descriptive-only (Path 3, honest-transparent-track). Staying in the middle — claiming some parts are falsifiable, flagging some parts as conjecture, offering unmeasurable 2D planes — is the worst position. It is neither testable nor transparent.

**Recommendation 3 (practical): If you keep the model as-is, fix the anchor language.**

At minimum, change all mechanism-anchor sections to say "Related literature:" instead of "Mechanism anchor:" This signals to readers that the connection is conceptual, not explanatory.

**Recommendation 4 (process): Stop adding detail before measuring the skeleton.**

V4 → V5 added: mechanism anchors (unmeasured), a 2D dropout plane (unmeasured), and a "Limits" section (admitting unmeasurability). V5 → V6 should not add more states, more axes, or more mechanism labels. It should test the existing structure or simplify it.

You have built a rich taxonomy. The question now is whether it reflects reality or just organizes your prior intuitions. Stop expanding until you answer that question.
