# Missing Populations & Edge Cases — Model V5

## TL;DR

- **AI-native users (children/teens)** bypass Initial Encounter entirely and have no pre-formed identity to threaten; the model has no state for "AI is ambient baseline."
- **Non-knowledge work** (trades, manual labor, service) doesn't fit the model's skill-and-expertise frame; an electrician's S3 triggers and T-level intensity scale differently than a designer's.
- **Cognitive disabilities** are absent: the model assumes users can perform schema integration and metacognitive calibration; it ignores accessibility-aid use cases where AI is a prosthetic, not a threat.
- **Emotional/companionship use** has no pathway; the entire model assumes skill-domain or productivity anxiety, which doesn't apply to someone seeking a virtual friend.
- **AI researchers** can short-circuit S2D and jump to S3 or S7C because they understand capability limits; the model assumes domain ignorance.
- **Economic dependence on non-adoption** (human-only service providers, skilled-labor advocates) has no state; S3 is not a *problem to solve* for them, it's a *permanent survival threat*.
- **Pre-emptive ethical rejection** (religious or principled, before any shock) doesn't fit; the model assumes ethical reflection comes *after* S3, not before S1.
- **Adoption Ceiling gap**: V5 removes the construct that allowed bounded use without model failure. When someone with D1 stability is mandated to D3+, they destabilize—but S5 (Understanding) doesn't distinguish "I understand it AND I choose limits" from "I understand it AND I *can't* absorb higher stakes."

## Verdict

The model describes one primary adaptation cycle well: a knowledge worker (medium to high stakes in their domain) who encounters AI, faces identity shock, integrates it or rejects it, and settles into an end state. It does not describe adaptation when AI is ambient rather than encountered, when stakes are economic survival rather than identity, when use is for relationship rather than productivity, when the user has domain expertise about the technology itself, or when cognitive or ethical commitments override the threat-appraisal pathway entirely. The removals in V5 (Adoption Ceiling, numerical formulas) close off legitimate use cases without declaring them out of scope. A revised version should either restore scope-limit declarations for each gap or introduce parallel states for non-knowledge-work and non-threat pathways.

## Findings

### 1. AI-native users (children/teens who first encounter AI before age 18)

**The gap:** The model requires S0 → S1 (Initial Encounter) as mandatory. An AI-native user never has this moment. For someone who has used ChatGPT since age 14 or who grew up with Alexa in the house, there is no "what is this thing?" transition. AI is already part of their baseline.

**What breaks:** Identity Stakes are not yet formed. A 16-year-old hasn't decided whether "writing" or "coding" is core to their self-concept. So when they use AI for writing, it doesn't trigger S2D (Defensive Resistance) because there's no identity to defend. The model predicts S2D for high-stakes encounters; this user has no stakes because they haven't built them yet. They may enter S7B (Identity Expansion) directly—"I can write fiction with AI"—without ever passing through S3 (Ego Shock).

**Proposed fix:** Add an optional parallel path: S0 → S1* (Ambient Exposure, not encounter) → either S5 (Understanding) or S7B (Identity Expansion) directly. Or explicitly limit the model to users who have pre-formed identity in the domain and declare AI-natives out of scope in version 5.

---

### 2. Non-knowledge work (trades, manual labor, service work)

**The gap:** The model is written entirely in knowledge-work language: "skill," "craft," "expertise," "domain." An electrician has skills, but the skill space is physical-diagnostic, not abstract. A service worker (cashier, nurse, warehouse worker) faces different axes of AI threat.

**What breaks:** Task Delegation Levels are defined as D1 (Information), D2 (Task Execution), D3 (Creative), D4 (Autonomous Partnership). These assume the worker is deciding, analyzing, or creating. An electrician *does* diagnose and decide, but framed differently. A warehouse picker doesn't fit any D-level cleanly—the work is not delegable in the D-level sense, but it *is* replaceable by automation. S3 (Ego Shock) would hit them as "I am being replaced," not "my skills are being replicated." The emotional flavor is different—less "identity crisis" and more "economic threat." But the model doesn't separate these.

**Proposed fix:** Define a separate D-level frame for non-knowledge work (e.g., physical-task replacement, decision-tree automation, routine-pattern detection) and warn that S3 in that domain is economic, not identity-based. Or explicitly scope the model to knowledge workers.

---

### 3. People with cognitive disabilities

**The gap:** The model assumes all users can perform the cognitive operations it requires. S5 (Understanding) stability requires schema integration and internal reframing—integrating a new tool into mental models. S7A (Maturity) requires metacognitive calibration—accurate self-assessment of when your judgment beats the tool's. Neither is accessible to everyone.

**What breaks:** A user with significant cognitive disability (intellectual disability, working-memory deficit, processing-speed impairment) might use AI as a cognitive prosthetic—not to replace skill, but to *enable* it. A person with dyslexia using AI to rewrite text is not in S3 (identity threat); they are in S5 or S7A directly because the tool provides access to a domain they were previously excluded from. The model has no state for "AI as accessibility aid." It assumes all AI is capability-matching (you already have the skill, and AI does it too), not capability-enabling.

**Proposed fix:** Add an optional S5-equivalent (Understanding + Access) that describes bounded use where the tool's purpose is enablement, not replacement. Or explicitly state that the model applies only to users without requiring cognitive assistive technology.

---

### 4. Emotional and companionship use

**The gap:** The entire model assumes the use case is productivity, skill, or domain-based work. A user seeking emotional companionship from AI (a chatbot friend, a virtual mentor, a presence) is not in any state the model describes.

**What breaks:** Identity Stakes don't apply ("is friendship core to my identity?" is not how this works). Task Delegation Levels don't map (D3 Creative Delegation doesn't make sense for conversation). S3 doesn't trigger because there's no capability threat—having an AI conversation partner doesn't threaten the user's belief they can have human relationships. S5 (Understanding) requires the user to build a "mental model of what the tool is and isn't"—but if the use case is relationship, the whole point is *not* to treat it as a tool requiring calibration. The model assumes use is instrumental; emotional use is not.

**Proposed fix:** Acknowledge that emotional-companionship use is out of scope and requires a separate model. Or add a parallel fork after S1 that describes emotional-relational transitions (S2E: Relationship Formation, S5E: Attachment, end states like S7E: Dependent Companionship).

---

### 5. AI researchers

**The gap:** The model assumes users lack insider domain knowledge. An AI researcher (even one not in LLMs) has accurate mental models of what the technology can and can't do. They have specialized knowledge that short-circuits the typical cycle.

**What breaks:** They don't spend time in S2T (Trust Evaluation) testing because they already know the failure modes. They don't enter S2D (Defensive Resistance) because they're not in ego-defense mode—they're in technical-assessment mode. A researcher might jump S2D → S3 directly (the capability shock is real even knowing how it works), or skip S3 entirely and enter S7C (Ethical / Philosophical Integration) because they're already thinking about the societal implications. The model predicts S2T or S2D is typical after S1; it's not typical for researchers.

**Proposed fix:** Note that high domain-expertise users may skip S2D and accelerate to S3 or S7C. Or add a notation (*high-expertise entry point*) at S3 and S7C to flag that domain knowledge changes transition sequence.

---

### 6. People economically dependent on NOT adopting AI

**The gap:** A skilled service provider (high-end writing service, bespoke design, personalized tutoring) or a worker whose labor is explicitly positioned as "human-only" faces a permanent economic threat from AI. For them, S3 (Ego Shock) is not a temporary crisis to integrate—it's a permanent condition.

**What breaks:** S7D (Burnout / Withdrawal) or emotional dropout are the only end states available to them. But they can't actually withdraw—their livelihood is at stake. S3B (Bargaining) doesn't work ("I'll use AI only for X") because the whole point is that they *can't* use it or they're economically dead. They might stay in S2D (Defensive Resistance) permanently, locked in a state of "proving AI is wrong" not because of ego defense, but because economic survival requires it. The model has no state for sustained, rational, economically-motivated non-adoption.

**Proposed fix:** Add a notation that S2D can be a stable end state for users with economic incentives to stay there, distinct from the identity-defense flavor of S2D. Or explicitly scope the model to users who *can* choose to adopt.

---

### 7. Pre-emptive ethical rejection (without S2D/S3)

**The gap:** The model's pathway for ethical or religious objection is: S1 → S2D → S3 → S7C (ethical reckoning) or dropout. But a user might reject AI on principle *before* ever passing through S2D or S3—they decide it's unethical and choose not to engage without the cognitive dissonance journey.

**What breaks:** Someone who says "I don't use AI because it's built on scraped data and I don't consent to that" hasn't *experienced* S2D (defensive testing) or S3 (ego shock). They've skipped both. The model has S1 → S7C as a possible path (ethical reflection), but S7C is described as arriving *after* other engagement. A user who enters S7C before S2D is effectively S7C-without-use, which is not really a state in the model—it's an end state only reachable from S7A or S7B.

**Proposed fix:** Add an explicit S1 → S7C (direct ethical integration) pathway for users with principled objections prior to engagement, or expand S7C to include "pre-engagement ethical reckoning" as a distinct mode.

---

### 8. The Adoption Ceiling gap

**The background:** V4 included an optional "Adoption Ceiling" construct—a limit on how far a given user could go in the model based on their values, cognitive capacity, or emotional tolerance. V5 removes it, saying "if a use case requires it, that use case justifies V6."

**The gap:** This creates a blind spot for users who are stable at one intensity level but destabilize under pressure to go higher. A manager at S7A (Maturity) + low stakes who uses AI for email summaries (D1) is comfortable and productive. Then the company mandates using AI for performance reviews and hiring decisions (D3+, high stakes). The manager crashes into S3 because the emotional weight of higher delegation exceeds their psychological integration of AI. They didn't fail to understand AI; they understood it and chose not to delegate certain decisions to it. S5 (Understanding) doesn't distinguish between "I can absorb D4 if I want" and "I can absorb D4 if forced, but I'll destabilize."

**What breaks:** S5 is described as stable because the person has a working mental model. But stability doesn't account for *will or values*. A person can reach S5 and still have a ceiling on what they'll do. The model assumes that understanding leads to capability; it doesn't account for principled refusal based on adequate understanding. When forced past that ceiling, the person regresses to S3—but this is not modeled as a ceiling hit, it's modeled as a fresh shock.

**Proposed fix:** Either restore an Adoption Ceiling construct (tied to D-level, not universal) or add a note to S5 that distinguishes "I understand it and I'm ready for D4" from "I understand it and I'm choosing D1–2" as different S5 stable states. The latter is not a failure of understanding; it's a success of integration with boundaries.

---

## The Adoption Ceiling Gap (elaborated)

When V5 removed the Adoption Ceiling, it removed the only mechanism for modeling users who are mature (S7A, or S5-stable) at low intensity but can't scale up without destabilization. Removing it was justified as "if you need it, you should design a new model." But the gap is real enough that users hit it regularly:

1. A researcher uses AI as a brainstorming aid (D2, low stakes in the workflow). Mature use, boundaries clear.
2. Institution mandates AI for proposal writing (D3, high stakes in the domain).
3. Researcher hits S3 because the stakes changed, not the understanding.
4. The model calls this regression. In fact, it's a ceiling hit.

**Proposed fix:** Restore Adoption Ceiling as a D-level cap, defined per user. A user might have D-ceiling = 2 (can't absorb D3–4 without destabilization) even at S7A. When forced above it, they regress to S3, but the regression is predictable and avoidable (reset the mandate, or invest in the person to expand their ceiling). Without the construct, this is invisible.

## Recommendations

### Priority 1 — Scope Declaration

**Action:** Explicitly declare which populations the model *is* and *is not* designed to describe in the opening section of V5.

**Rationale:** Every gap above is a potential misapplication. A researcher reading the model and finding they don't fit it should be able to point to the scope statement, not wonder if they're broken.

**Proposed text:**
> This model describes adaptation of knowledge workers and creatives who encounter AI after building identity in their domain. It assumes pre-formed Identity Stakes and is tested only for users whose first encounter with AI happens in adulthood. It does not describe AI-native users, non-knowledge-work domains, emotional-relational use, users with cognitive disabilities requiring AI as assistive technology, or users facing permanent economic threat from adoption.

---

### Priority 2 — Adoption Ceiling Restoration

**Action:** Restore Adoption Ceiling as a D-level ceiling (D-ceiling: 1, 2, 3, or unbounded) that a user may have regardless of their state. Regression from S5/S7 under mandate to higher D-level is predictable when D-mandate > D-ceiling.

**Rationale:** The absence of the construct creates an invisible failure mode. With it, training and organizational design become possible interventions (expand the ceiling, lower the mandate, or accept the regression).

**Proposed formula:**
> A user in S5 or S7A is stable *within their D-ceiling*. Forcing them to delegate authority beyond D-ceiling will trigger regression to S3, even if they fully understand AI. D-ceiling is independent of state and may be modified only through sustained intervention (skill-building, cultural change, personal readiness work).

---

### Priority 3 — Add AI-Native Pathway

**Action:** Add an optional S0* (Ambient Exposure) → S5/S7B pathway for users whose first meaningful exposure happens before age 18 or before pre-formed domain identity.

**Rationale:** This population is growing. They don't fit the S0 → S1 required path because AI was never *novel* to them.

**Proposed addition:**
> Users whose exposure to AI predates or coincides with formation of domain identity (typically age < 18 or early-career learners) may bypass S1 (Initial Encounter) and enter S5 or S7B directly. No identity threat is present because the identity is still forming. This pathway is increasingly common.

---

### Priority 4 — Non-Knowledge-Work Subsection

**Action:** Add a subsection under "Cultural Variability" (or a new "Domain Variability" section) describing how S3 and D-level experience differs in non-knowledge-work domains.

**Rationale:** The model is not broken for trades and service work; the language just doesn't fit. A clear mapping makes the model usable for these domains.

**Proposed text:**
> In non-knowledge domains (trades, manual labor, service work), Task Delegation Levels map differently: D1 (information) → diagnostic tools; D2 (task execution) → automation of routine decisions; D3–4 don't apply (work is not creative in the model's sense). Ego Shock in these domains is primarily economic (replacement threat) rather than identity-based, and manifests as rapid regression to S2D (defensive resistance) tied to job security, not professional pride. Identity Stakes are high but orthogonal to the domain's prestige, making S3B (bargaining) less common.

---

### Priority 5 — Accessibility and Assistive-Tech Use Case

**Action:** Add a note to S5 (Understanding) acknowledging that accessibility-aid use is out of scope. Or add an optional S5-A (Understanding + Access) describing users for whom AI is a prosthetic, not a threat.

**Rationale:** Not modeling this is fine, but users who rely on AI for accessibility shouldn't be confused by a model that assumes all users are capability-matching.

**Proposed addition:**
> The model assumes the user's baseline capability is intact and AI is capability-matching (replicating existing skill). Users relying on AI as assistive technology (e.g., dyslexic writers using AI to rework prose) bypass S3 (Ego Shock) entirely because the tool provides access, not competition. This use case requires separate analysis and is not covered in V5.

---

### Priority 6 — Economic Dependence (Out-of-Scope Declaration)

**Action:** Add a bullet to the Scope Declaration acknowledging that users whose economic survival depends on non-adoption are out of scope.

**Rationale:** This is a real population, and the model doesn't describe them well. Flag it explicitly.

**Proposed text:**
> Users whose economic livelihood depends on human-only service provision face permanent S3 (Ego Shock) and cannot exit the model through integration; economic dropout is the only option. This population is out of scope.

---

### Priority 7 — Pre-Emptive Ethical Rejection Pathway

**Action:** Add S1 → S7C direct pathway with a note describing principled rejection prior to engagement.

**Rationale:** S7C is described as a reflective stance *after* engagement. Users who reject AI before engagement exist and should fit the model cleanly.

**Proposed text:**
> Some users enter S1, perform rapid ethical appraisal, and move directly to S7C (Ethical / Philosophical Integration) or dropout without ever testing the tool. This is distinct from S7C *post-engagement* and represents ethical commitment prior to capability assessment.
