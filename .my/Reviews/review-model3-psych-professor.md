# Faculty Review: The AI Psychological Adaptation Cycle — Version 3

**Reviewer:** Academic psychology perspective
**Document reviewed:** Model3.md — The AI Psychological Adaptation Cycle, Version 3
**Review type:** Substantive theoretical and methodological critique

---

## General Assessment

This document presents a multi-stage, multi-dimensional model of psychological adaptation to AI technology. It is ambitious in scope, attempting to integrate identity theory, emotional regulation, social influence, and technology adoption into a unified framework. There is genuine psychological intuition here — particularly around identity threat and ego-defensive behavior — but the model suffers from fundamental problems of theoretical grounding, methodological absence, and structural overextension that would need to be resolved before it could be taken seriously as a contribution to psychological science.

I will organize this review around the standards any psychology faculty would apply: theoretical foundations, construct validity, structural coherence, empirical status, and contribution relative to existing work.

---

## Theoretical Foundations

### What is anchored well

The model's emotional core — the sequence from dismissal through defensive resistance to ego shock — maps recognizably onto established psychological territory. The author appears to be drawing on several well-studied phenomena, whether consciously or not:

- **Identity threat theory** (Steele, 1988; Petriglieri, 2011). The idea that encountering AI capability triggers ego-protective responses is consistent with decades of research on how people respond when core self-concepts are challenged. Stage 2 (Defensive Resistance) and Stage 3 (Ego Shock) are essentially describing identity threat and identity disruption, respectively.

- **Terror Management Theory and mortality salience**, loosely. The existential flavor of Stage 3 at D4 ("What is my role now?") echoes research on how people respond to threats to their sense of significance and purpose, though the parallel is not exact.

- **Cognitive dissonance** (Festinger, 1957). Stage 3B (Bargaining) is recognizable as a dissonance-reduction strategy: the person cannot reconcile "AI is capable" with "my skills define me," so they partition — using AI only in domains that don't threaten the self-concept. This is a known and well-documented pattern.

- **Diffusion of Innovations** (Rogers, 1962). The 1A/1B branching at Stage 1, distinguishing low-identity from high-identity users, is functionally similar to Rogers' adopter categories, though the segmentation variable is different (identity attachment rather than risk tolerance or social position).

- **The Kübler-Ross echo.** The sequence — denial, anger, bargaining, depression/disruption, acceptance — clearly parallels the grief model, and the author seems aware of this even if it isn't cited. This is both a strength (the parallel is intuitively compelling) and a liability (the Kübler-Ross model itself has been extensively criticized for lacking empirical support as a sequential process).

### What is not anchored

- **The delegation-depth axis has no theoretical home.** The four levels (Information → Task → Creative → Autonomous) are presented as though they form a natural hierarchy of psychological intensity. But why these four? The jump from D2 to D3 is doing enormous psychological work — it's where "skill relevance" becomes "core identity engaged" — and this boundary is simply asserted. In self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985), the critical variable would be autonomy and competence need-satisfaction, which might cut across these categories differently. In job demands-resources theory (Bakker & Demerouti, 2007), the relevant variable would be the ratio of resources lost to resources gained. The axis is plausible but theoretically ad hoc.

- **The social context layer invokes social influence without engaging with social influence theory.** Social contagion, cultural framing, and normalization are enormous literatures. The model names them as forces but doesn't engage with any specific mechanism. Is social contagion operating through informational influence or normative influence (Deutsch & Gerard, 1955)? Is normalization a function of descriptive norms, injunctive norms, or both (Cialdini, Reno, & Kallgren, 1990)? Does cultural framing operate through values (Schwartz, 1992), through uncertainty avoidance (Hofstede, 1980), or through something else? Naming "social contagion" as a force without specifying the mechanism is like naming "gravity" without specifying what it acts on.

- **The external trigger layer lacks any psychological mechanism.** Why does a model release cause regression? Presumably because it creates a new identity threat, a new capability gap, or disconfirms existing mental models. But the paper doesn't say this. It just says "a capability jump can send someone from Stage 4 back to Stage 2 or 3." What mediates this? Is it surprise? Schema violation? Renewed self-efficacy threat? Without a mechanism, the trigger layer is descriptive, not explanatory.

- **Overcompensation (Stage 6) is not theoretically distinguished from engagement.** The paper describes 6A as "dopamine-driven experimentation" — but dopamine-mediated reward-seeking during skill acquisition is *normal*. When does enthusiastic adoption become "overcompensation"? The paper offers no threshold, no criterion, and no distinction from healthy high engagement. In clinical psychology, we distinguish pathological from normal behavior by functional impairment. The paper doesn't do this.

## Construct Validity

Several of the model's constructs have unclear boundaries:

**Stage 3 vs. Stage 3B:** If bargaining is a response to ego shock, it should be a coping strategy *within* Stage 3, not a separate stage. Making it a separate stage implies it has a distinct onset, a distinct phenomenology, and distinct antecedents. The paper doesn't establish any of these — it just says some people negotiate partial adoption. That is a behavioral pattern within an identity-threat response, not a distinct psychological phase.

**Stage 4 (Trust Evaluation) vs. rational behavior at any stage.** The paper presents trust evaluation as a stage, but rational assessment of a tool's capabilities is something people can do at any point in the process. A person in Stage 5 who encounters a new AI tool evaluates its trustworthiness. A person in Stage 3B is implicitly evaluating trust by setting boundaries. Elevating "rational evaluation" to a stage conflates a cognitive process with a psychological phase. Processes can operate within phases; they are not phases themselves.

**Stage 7A–7F are not end states — they are personality-environment configurations.** Calling these "stabilization" implies they are outcomes of the adaptation process. But 7C (Ethical/Philosophical Integration) could describe someone who has never used AI personally. 7F (Influence/Evangelism) describes a social role, not a psychological state. 7D (Burnout) is a well-studied construct with established diagnostic criteria (Maslach & Jackson, 1981) that the paper doesn't engage with. These are not stages in a psychological process; they are different ways of relating to a technology, and they could be better treated as outcome variables or moderator profiles.

**The 1A/1B distinction conflates a moderator with a stage feature.** Whether someone has high or low identity attachment to AI-relevant skills is a *person-level variable* that should moderate the entire model, not a branching condition within Stage 1. This is a modeling error: the variable operates across all stages (a low-identity person will experience less intensity at every stage, not just at Stage 1), but the model treats it as though it only matters at the entry point.

## Structural Coherence

The model has a numbering problem that reflects a deeper conceptual problem. It presents itself as a sequential stage model (0 → 1 → 2 → 3 → ... → 7) but describes a directed graph with multiple entry points, bypass paths, regression edges, and co-occurring states. This is not merely a labeling issue — it reveals confusion about what kind of model this is.

In psychology, there are at least three distinct types of process models:

1. **Stage models** (Piaget, Kohlberg, Erikson) — invariant sequence, each stage builds on the prior, no skipping. The model claims to be this but is not.

2. **Phase models** (Prochaska & DiClemente's Transtheoretical Model) — typical sequence with allowed regression and recycling. The model is closest to this, and should acknowledge the parallel explicitly.

3. **State-space models** — no inherent sequence; the system occupies a state based on current conditions and transitions based on inputs. The model's regression loops, external triggers, and multiple entry points suggest it is actually closer to this.

The model would benefit enormously from choosing one of these structures and committing to it. As written, it inherits the intuitive appeal of a stage model (people like narratives with beginnings and endings) while actually describing something much less orderly. This creates false confidence in the model's predictive utility.

The numbering itself is misleading. Stage 4 (Trust Evaluation) can be entered from Stage 1A, 3B, or 5 — it is not "after" Stage 3B in any meaningful sense. Stage 3B can lead back to Stage 2. A state-transition diagram would be far more honest than a numbered list.

## Empirical Status

This is the model's most serious deficiency. There is no empirical grounding of any kind:

- No data (qualitative or quantitative)
- No cited studies
- No case examples with enough specificity to be evaluated
- No described methodology for how the stages were derived
- No sample or population description
- No acknowledgment of the model's evidential basis

Every claim in the paper — the stage sequence, the delegation-depth interaction, the external trigger effects, the cultural variability, the dropout typology — is presented as established fact but is actually untested assertion.

This does not mean the claims are wrong. Many of them are psychologically plausible, and some are likely correct. But plausibility is not evidence. Psychology has a long and painful history of intuitively compelling models that turned out to be wrong when tested (e.g., learning styles, the Kübler-Ross stages as a fixed sequence, Maslow's hierarchy as empirical rather than philosophical).

To be taken seriously, this model would need at minimum:

1. **Qualitative grounding:** Semi-structured interviews with people at various points in AI adoption, coded for the proposed stages, with independent raters assessing stage membership. This would establish whether the stages are recognizable as distinct lived experiences.

2. **Construct operationalization:** Clear behavioral or self-report indicators for each stage. When is someone "in" Stage 3 vs. Stage 3B? What observable behaviors distinguish Stage 2 from Stage 4? Without operational definitions, the model cannot be tested.

3. **Longitudinal data:** Even a small panel study tracking individuals over months of AI adoption would allow assessment of the proposed sequence, typical transitions, and regression frequency.

4. **Discriminant validity:** Demonstration that this model captures something that existing frameworks (Technology Acceptance Model, Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology, Transtheoretical Model, Identity Threat Theory) do not.

## Relationship to Existing Work

The model does not cite or position itself relative to the large existing literature on technology adoption psychology. This is a significant omission. Key frameworks that should be engaged:

- **Technology Acceptance Model (Davis, 1989)** and **UTAUT (Venkatesh et al., 2003)** — the dominant models in technology adoption. They emphasize perceived usefulness, perceived ease of use, social influence, and facilitating conditions. The current model's social context layer overlaps with UTAUT's social influence construct, and Stage 4 (Trust Evaluation) overlaps with perceived usefulness assessment. How does this model differ from, extend, or improve upon these frameworks?

- **Transtheoretical Model of Change (Prochaska & DiClemente, 1983)** — the most direct structural parallel. The TTM has precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance, and allows regression. The current model's structure is strikingly similar, and the TTM has decades of empirical testing (with mixed results). The author should either acknowledge this parallel explicitly or explain why a new framework is needed.

- **Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 2000)** — the delegation-depth axis implicitly engages with autonomy and competence needs. SDT would predict that AI threatening perceived competence triggers ego-defensive responses, which is exactly what this model describes. The theoretical mechanism is already articulated in SDT; the model should build on it rather than reinventing it without attribution.

- **Psychological Reactance Theory (Brehm, 1966)** — relevant to workplace mandates and forced adoption. When people perceive their freedom to choose is restricted, they resist. This is a well-studied mechanism that could explain why mandated adoption sometimes produces "delayed ego shock" — the initial compliance suppresses reactance that surfaces later.

- **Professional Identity Theory** (e.g., Ibarra, 1999; Pratt, Rockmann, & Kaufmann, 2006) — directly relevant to the model's core claim that AI threatens skill-based identities. This literature has detailed mechanisms for how professionals construct, maintain, and revise their identities when facing disruption. The model's Stage 3 (Ego Shock) is essentially describing professional identity threat, and the existing literature has frameworks for how people respond.

The absence of any engagement with these bodies of work means the model exists in a theoretical vacuum. It may well be saying something new — the delegation-depth interaction and the emotional/practical dropout distinction are potentially genuine contributions — but without positioning, there is no way to evaluate novelty.

## What Is Genuinely Promising

Despite the criticisms above, several elements of this model have real psychological merit:

1. **The delegation-depth × emotional-phase interaction.** This is the model's most original contribution. The insight that psychological intensity depends not just on the phase of adaptation but on what cognitive authority is being delegated is genuinely useful and is not captured by existing technology adoption models. This deserves to be developed into a properly operationalized two-dimensional framework with testable predictions at each cell.

2. **The emotional/practical dropout distinction.** This is a clinically useful observation. Interventions for identity threat are fundamentally different from interventions for usability failure, and conflating them is a common error in organizational change management. This distinction could be empirically tested and, if validated, would have practical value.

3. **Bargaining as a stable but fragile coping strategy.** The observation that people set boundaries around AI use to preserve identity, and that capability improvements destabilize these boundaries, is psychologically sound and practically recognizable. This could be fruitfully connected to the literature on coping strategies (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984) and boundary work (Nippert-Eng, 1996).

4. **The regression mechanism.** Most adoption models assume monotonic progress. The explicit modeling of regression triggered by capability jumps is realistic and theoretically interesting. It connects to research on schema disruption and the need for iterative sense-making during sustained technological change.

5. **The focus on affect.** Mainstream technology adoption models (TAM, UTAUT) are heavily cognitive — perceived usefulness, perceived ease of use, behavioral intention. This model foregrounds emotional experience: fear, grief, existential discomfort, euphoria, burnout. There is a growing recognition that affect plays a larger role in technology adoption than cognitive models capture (Beaudry & Pinsonneault, 2010). The model is aligned with this direction even if it doesn't cite it.

## Recommendations

1. **Ground the model theoretically.** Identify the specific psychological theories being drawn upon (identity threat, cognitive dissonance, self-determination, reactance) and make the connections explicit. This transforms the model from folk psychology into an integrative framework.

2. **Clarify the model's type.** Decide whether this is a stage model, a phase model, or a state-space model, and restructure accordingly. If it's a state-space model (which the evidence of regression, multiple entry points, and non-linear paths suggests), present it as a state-transition diagram with defined transition conditions, not as a numbered sequence.

3. **Operationalize the constructs.** Define observable, measurable indicators for each stage/state. Without this, the model cannot be tested, cannot be applied consistently, and cannot be distinguished from narrative post-hoc explanation.

4. **Conduct empirical work.** Start with qualitative interviews and thematic coding. Follow with a longitudinal panel study. Even a small-N study (20–30 participants tracked over 6 months) would provide more evidential basis than the model currently has.

5. **Reduce the taxonomy.** The current proliferation of subtypes (6A–6D, 7A–7F) weakens the model. Each distinction should earn its place by predicting different outcomes or requiring different interventions. If 6A and 6C lead to the same places and respond to the same interventions, they should be collapsed.

6. **Develop the delegation-depth axis fully.** If this is truly a two-dimensional model, build the full matrix. Show how each stage/state manifests at each delegation level. This would be a genuinely original contribution and would give the model analytical power that existing frameworks lack.

7. **Position relative to existing literature.** A model that cannot explain what it adds beyond TAM, UTAUT, the TTM, and identity threat theory has no clear contribution. The positioning work is not optional — it is what transforms a private framework into a scholarly one.

---

## Summary Judgment

This is an early-stage theoretical framework with genuine psychological insight buried under structural problems and a complete absence of empirical grounding. The core observation — that AI adoption involves identity threat modulated by delegation depth, with non-linear progression and distinct dropout mechanisms — is worth pursuing. The author has good intuitions about psychological processes but has not yet done the theoretical or empirical work needed to transform those intuitions into a defensible model.

In its current form, this is a provocative essay, not a scientific model. That is not necessarily a problem — many influential ideas in psychology started as essays (Kübler-Ross, Maslow, Erikson). But the path from essay to model requires operationalization, evidence, and engagement with the field. Until that work is done, the framework should be presented as a hypothesis to be tested, not a model to be applied.

**Rating:** Revise and resubmit with major revisions. The core ideas merit development, but the theoretical, structural, and empirical deficits are too significant for the current version to stand as a contribution to the psychological literature.
